*Manufacture · Hwaseong, South Korea · 2010s*
**Samsung Electronics** · vertically stacked NAND flash

**Front.** In August 2013, at a chip plant in Hwaseong, south of Seoul, [Samsung](https://news.samsung.com/global/samsung-starts-mass-producing-industrys-first-3d-vertical-nand-flash) engineers ran into a wall that had been coming for years. Flash memory kept each bit in a tiny cell, and to hold more bits they had gone on printing the cells smaller and packing them closer, until the cells sat so tight that stray electrons leaked from one to the next and the saved data went bad. So they stopped going sideways and went up: they laid down [24 layers](https://www.eetimes.com/3d-nand-production-starts-at-samsung/) of cell material one on top of another, then [punched a narrow hole straight down through all 24 at once](https://www.businesswire.com/news/home/20130805006358/en/Samsung-Starts-Mass-Producing-Industry%E2%80%99s-First-3D-Vertical-NAND-Flash) and lined it so a single upright pillar became a stack of 24 memory cells. One chip now held [128 billion bits](https://news.samsung.com/global/samsung-starts-mass-producing-industrys-first-3d-vertical-nand-flash), and from then on you held more by adding floors, not by printing finer.

**Back.** The move opened a clear road: leave the printing where it was and just stack more layers. Every rival soon switched to the same vertical trick, and the count climbed from 24 to past 100, and now [beyond 200](https://news.samsung.com/global/samsung-electronics-begins-industrys-first-mass-production-of-9th-gen-v-nand). The lead in NAND flash had sat in Japan since [Toshiba's Fujio Masuoka worked it out in 1987](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flash_memory), but the machines that could etch these deep, clean holes were in Korea. Today Samsung and its neighbor SK Hynix together make [more than half](https://blocksandfiles.com/2024/09/13/samsung-and-sk-hynix-gain-nand-market-share/) of the world's flash memory.
