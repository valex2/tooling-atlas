*Model · Jena, Germany · 1873*
**Ernst Abbe** · a wave theory of the lens

**Front.** In 1873 a mathematics professor in Jena named [Ernst Abbe](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ernst-Abbe), [hired by the lensmaker Carl Zeiss](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ernst-Abbe) to work out why his microscopes came out good or bad, hit a limit no polishing could pass. Past a point, a sharper lens stopped giving a sharper picture. Abbe worked out the math of how light bends as it slips past the thing you are looking at, and [proved the blur was not bad glass but a hard floor](https://www.britannica.com/science/Abbe-limit). No lens, however perfect, can separate two points closer than about [half the wavelength of the light](https://www.microscopyu.com/techniques/super-resolution/the-diffraction-barrier-in-optical-microscopy), around 200 nanometres for visible light. He had not built a better lens; he had written the formula that turned lens-making from trial and error into calculation and drew the exact line past which light cannot go.

**Back.** With the formula in hand, [Zeiss could design lenses straight to that limit](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ernst-Abbe) instead of grinding and guessing, and Jena became the center of the world's fine optics for generations. The same proof carried a harder message: an atom is a thousand times smaller than the floor, so by the rules of light it can never be seen, no matter how good the glass. Anyone could read the formula; the craft of grinding to that limit lived on one street in Jena, which held the apex of fine optics for more than a century. The wall Abbe drew is the door the electron microscope later walked through.
