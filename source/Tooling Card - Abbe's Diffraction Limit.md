*Model · Jena, Germany · 1873*
**Ernst Abbe** · a wave theory of the lens

**Front.** In 1873, a mathematics professor in Jena named [Ernst Abbe](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ernst-Abbe), [hired by the lensmaker Carl Zeiss](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ernst-Abbe) to work out why his microscopes came out good or bad, hit a limit no amount of polishing could pass: past a point, a sharper lens stopped giving a sharper picture. Abbe worked out the math of how light bends as it slips past the thing you are looking at, and [proved the blur was not bad glass but a hard floor](https://www.britannica.com/science/Abbe-limit). No lens, however perfect, can separate two points closer than about [half the wavelength of the light](https://www.microscopyu.com/techniques/super-resolution/the-diffraction-barrier-in-optical-microscopy), around 200 nanometres for visible light. The new thing was not a lens at all. It was a formula that turned lens-making from trial and error into calculation, and it drew the exact line past which light cannot go.

**Back.** With the formula in hand, [Zeiss could design lenses straight to that limit](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ernst-Abbe) instead of grinding and guessing, and Jena became the center of the world's fine optics for generations. The same proof carried a harder message: an atom is a thousand times smaller than the floor, so by the rules of light it can never be seen, no matter how good the glass. The formula was free for anyone to read, yet the lead stayed in Jena because the craft of grinding to the limit lived there. The wall Abbe drew is the door the electron microscope later walked through.
