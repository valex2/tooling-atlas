*Model · Baghdad, Abbasid Caliphate · 820s*
**Muhammad al-Khwarizmi** · rules for balancing equation terms

**Front.** Sometime around [820](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Compendious-Book-on-Calculation-by-Completion-and-Balancing) in Baghdad, a scholar named [Muhammad al-Khwarizmi](https://www.britannica.com/biography/al-Khwarizmi) worked in the caliph's library, the House of Wisdom. People brought him money puzzles, like splitting an inheritance or measuring a field, and each was solved by its own trick or a drawing in the sand. He wrote a short book that sorted every such puzzle into [six set forms](https://mathshistory.st-andrews.ac.uk/Biographies/Al-Khwarizmi/), then fixed each form with two moves: al-jabr, adding a term to both sides to clear away what had been taken off, and al-muqabala, cancelling matching terms until the unknown stood alone. The two moves were written as plain rules a reader could run on any problem, with no picture to lean on.

**Back.** The book's short name for the first move, al-jabr, stuck. Three hundred years on, [Robert of Chester](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_of_Chester) turned it into Latin in 1145, reckoning masters across Europe began to solve by rule instead of by diagram, and the Latin al-jabr became the [word "algebra"](https://www.etymonline.com/word/algebra). The lead never sat still in Baghdad. It moved with each copy of the book, opening the way to a shared method that a merchant in Toledo and a scholar in Baghdad could both run the same way.
