*Model · Leiden, Dutch Republic · 1630s*
**René Descartes** · curves written as grid equations

**Front.** In [1637](https://www.britannica.com/topic/La-Geometrie), a French soldier-turned-thinker named [René Descartes](https://mathshistory.st-andrews.ac.uk/Biographies/Descartes/), living quietly in the Dutch Republic, printed a thin appendix to his *Discourse on Method* at a shop in Leiden. Geometry and algebra had lived in separate rooms: to study a curve you drew it by hand, and to solve an equation you pushed symbols around, and neither trick could reach into the other's work. Descartes drew two lines crossing at a corner, marked off distances along them as *x* and *y*, and showed that every point on a curve follows one rule written in those two letters, so a circle or a parabola became a short equation you could then attack with algebra. The [new method](https://www.diva-portal.org/smash/get/diva2:631055/FULLTEXT01.pdf) was a grid that turned a shape into a number sentence and a number sentence back into a shape.

**Back.** A lawyer in Toulouse, [Pierre de Fermat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_de_Fermat), had worked out nearly the same grid a year earlier, but he kept it in letters that stayed unprinted until 1679, so the version people learned was the one Descartes had put in a book. In 1649 [Frans van Schooten](https://mathshistory.st-andrews.ac.uk/Biographies/Schooten/) turned that book into Latin at Leiden, and the fuller editions that followed were what Newton and Leibniz read as young men. From the grid they built the calculus, and because the method was printed for anyone to copy it never stayed the property of one man; it became the common floor that later mathematics was built on, the same *x*-and-*y* grid still sitting under every equation graphed today.
