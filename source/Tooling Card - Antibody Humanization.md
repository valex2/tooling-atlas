*Make · Cambridge, UK · 1980s*
**Greg Winter (MRC LMB)** · mouse grip on a human frame

**Front.** In 1986, a protein chemist named [Greg Winter](https://www2.mrc-lmb.cam.ac.uk/group-leaders/emeritus/greg-winter/) was at the MRC lab in Cambridge, stuck on a problem with a promising kind of drug. Mouse antibodies could be built to lock onto almost any target in the body, but the body saw the mouse protein as an intruder and cleared it out; as many as [half](https://www.jbc.org/article/S0021-9258(23)02583-8/fulltext) of patients rejected it. Winter treated the antibody as two parts: a large frame that holds it together, and six tiny loops at the tip that do the gripping. He [cut out the genes](https://www.nature.com/articles/321522a0) for a mouse antibody's six loops and [grafted them into](https://www.whatisbiotechnology.org/index.php/exhibitions/campath/engineering) a human antibody's frame, so almost the whole molecule now read as human while the grip stayed mouse.

**Back.** The method, called [CDR grafting](https://www.nature.com/articles/321522a0), turned mouse antibodies into drugs the body would keep. It took about a decade to reach patients, but Winter's antibody work now underlies [two-thirds](https://www.unige.ch/medecine/frontiers-in-biomedicine/gregwinter/) of the antibody drugs on the market, from the cancer antibody Herceptin to [Humira](https://www.axios.com/2018/01/26/humira-sales-surpass-18-billion-1516983676), which sold [$18 billion](https://www.axios.com/2018/01/26/humira-sales-surpass-18-billion-1516983676) in 2017. His group in Cambridge went on to grow fully human antibodies inside viruses and held the lead in antibody engineering for close to twenty years. In 2018 Winter shared the [Nobel Prize in Chemistry](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/chemistry/2018/winter/facts/).
