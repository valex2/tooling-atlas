*Make · Cambridge, UK · 1990s*
**Greg Winter & John McCafferty** · antibody shown on a virus coat

**Front.** In 1990, at the [MRC lab in Cambridge](https://www2.mrc-lmb.cam.ac.uk/greg-winter-wins-2018-nobel-prize-for-chemistry/), John McCafferty and Greg Winter had a slow, ugly problem: to find a human antibody that grabbed one target, you had to guess which cell out of millions made it, and testing them one by one took forever. So [McCafferty stitched the gene for an antibody onto the gene for a coat protein of a tiny virus](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/2247164/) that infects bacteria. Each virus then grew wearing its own antibody on the outside, with the gene that spelled it tucked inside. Now you could pour billions of these viruses over the target at once, wash the rest away, and keep only the [rare one in a million](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/2247164/) that stuck.

**Back.** Because the winning virus carried its own gene, you could read it straight off and copy the antibody without ever touching a mouse or a human cell. Winter's spin-out, Cambridge Antibody Technology, used the trick to fish out [adalimumab, the first fully human antibody](https://www.nature.com/articles/d43747-020-00348-1) approved in this way; sold as Humira, it went on to earn [close to $200 billion](https://www.britannica.com/science/adalimumab), more than any other drug. The method spread to lab after lab, and in [2018 Winter shared the Nobel Prize in Chemistry](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/chemistry/2018/winter/facts/) for it.
