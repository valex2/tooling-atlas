*Make · Kourou, French Guiana · 1970s*
**ESA / CNES / Arianespace** · Europe's three-stage commercial launcher

**Front.** In 1973, France and Germany had a telephone satellite named [Symphonie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symphonie) nearly ready and no rocket of their own to fly it; America would [launch it only if Europe promised never to sell its service](https://www.esa.int/esapub/bulletin/bullet83/bau83.htm). That July, European governments [agreed to build their own rocket](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ariane_1), with France paying most of the bill and running the work. Its engineers stacked three stages into a tower [47 metres tall](https://www.esa.int/About_Us/ESA_history/History_Ariane_L01_1979) on [four Viking engines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ariane_1) burning fuels that keep at room temperature, the first rocket anywhere [built mainly to carry paying satellites](https://www.esa.int/Enabling_Support/Space_Transportation/Ariane/40_years_of_Ariane) to the high orbit where they relay calls and television. On [15 December 1979](https://www.esa.int/About_Us/ESA_history/History_Ariane_L01_1979) the engines lit at Kourou and went out on the pad; nine days later, on Christmas Eve, [Ariane flew](https://historicalarchives.esa.int/christmas-kourou).

**Back.** Three months after that first flight Europe set up [Arianespace](https://newsroom.arianespace.com/four-decades-of-setting-the-launch-service-standards-is-celebrated-by-arianespace/), the first company in the world formed to sell rocket launches. The rocket grew stronger version by version, and by the early 1990s Ariane 4 was carrying [over half](https://commons.erau.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1829&context=space-congress-proceedings) of the world's commercial satellites, many of them American, out of the same jungle pad. The lead compounded for two decades and broke in the 2010s, when SpaceX's cheaper reusable Falcon pulled the trade back to America; Kourou has been chasing it since.
