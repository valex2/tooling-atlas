*Make · Cromford, England · 1760s*
**Richard Arkwright** · water-powered warp-spinning frame

**Front.** In July [1769](https://www.nationalarchives.gov.uk/education/resources/georgian-britain-age-modernity/arkwrights-spinning-frame/) Richard Arkwright, a former barber and wig-maker, patented a spinning frame that pulled raw cotton through four pairs of rollers turning at rising speeds, drawing it out, then twisted it tight on a spindle. The machine did the one thing hand-spinning and the earlier jenny could not do well: it made cotton thread hard and even enough to serve as [warp](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Richard-Arkwright), the lengthwise threads strung tight on a loom, which until then had to be costly linen or wool. A single frame worked [many spindles at once](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_frame), and the rollers, not a spinner's fingers, set the pace. By [1771](https://www.scienceandindustrymuseum.org.uk/objects-and-stories/richard-arkwright) Arkwright had a row of these frames driven off one water wheel at Cromford on the River Derwent, which is where the name water frame stuck.

**Back.** All-cotton cloth, cheap and washable, now ran on British looms, and the way of making it became the thing to copy. Parliament tried to lock it down: Acts in [1774, 1781 and 1782](https://academic.oup.com/book/45624/chapter/394884888) barred shipping the machines, plans, or models abroad, and skilled textile workers were forbidden to emigrate. The guard held against drawings and crates but not against a trained memory. In September [1789](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Samuel-Slater) Samuel Slater, an apprentice under Arkwright's partner Jedediah Strutt, slipped out of England disguised as a farm laborer with nothing written down, then rebuilt the frames from memory at Pawtucket, Rhode Island. Britain called him [Slater the Traitor](https://www.ebsco.com/research-starters/history/samuel-slater); America called him the father of its factories, and the secret that no law could hold opened the way to a cotton industry across the ocean.
