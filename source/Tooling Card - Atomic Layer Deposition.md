*Manufacture · Helsinki, Finland · 1970s*
**Tuomo Suntola** · a film laid one atom-layer at a time

**Front.** In 1974 a Finnish physicist, [Tuomo Suntola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuomo_Suntola), was trying to build flat glowing display panels. They needed a perfectly even, ultra-thin film spread over a large sheet, and ordinary coating gave thickness that wandered. His fix was to let the chemistry police itself. [Pulse in a gas that reacts with the surface but stops the instant the surface is fully covered](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1002/cvde.201402012), one layer and no more, then flush it out, then pulse in a second gas that finishes that layer, then flush again, and repeat. Each full cycle lays down exactly [one atomic layer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_layer_deposition), no matter how uneven or trenched the surface is. Thickness no longer depended on timing or rate; you set it by counting cycles, atom-layer by atom-layer, even everywhere.

**Back.** It sat in a display-making niche for decades until chipmaking ran out of other ways to coat a few atoms evenly. By [2007 Intel's 45-nanometer chips used it to lay down a hafnium insulator only a few atoms thick under each transistor gate](https://www.realworldtech.com/intel-45nm-hkmg/3/), [cutting leakage more than tenfold](https://www.realworldtech.com/intel-45nm-hkmg/3/) and keeping the shrink alive. Thirty-three years separated Suntola's 1974 patent from that breakout, and no advanced node built since can skip the step.
