*Manufacture · Wuxi, China · 2020s*
**Wuxi Lead Intelligent Equipment** · the integrated cell production line

**Front.** By 2024 the firm [Wuxi Lead Intelligent](https://www.leadintelligent.com/en/company-profile/) in Wuxi, China sold the whole machine line that turns electrode paste into a finished battery cell, end to end. A cell starts as wet paste and ends as a sealed can that holds charge, and getting there is a chain of machines that must hand a wafer-thin electrode off without a wrinkle: coat the metal foil with the paste, dry it, slit it, then a winding or stacking machine rolls or layers the anode and cathode together so thin that a misalignment of a fraction of a millimeter ruins the cell, then fill it with electrolyte and run formation, the first slow charge that wakes the chemistry up. The hardest step is that stacking machine, and Wuxi Lead's placed a layer every [0.075 seconds](https://www.batterytechonline.com/battery-manufacturing/wuxi-lead-s-wang-on-driving-battery-gigafactory-innovation-sustainability) and held [more than 60 percent](https://www.batterytechonline.com/battery-manufacturing/wuxi-lead-s-wang-on-driving-battery-gigafactory-innovation-sustainability) of that segment worldwide. What was new was that one supplier now sold the entire line as a product, so a gigafactory could be bought rather than built.

**Back.** Wuxi Lead booked a [$4.3 billion](https://www.yicaiglobal.com/news/lithium-battery-gear-supplier-wuxi-lead-to-reap-usd43-billion-from-catl-deal) three-year framework with CATL and held about [15.5 percent](https://www.marketscreener.com/news/wuxi-lead-intelligent-equipment-raises-over-630-million-in-hong-kong-listing-ce7e5adcd188f620) of the global equipment market by 2024 revenue, but the telling part is who else it shipped to: Tesla, LG, Panasonic, and a [20-gigawatt-hour line](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/american-battery-factory-partners-with-lead-intelligent-equipment-for-next-phase-of-arizona-gigafactory-302091005.html) sold to a startup gigafactory in Tucson, Arizona. The machine travels freely, so it is not the moat. Sweden's Northvolt bought the same kind of line, [burned through roughly $15 billion](https://kr-asia.com/northvolts-fall-how-hubris-and-blunders-shattered-europes-battery-ambitions), flew in [Chinese technicians week after week](https://thedeepdive.ca/did-china-sabotage-northvolt-unraveling-the-crisis-behind-swedens-battery-giant/) to nurse the machines, and still took years to reach a yield Chinese rivals claimed to hit in [four months](https://kr-asia.com/northvolts-fall-how-hubris-and-blunders-shattered-europes-battery-ambitions) before it collapsed. This is the chip story again, the same split the [[Tooling Card - The High-Yield Process Recipe]] names: the hardware exports, the know-how to ramp it to yield does not, and from [November 2025](https://www.ess-news.com/2025/10/13/china-imposes-export-controls-on-lithium-ion-battery-technology/) China began licensing the cathode-process and high-density LFP technology underneath to lock the one layer it controls.

*Curator's note:* **Placement.** Manufacture cell, Wuxi, dated 2024 to the CATL framework and the Arizona line, on [[Thread - Electricity & Batteries]]. Serves both goals: leadership in making cells (Goal 2) and the strategic dependence it creates (Goal 1). Mechanism is pace-layers, not convergence: the fast-moving exportable machine sits on top of a slow tacit ramp-to-yield cadre that does not travel. Confidence direct. **Analysis.** This is the battery analog of the chip high-yield recipe, and it is written as the deliberate inverse of the usual moat card: here the hardware is freely sold, so the card's whole job is to point past the machine to the non-exportable layer. Pairs with [[Tooling Card - The High-Yield Process Recipe]] as the cross-industry restatement of "design and even the machine are public, the doing is the moat." The Northvolt collapse is the clean proof point that buying the line is not buying the capability. **Nuance.** The 15.5 percent global share is 2024 revenue per Frost & Sullivan; the brief's ~22 percent figure was not confirmed in sourcing, so the lower sourced number is used and the >60 percent winding/stacking dominance is the firmer claim. The Northvolt "96 percent in four months vs 70 percent in four years" contrast is a Chinese executive's quote reported in trade press, directional rather than audited, and Northvolt's failure had many causes beyond yield. Whether the cadre moat is durable or rebuildable is the open question: unlike a single fab's recipe it is a national pool of trained ramp engineers, which a rival region could in principle grow given a decade and demand, which is exactly why the November 2025 export licensing matters.
