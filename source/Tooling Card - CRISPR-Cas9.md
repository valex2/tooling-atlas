*Make · Berkeley, California and Umea, Sweden · 2010s*
**Jennifer Doudna and Emmanuelle Charpentier** · edit DNA by typing a sequence

**Front.** Bacteria have an old way to fight the viruses that attack them. They keep a cutting tool that slices a virus apart, and they steer it with a short tag that matches the virus by spelling. [Emmanuelle Charpentier](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/chemistry/2020/press-release/) had been picking this defense apart in Sweden, and in 2011 she asked [Jennifer Doudna](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/chemistry/2020/press-release/) in Berkeley to help her rebuild it in a dish. In a [2012 paper](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6286148/) they showed what was new. Swap in your own short tag, written to match any spot you choose, and the cutter goes there. The aim was now a thing you type, not a thing you build.

**Back.** That turned editing DNA into a typing job. To aim an edit before, you had to build a fresh custom protein for each new target, slow and expensive. Now you ordered a short matching tag and let the same cutter follow it, [roughly 150 times cheaper](https://www.genetherapynet.com/gene-therapy-news/456-disruptive-crispr-gene-therapy-is-150-times-cheaper-than-zinc-fingers-and-crispr-is-faster-and-more-precise.html) than the old custom-protein way. Labs everywhere picked it up almost at once, and the two won the [Nobel in 2020](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/chemistry/2020/press-release/). The lead in editing pooled where the tag-and-cutter trick was born and turned into companies: the San Francisco Bay Area and the Boston biotech cluster.

Cross-thread, it rhymes with the computing stack on [[Thread - Electronic Switching]] (the thread note names the parallel): the moat keeps climbing to the next abstraction layer in both wetware and silicon. **Nuance.** The ~150x-cheaper figure is a 2015 trade-press number (Nextbigfuture, repeated by gene-therapy outlets), about $5,000 for the old custom protein versus as little as $30 for a guide, not a peer-reviewed cost study; per-edit dollar amounts circulate widely but are approximate, so the face says "roughly." On the face the cutting tool is Cas9, the short steering tag is the guide RNA, and the old custom proteins were zinc-finger (and TALEN) nucleases; all named plainly here and kept off the front per the deck's no-jargon rule. Credit is genuinely shared and contested: Charpentier found the tracrRNA piece and brought the question; Doudna brought the RNA biochemistry; the work spanned three sites, not only Sweden, with Krzysztof Chylinski and Ines Fonfara in Vienna; the 2012 paper's first author was Martin Jinek; and a separate priority dispute runs with the Broad Institute (Feng Zhang) over editing in animal and human cells, which the patent fights and the Nobel committee's narrower wording both reflect. The card credits the two Nobel laureates for the programmable-editor demonstration, not for every downstream step.
