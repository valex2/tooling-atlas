*Make · Berkeley, California and Umea, Sweden · 2010s*
**Jennifer Doudna and Emmanuelle Charpentier** · edit DNA by typing a sequence

**Front.** Bacteria have an old way to fight the viruses that attack them. They keep a cutting tool that slices a virus apart, and they steer it with a short tag that matches the virus by spelling. [Emmanuelle Charpentier](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/chemistry/2020/press-release/) had been picking this defense apart in Sweden, and in 2011 she asked [Jennifer Doudna](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/chemistry/2020/press-release/) in Berkeley to help her rebuild it in a dish. Their [2012 paper](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6286148/) showed that you could swap in your own short tag, written to match any spot you choose, and the cutter would go there. Aiming an edit had become a thing you type, not a thing you build.

**Back.** That turned editing DNA into a typing job. To aim an edit before, you had to build a fresh custom protein for each new target, slow and expensive. Now you ordered a short matching tag and let the same cutter follow it, [roughly 150 times cheaper](https://www.genetherapynet.com/gene-therapy-news/456-disruptive-crispr-gene-therapy-is-150-times-cheaper-than-zinc-fingers-and-crispr-is-faster-and-more-precise.html) than the old custom-protein way. Labs everywhere picked it up almost at once, and the two won the [Nobel in 2020](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/chemistry/2020/press-release/). When the trick turned into companies, it did so in the San Francisco Bay Area and the Boston biotech cluster, and the editing lead has stayed in those two clusters since.
