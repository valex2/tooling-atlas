*Manufacture · Luoyang, Han China · 100s*
**Cai Lun** · a screen dipped in wet pulp

**Front.** In 105 CE, [Cai Lun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cai_Lun), a court official in Luoyang who ran the emperor's workshops, wrote up a cheap way to make something to write on. Officials still kept records on strips of bamboo heavy enough to need a cart, or on silk too dear for daily use. He had [bark, hemp, old rags and worn fishnets](https://www.worldhistory.org/article/1120/paper-in-ancient-china/) pounded in water into a wet pulp, dipped a mesh screen to lift a thin even mat of fibre, then pressed and dried it flat. Scraps of paper had been pressed in China [two hundred years before](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hemp_paper) him, but each maker worked their own way; Cai Lun set down one recipe, made from waste, that anyone could copy and repeat.

**Back.** The screen-and-pulp method left China slowly. When Tang soldiers were captured at the [Battle of Talas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Talas) in 751, papermakers among them carried the craft to Samarkand and then Baghdad, where mills beat cotton and linen rags in place of bark, and from there it reached Egypt, Muslim Spain, and the first Italian mills by the [1200s](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_paper). Cheap even sheets opened the way to books and, much later, the printing press. The lead followed the mills west each time, so a Han court recipe ended up feeding Europe's presses a thousand years on.
