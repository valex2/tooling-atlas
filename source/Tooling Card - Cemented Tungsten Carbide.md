*Manufacture · Berlin, Germany · 1920s*
**Karl Schröter (Osram/Krupp WIDIA)** · carbide grains cemented with cobalt

**Front.** In [1923](https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-031-07175-1_1), an engineer named [Karl Schröter](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0263436814000730) worked at Osram, the Berlin firm that made Germany's light bulbs. Every bulb needed a hair-thin tungsten wire, and the wire had to be pulled through holes drilled in diamonds, which postwar Germany could barely buy. Schröter took powdered tungsten carbide, a man-made grit nearly as hard as diamond but too brittle to use by itself, mixed in a small measure of cobalt, pressed the mix into shape, and baked it until the cobalt melted and cemented the hard grains into one dense block. His patent, filed [March 30, 1923](https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-031-07175-1_1), described a material close to diamond in hardness that a factory could now press into any shape it wanted.

**Back.** Osram made lamps, not machine tools, so at the end of [1925 it sold the license to Krupp](https://www.nashira-hm.it/en/history/), which pressed cutting tips in secret from April 1926 and sold them as [WIDIA](https://nanopartikel.info/en/knowledge/materials/tungsten-carbide-cobalt/), short for wie Diamant, like diamond. At the [Leipzig fair in 1927](https://www.tsijournals.com/articles/the-100th-anniversary-of-the-establishment-of-the-carbide-carbide-bit.html), a tip of 94 parts carbide to 6 parts cobalt cut metal roughly ten times faster than the best tool steel, and shops had to rebuild their lathes stiffer just to keep up. Krupp licensed [General Electric in 1928](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/district-courts/FSupp/80/989/1869204/), and GE raised the American price from $48 a pound to $453, a dollar a gram, until the courts broke the cartel in 1948; nearly every metal-cutting edge in the world today is still carbide grains cemented with cobalt.
