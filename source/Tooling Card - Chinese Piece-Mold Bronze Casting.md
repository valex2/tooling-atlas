*Manufacture · Anyang, Shang China · -1200s*
**Shang foundries** · cut-apart clay molds for bronze

**Front.** Around 1200 BCE, in the royal foundries at [Anyang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yinxu), the last capital of Shang China, casters took an order for a bronze cauldron to honor a dead queen. It had to stand taller than a man and wear sharp raised patterns over every face, and no whole clay mold could be pulled off a shape like that without tearing the pattern away. So they packed wet clay around a carved model, let it harden, then sliced the clay skin into many [pieces](https://www.metmuseum.org/essays/shang-and-zhou-dynasties-the-bronze-age-of-china) and lifted each one clear, fired them hard, and set them back together around an inner clay core, leaving a thin gap the width of the metal to come. The mold now came apart and went back together in sections, so it could hold fine relief that no single mold could carry, and molten bronze filled the gap in one pour.

**Back.** The piece-mold method let the Anyang foundries cast the [Houmuwu ding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Houmuwu_ding), a rectangular cooking vessel of 832.84 kilograms that is still the [heaviest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Houmuwu_ding) ancient bronze found anywhere. The rest of the Bronze Age world melted a wax model out of a mold and poured into the hollow it left behind; China went the other way, cutting hard molds apart and building them back, and its courts kept casting [ritual bronzes](https://www.metmuseum.org/essays/shang-and-zhou-dynasties-the-bronze-age-of-china) this way through the Shang and Zhou. The skill stayed home. It made China the master of big, crisp bronze while that mattered most, then faded as iron working spread and the great ritual vessels fell out of use.
