*Measure · Heidelberg, Germany · 1980s*
**Jacques Dubochet, Joachim Frank, Richard Henderson** · a molecule frozen mid-life

**Front.** To learn a protein's shape you used to have to coax it into a crystal and shoot X-rays at it, but many proteins refuse to crystallize, and an electron beam dries out and fries a delicate molecule in a vacuum. In the early 1980s [Jacques Dubochet found the fix: plunge the sample into coolant so fast the water froze glassy](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/chemistry/2017/press-release/) rather than into sharp ice crystals, locking the molecule in its natural shape. [Joachim Frank worked out the math to merge thousands of faint, noisy images into one sharp three-dimensional shape](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/chemistry/2017/press-release/), and [Richard Henderson pushed the method to atomic detail](https://cen.acs.org/articles/95/web/2017/10/Cryo-electron-microscopy-innovators-win-2017-Nobel-Prize-in-Chemistry.html). The new thing was seeing a molecule's true shape frozen mid-life, no crystal required.

**Back.** For years it gave only blurry blobs, until [around 2013 new electron detectors brought it to near-atomic resolution](https://cen.acs.org/articles/95/web/2017/10/Cryo-electron-microscopy-innovators-win-2017-Nobel-Prize-in-Chemistry.html), the "resolution revolution," and it became how a huge share of new structures are now solved, winning the [2017 Nobel Prize](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/chemistry/2017/press-release/). The shapes it pours out, alongside X-ray crystallography, are the very pile of structures a machine then learned to predict. It is the second great way to answer the oldest question in structural biology, what shape is this molecule, the one the deck's whole DNA thread loops around.
