*Measure · Cambridge, England · 1970s*
**Frederick Sanger** · a way to read the DNA letters

**Front.** In 1977, in a lab in Cambridge, [Frederick Sanger](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/chemistry/1980/sanger/facts/) worked out how to read the order of the letters in a strand of DNA. A living thing carries its instructions as a long string of four chemical letters, and by the 1970s scientists knew the letters were there but could not read them in order. Sanger's trick was to make many copies of a stretch of DNA and rig the copying so each copy stopped at a different letter. That gave him a pile of pieces, every length from short to long. Sort them by size, smallest at the bottom, and read the pile from the bottom up: the order of the letters falls out. With it he read the [whole 5,386-letter code of a tiny virus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phi_X_174), the first time anyone had spelled out the full instructions of a living thing. The new thing was not the DNA. It was a way to read it.

**Back.** Reading the code was now possible, but slow and costly. The work won Sanger his [second Nobel Prize in 1980](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/chemistry/1980/sanger/facts/), and it opened a forty-year race to read faster. The first full human code took an army of labs [thirteen years and about $2.7 billion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_Genome_Project), finished in 2003. Then machines, most of them from one San Diego company, [Illumina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Illumina,_Inc.), read a million pieces at once instead of one at a time, and the price fell [faster than computer chips got cheap](https://www.genome.gov/about-genomics/fact-sheets/DNA-Sequencing-Costs-Data). By 2014 a human code cost [about $1,000](https://investor.illumina.com/news/press-release-details/2014/Illumina-Introduces-the-HiSeq-X-Ten-Sequencing-System/default.aspx) and took a day. Sanger gave the world the read; the lead in doing it cheaply moved to whoever built the better machine.
