*Measure · Cambridge, England · 1970s*
**Frederick Sanger** · a way to read the DNA letters

**Front.** In 1977, in a lab in Cambridge, [Frederick Sanger](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/chemistry/1980/sanger/facts/) worked out how to read the order of the letters in a strand of DNA. A living thing carries its instructions as a long string of four chemical letters, and by the 1970s scientists knew the letters were there but could not read them in order. Sanger's trick was to make many copies of a stretch of DNA and rig the copying so each copy stopped at a different letter. That gave him a pile of pieces, every length from short to long. Sort them by size, smallest at the bottom, and read the pile from the bottom up: the order of the letters falls out. With it he read the [whole 5,386-letter code of a tiny virus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phi_X_174), the first time anyone had spelled out the full instructions of a living thing. The new thing was not the DNA. It was a way to read it.

**Back.** Reading the code was now possible, but slow and costly. The work won Sanger his [second Nobel Prize in 1980](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/chemistry/1980/sanger/facts/), and it opened a forty-year race to read faster. The first full human code took an army of labs [thirteen years and about $2.7 billion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_Genome_Project), finished in 2003. Then machines, most of them from one San Diego company, [Illumina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Illumina,_Inc.), read a million pieces at once instead of one at a time, and the price fell [faster than computer chips got cheap](https://www.genome.gov/about-genomics/fact-sheets/DNA-Sequencing-Costs-Data). By 2014 a human code cost [about $1,000](https://investor.illumina.com/news/press-release-details/2014/Illumina-Introduces-the-HiSeq-X-Ten-Sequencing-System/default.aspx) and took a day. Sanger gave the world the read; the lead in doing it cheaply moved to whoever built the better machine.

*Curator's note:* **Placement.** Measure cell, Cambridge, 1977. The tool is a way to read the order of the four DNA letters, which makes it Measure, not Make. Serves Goal 1 (knowing DNA carried the code was not the same as being able to read it; reading was the bottleneck the science waited on) and Goal 2 (the lead moved: the method was born British, the cheap-reading machines became American at Illumina, and the lead is now contested by China's MGI, covered in the home-doc write-up). Confidence: Direct on the 1977 method, the phiX174 first full genome, the 1980 second Nobel, and the cost collapse, all multiply sourced. **Analysis.** This is the "read" tool of [[Thread - Reading & Writing DNA]], the bottom of the read/write/edit stack: you cannot copy, cut, or edit a code you cannot first read. It pairs forward against the edit tool, [[Tooling Card - CRISPR-Cas9]] — and the link is literal, not just thematic, because the repeats that CRISPR was built from were [first spotted in 1987 while reading a bacterial genome](https://journals.asm.org/doi/10.1128/jb.00580-17). Reading enabled the edit. Against the deck's un-copyable craft cards ([[Tooling Card - The EUV Machine|EUV]], [[Tooling Card - The High-Yield Process Recipe|turbine casting]]) this is the opposite outcome on purpose: the chemistry is published and the patents expire, so the lead did not lock in place, it migrated. **Nuance.** Mechanism is tagged Convergence (the cost collapse pools shared parallel-reading machinery, the way the reservoir pools craft), but it strains, and the strain is the point: a true convergence moat compounds and stays, while this lead *moved*, so the deck's migration reading fits the back better than convergence does. The face keeps it plain and does not name the tag. Sanger had an earlier Nobel in 1958 for reading the order of a protein; the 1980 prize for DNA was his second, shared with Walter Gilbert and Paul Berg. The $2.7 billion and thirteen years are the whole Human Genome Project (1990–2003); the US genome agency puts the first sequence alone nearer $300 million, so the face uses the well-known project headline figure, not the lower one. The $1,000 figure is Illumina's 2014 marketing claim and assumed the machines ran flat out; the home doc carries that caveat and the later $200/$100 numbers. The face says "most of them from one company" rather than "all," since Oxford Nanopore and others also read DNA.
