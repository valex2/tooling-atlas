*Make · Boulder, USA · 1980s*
**Marvin Caruthers** · DNA written to order, base by base

**Front.** By 1980 you could read a stretch of DNA, but to make a specific one from scratch took months of slow, failure-prone chemistry. In 1981 a chemist at Colorado named [Marvin Caruthers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marvin_H._Caruthers) introduced a better set of building blocks, [protected phosphoramidites](https://ipwatchdog.com/2018/07/03/synthesize-dna-marvin-caruthers-revolutionized-dna-synthesis/), that snapped onto a growing chain cleanly and fast. The chain was built on a tiny solid bead, one chosen base added per cycle, [each step joining with about 99.9% success](https://ipwatchdog.com/2018/07/03/synthesize-dna-marvin-caruthers-revolutionized-dna-synthesis/), reliable enough to hand to a machine. A machine could now write a chosen sequence letter by letter, and what had taken months took [less than a day](https://www.colorado.edu/asmagazine/2023/06/28/cu-boulders-marvin-caruthers-wins-inaugural-merkin-prize-biomedical-technology-developing).

**Back.** Made-to-order DNA became the raw feedstock of molecular biology. The short [primers that PCR needs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marvin_H._Caruthers), the guide strands that steer CRISPR, the genes that synthetic biology stitches together, all are written this way. Four decades on, every DNA synthesizer in the world still runs the phosphoramidite chemistry Caruthers worked out in Boulder.
