*Make · Boulder, USA · 1980s*
**Marvin Caruthers** · DNA written to order, base by base

**Front.** By 1980 you could read a stretch of DNA, but to make a specific one from scratch took months of slow, failure-prone chemistry. In 1981 a chemist at Colorado named [Marvin Caruthers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marvin_H._Caruthers) introduced a better set of building blocks, [protected phosphoramidites](https://ipwatchdog.com/2018/07/03/synthesize-dna-marvin-caruthers-revolutionized-dna-synthesis/), that snapped onto a growing chain cleanly and fast. The chain was built on a tiny solid bead, one chosen base added per cycle, [each step joining with about 99.9% success](https://ipwatchdog.com/2018/07/03/synthesize-dna-marvin-caruthers-revolutionized-dna-synthesis/), reliable enough to hand to a machine. What had taken months now took [less than a day](https://www.colorado.edu/asmagazine/2023/06/28/cu-boulders-marvin-caruthers-wins-inaugural-merkin-prize-biomedical-technology-developing). The new thing was writing DNA to order, letter by letter, on demand.

**Back.** Made-to-order DNA became the raw feedstock of molecular biology. The short [primers that PCR needs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marvin_H._Caruthers), the guide strands that steer CRISPR, the genes that synthetic biology stitches together, all are written this way, and every DNA synthesizer in the world still runs Caruthers' chemistry. Having learned to read the code, biology learned to type in it.

A published, non-rival method, so its leadership is universal use, not a moat. **Nuance.** Caruthers' 1981 phosphoramidite chemistry is the dated step; automated synthesizers (Applied Biosystems and others) followed within a couple of years and are the form most people meet, folded in here. Earlier DNA synthesis (Khorana, 1960s) existed but was far too slow to be a routine tool.
