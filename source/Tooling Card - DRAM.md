*Make · Yorktown Heights, USA · 1960s*
**Robert Dennard** · a bit held as charge in one cell

**Front.** A computer's memory in 1966 was a grid of tiny magnetic rings, each threaded by hand with fine wires, bulky and dear and slow to shrink. [Robert Dennard](https://www.ibm.com/history/dram), an engineer at IBM, worked out a far simpler way to hold a single bit: park a little [charge on a capacitor, and put one transistor in front of it as a gate](https://www.historyofinformation.com/detail.php?id=840) to let you write the charge in or read it out. One transistor, one capacitor, per bit. It was so simple you could pack thousands, then millions, onto one sliver of silicon. The catch was in the name, dynamic: the charge leaks away, so the chip has to stop and top every cell back up many times a second. Memory no longer had to be threaded by hand; it could be printed on silicon by the million.

**Back.** Intel turned it into a product with the [1103 in 1970](https://timeline.intel.com/1970/the-intel-1103-dram), and within two years it had beaten magnetic-core memory and become the memory in every computer. But the design was simple and shared, so the lead went to whoever could make it best, and that was not America for long. Japanese firms, NEC, Toshiba, Hitachi, built cleaner factories with [yields 20 to 30% higher and took about 80% of the market by the mid-1980s](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semiconductor_industry_in_Japan). That [drove Intel out of memory entirely in 1985](https://timeline.intel.com/1985/farewell-to-dram) and into microprocessors instead. Then Korea, Samsung and Hynix, took it from Japan on sheer scale and cheap capital, [overtaking them by 1992](https://history-computer.com/inventions/dram/). The chip was invented in America, and America never got the lead back.
