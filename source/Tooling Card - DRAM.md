*Make · Yorktown Heights, USA · 1960s*
**Robert Dennard** · a bit held as charge in one cell

**Front.** In 1966 a computer's memory was a grid of tiny magnetic rings, each threaded by hand with fine wires, bulky and dear and slow to shrink. [Robert Dennard](https://www.ibm.com/history/dram), an engineer at IBM, worked out a far simpler way to hold a single bit: park a little [charge on a capacitor, and put one transistor in front of it as a gate](https://www.historyofinformation.com/detail.php?id=840) to let you write the charge in or read it out. One transistor, one capacitor, per bit. It was so simple you could pack thousands, then millions, onto one sliver of silicon. The catch was in the name, dynamic: the charge leaks away, so the chip has to stop and top every cell back up many times a second. The new thing was that memory could now be printed on silicon by the million instead of threaded by hand.

**Back.** Intel turned it into a product with the [1103 in 1970](https://timeline.intel.com/1970/the-intel-1103-dram), and within two years it had beaten magnetic-core memory and become the memory in every computer. But the design was simple and shared, so the lead went to whoever could make it best, and that was not America for long. Japanese firms, NEC, Toshiba, Hitachi, built cleaner factories with [yields 20 to 30% higher and took about 80% of the market by the mid-1980s](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semiconductor_industry_in_Japan), which [drove Intel out of memory entirely in 1985](https://timeline.intel.com/1985/farewell-to-dram) and into microprocessors instead. Then Korea, Samsung and Hynix, took it from Japan on sheer scale and cheap capital, [overtaking them by 1992](https://history-computer.com/inventions/dram/). The chip was invented in America, and America never got the lead back.

*Curator's note:* **Placement.** Make cell, IBM, 1966, on [[Thread - Electronic Switching]]. Goal Both; Mechanism Pace-layers, the manufacturing capability being the slow, hard-won layer that, once it moved east, did not move back. Confidence Direct. **Analysis.** This is the deck's cleanest digital-age migration, the gauge story retold in silicon: a commodity whose design is shared, so leadership follows the best manufacturer and slides region to region, America to Japan to Korea. It connects straight to [[Tooling Card - The Microprocessor]], since losing DRAM is exactly what pushed Intel into the processor business it then led. It also fills the deck's memory gap, the counterpart to all the logic-chip cards. The migration is the point, so unlike a moat card it ends on the lead leaving, not staying. **Nuance.** Dennard invented the cell in 1966 and the patent issued in 1968; the Intel 1103 (1970) is the commercial start. The Japanese 80% share is mid-1980s; Korea's overtaking of Japan is usually dated to 1992. Today memory is a Korea-led oligopoly (Samsung, SK Hynix) with Micron the lone large American maker, and the high-bandwidth memory stacked next to AI chips is the latest prize on the same migrating road.
