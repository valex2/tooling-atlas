*Manufacture · Coalbrookdale, England · 1700s*
**Abraham Darby** · smelting iron with coke fuel

**Front.** On a January day in 1709, at a rented furnace in the wooded Severn valley at [Coalbrookdale](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Abraham-Darby), an ironmaster named [Abraham Darby](https://www.encyclopedia.com/people/history/british-and-irish-history-biographies/abraham-darby) had a fuel problem. English furnaces melted their iron with charcoal, and the woods that made charcoal were being cut faster than they grew, so the fuel was scarce and dear and no furnace could burn much of it at once. Darby baked local coal in heaps until its smoke and tar burned off, leaving a hard grey cinder called [coke](https://www.environmentandsociety.org/tools/keywords/coke-blast-furnaces), and fed that into the furnace instead. The coke burned hot and stood up under a tall heavy charge of ore, so for the first time iron was smelted with coal, not wood.

**Back.** The payoff came slow. Darby's coke iron came out brittle and full of phosphorus, fine for casting cookpots but too poor for the wrought bars that smiths worked into tools and rails. His son [Abraham Darby II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abraham_Darby_II) spent years working out a coke pig iron the forges could take, and even so coke smelting did not spread across the trade for about [forty years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abraham_Darby_II). By the time the grandson cast the ribs of the [Iron Bridge](https://www.encyclopedia.com/people/history/british-and-irish-history-biographies/abraham-darby) in 1779, British furnaces grew on the coal seam, not the vanished woods, and Britain became the iron works of the world.
