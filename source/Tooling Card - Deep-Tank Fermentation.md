*Manufacture · Peoria, Illinois, USA · 1940s*
**Andrew Moyer and the Peoria lab** · grow mold by the thousand-gallon batch

**Front.** In July 1941, with Britain bombed and rationed, the Oxford biochemist Norman Heatley carried a sample of penicillin mold to the [USDA's Northern Regional Research Laboratory in Peoria, Illinois](https://www.ars.usda.gov/midwest-area/peoria-il/national-center-for-agricultural-utilization-research/docs/penicillin-opening-the-era-of-antibiotics/). The drug cured infections, but the mold only made it in thin films on the surface of a liquid, so Oxford had grown it a few milligrams at a time in [700 bedpan-shaped ceramic dishes](https://www.nms.ac.uk/discover-catalogue/how-a-ceramic-object-fueled-a-medical-breakthrough). The Peoria team did three things to that mold: they fed it [corn steep liquor, a corn-milling waste stream that raised yields about tenfold](https://www.invent.org/inductees/andrew-j-moyer), they screened a far richer strain, and then they grew it not on a surface but throughout a deep steel tank with sterile air bubbled through the whole volume. Tons of living mold now hung fed, oxygenated, and free of any stray germ in one giant stirred vat, where before there had been only the thin skin on a tray.

**Back.** [Pfizer already knew this craft from making citric acid by deep-tank fermentation](https://www.thechemicalengineer.com/features/cewctw-pfizers-penicillin-pioneers-jasper-kane-and-john-mckeen), and converted it almost directly: its Brooklyn plant opened in [March 1944 with fourteen 7,500-gallon tanks](https://www.acs.org/education/whatischemistry/landmarks/penicillin.html). US output climbed from [21 billion units in 1943 to more than 6.8 trillion in 1945](https://www.lib.niu.edu/2001/iht810139.html), enough that most of the penicillin that came ashore on D-Day was American. The published chemistry could not even make the drug; only the tanks could. The same vats in Brooklyn soon grew streptomycin and the antibiotics that built the postwar US drug industry.
