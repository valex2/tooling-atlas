*Manufacture · Peoria, Illinois, USA · 1940s*
**Andrew Moyer and the Peoria lab** · grow mold by the thousand-gallon batch

**Front.** In July 1941, with Britain bombed and rationed, the Oxford biochemist Norman Heatley carried a sample of penicillin mold to the [USDA's Northern Regional Research Laboratory in Peoria, Illinois](https://www.ars.usda.gov/midwest-area/peoria-il/national-center-for-agricultural-utilization-research/docs/penicillin-opening-the-era-of-antibiotics/). The drug cured infections, but the mold only made it in thin films on the surface of a liquid, so Oxford had grown it a few milligrams at a time in [700 bedpan-shaped ceramic dishes](https://www.nms.ac.uk/discover-catalogue/how-a-ceramic-object-fueled-a-medical-breakthrough). The Peoria team did three things to that mold: they fed it [corn steep liquor, a corn-milling waste stream that raised yields about tenfold](https://www.invent.org/inductees/andrew-j-moyer), they screened a far richer strain, and then they grew it not on a surface but throughout a deep steel tank with sterile air bubbled through the whole volume. What was new was the vessel: instead of stacking trays, you kept tons of a living mold fed, oxygenated, and free of any stray germ in one giant stirred vat.

**Back.** [Pfizer already knew this craft from making citric acid by deep-tank fermentation](https://www.thechemicalengineer.com/features/cewctw-pfizers-penicillin-pioneers-jasper-kane-and-john-mckeen), and converted it almost directly: its Brooklyn plant opened in [March 1944 with fourteen 7,500-gallon tanks](https://www.acs.org/education/whatischemistry/landmarks/penicillin.html). US output climbed from [21 billion units in 1943 to more than 6.8 trillion in 1945](https://www.lib.niu.edu/2001/iht810139.html), enough that most of the penicillin that came ashore on D-Day was American. The published chemistry could not even make the drug, so the lead stayed where the tanks were, and the same vats soon grew streptomycin and the antibiotics that built the postwar US drug industry.

*Curator's note:*
Placement: Manufacture cell, Goal Both, Convergence, Direct confidence. A bioprocess endpoint of the deck's manufacturing arc.
Analysis: This is the deck's cleanest leadership-migration case. Britain held the discovery (Fleming, 1928) and the bench tools that measured and purified the drug (Heatley's Oxford unit, the back-extraction); America held the one tool that made it real at scale, and the lead followed the tool, not the discovery. It pairs as a foil against pure-discovery and pure-model cards: here the moat is tacit process craft (sterile engineering, aeration, contamination control) that no paper carries, the opposite of a non-rival published abstraction. The legacy long-form write-up lives in `Manufacturing Tooling - Deep-Tank Fermentation -> Penicillin.md`.
Nuance: Credit is distributed, not single-author. Andrew Moyer pinned down corn steep liquor; the deep-tank scale-up was a joint effort across the Peoria lab and the drug firms (Pfizer's Jasper Kane and John McKeen on the engineering side). The "Moldy Mary" cantaloupe strain (NRRL 1951) is well documented but its origin story carries some legend per USDA. I dated the card to 1944 (the Brooklyn deep-tank plant and the D-Day ramp); the Peoria media and strain work runs 1941-1943. Production figures (21 billion → 6.8 trillion units) are the standard ACS/NIU numbers; popular "one patient → whole army" framings are color, not audited counts.
