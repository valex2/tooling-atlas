*Make · Pasadena, California, USA · 1990s*
**Frances Arnold and Keqin Chen** · breed an enzyme instead of designing it

**Front.** [Frances Arnold](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/chemistry/2018/arnold/facts/) trained as an engineer, and for years at Caltech she tried to design proteins the way an engineer designs a bridge. It did not work. A protein is a long chain of small parts that folds into a shape, and nobody in 1993 could say which part to change to get a better shape. So she and her student [Keqin Chen](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.90.12.5618) stopped guessing. They took the gene for subtilisin E, a protein-cutting enzyme of the sort used in laundry soap, and copied it badly on purpose, so every copy carried a few random spelling mistakes. Each bad copy went into its own bacterium, each bacterium made its own slightly different enzyme, and all of them were tested in a harsh solvent, [60 percent dimethylformamide](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC46772/), where the ordinary enzyme barely works. They kept the best performer, copied that one badly, and went round again. Three rounds later the winner carried [ten changed parts and worked 256 times better](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC46772/) than the enzyme they started with, and not one of the ten sat in the cutting site. They were scattered across surface folds, in spots no designer would have touched.

**Back.** Other labs picked up the loop. Merck and a company called Codexis ran it on an enzyme that builds one mirror-image of a molecule and not the other, pushing it far enough to land [27 changes and about 25,000 times the starting activity](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20558668/); the bred enzyme then replaced a [250-psi hydrogen reactor and its rhodium catalyst](https://www.epa.gov/greenchemistry/presidential-green-chemistry-challenge-2010-greener-reaction-conditions-award) in the plant that makes the diabetes drug sitagliptin, lifting yield 10 to 13 percent and cutting waste by 19. Arnold took [half the 2018 Nobel Prize in Chemistry](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/chemistry/2018/summary/) for the loop itself. In 2017 [Nicole Gaudelli, in David Liu's lab](https://phys.org/news/2017-10-enzyme-rewrites-genome.html), needed an enzyme that turns an A into a G in DNA and found that nothing alive does it, so she bred one out of an [E. coli enzyme that only works on RNA](https://www.nature.com/articles/nature24644). The protein at the heart of every adenine base editor now in trials was grown in a Cambridge lab and exists nowhere in nature.
