*Make · Kibbutz Hatzerim, Israel · 1960s*
**Simcha Blass & Kibbutz Hatzerim (Netafim)** · a plastic line that drips at each root

**Front.** In the early [1930s](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simcha_Blass) a water engineer named [Simcha Blass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simcha_Blass) was shown a big tree growing in the dry Negev with no water in sight. He dug down and found the cause: a pipe nearby leaked one slow drop at a time, and the water spread underground in an onion-shaped patch right at the roots, so that one tree drank while its neighbours stayed thirsty. Cheap plastic pipe after the war let him copy the leak on purpose. With [Kibbutz Hatzerim in 1965](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Netafim) he made a plastic line with a dripper built into its wall, a long narrow winding path that used [friction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simcha_Blass) to slow the water until it seeped out drop by drop at each plant, in place of a plain hole that would clog.

**Back.** Water and dissolved fertilizer now went straight to the roots, and crops grew on desert sand that a flooded field could never carry, since flooding leaves only about [half its water](https://www.fao.org/fileadmin/user_upload/agwa/docs/Efficiency_Thematic%20Brief_En.pdf) for the plant while a drip line delivers close to all of it. Kibbutz Hatzerim turned the emitter into a firm, [Netafim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Netafim), that shipped its first commercial dripper in 1966 and now sells in more than 100 countries. The lead held, and Israel built a whole export trade on the metered line, though even today only about [three percent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drip_irrigation) of the world's farmers water this way and the rest still pour it on the ground.
