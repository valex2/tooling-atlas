*Model · Bern, Switzerland · 1905*
**Albert Einstein** · equation linking a particle's wander to atom count

**Front.** In 1905 a twenty-six-year-old [patent clerk in Bern](https://www.aps.org/funding-recognition/historic-sites/einsteinhaus), Albert Einstein, was trying to find a way to prove that atoms were real and not just a handy story chemists told. The trouble was that no one could see an atom, so no one could count them. Einstein worked out, on paper, a short [equation](https://physicsworld.com/a/einsteins-random-walk/): the average squared distance a tiny floating speck drifts equals the temperature times a known gas constant, divided by the number of atoms in a standard amount of matter, the speck's size, and the thickness of the liquid, all times how long you watch. Everything in that equation could be measured with a microscope and a clock, except the atom count, which you could then solve for. The new thing was not the jiggle, known since 1827. It was the bridge: a number you could see, the wander of a speck, set equal to a number you could not, the atoms in a spoonful of matter.

**Back.** Three years later the physicist [Jean Perrin](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jean-Perrin) made up batches of tiny gamboge balls all the same size, timed how far they drifted under his microscope, and put the distances into Einstein's equation. Counting the wander of [hundreds of granules](https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/how-was-avogadros-number/), he solved for the atom count and landed near 6.9 × 10²³, close to the value used today, and the last serious holdouts against atoms gave way. The equation was printed and free, so the whole world held the tool at once: the fame went to Einstein and the [1926 Nobel](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Jean-Perrin) to Perrin, but the method itself was no one's to own.

Tagged Convergence only as the nearest of the three; folded into the framework as "Models are the exception" in [[Tooling Leadership Home]]. **Nuance.** The equation is usually written ⟨x²⟩ = (RT / 3πN aη)·t, with N the atom count (Avogadro's number), a the speck's radius, η the liquid's viscosity. Brown saw the jiggle in 1827 and ruled out life by seeing the same motion in dust and ground rock; Einstein explained it in 1905; Perrin measured it in 1908-1909. Perrin's gamboge-displacement series gave roughly 6.9 × 10²³, with other series running to about 7.0-7.05 × 10²³; today's value is about 6.022 × 10²³. Perrin's 1926 Nobel citation reads "the discontinuous structure of matter."
