*Model · Alexandria, Ptolemaic Egypt · -300s*
**Euclid** · state a few rules, then prove the rest

**Front.** Around [300 BCE](https://www.worldhistory.org/Euclid/), in the new Greek city of Alexandria in Egypt, a teacher named [Euclid](https://www.lindahall.org/about/news/scientist-of-the-day/euclid-of-alexandria/) set out to write down what people knew about shapes and numbers. The trouble was that the results sat in scattered pieces, each one argued its own way, with no shared ground to stand on. So he opened his book with a short list of things he only asked the reader to grant (a point has no parts, all right angles are equal, [five such rules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%27s_Elements) in all), and from that list alone he proved [465 statements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%27s_Elements), each leaning only on the ones already shown. The order was new: grant a few plain rules, then build every later truth by a chain of proof anyone could check.

**Back.** Because the book taught its own method, people could copy it, and they did for a very long time. Scribes carried it through the Greek and then the Arab world, and when print arrived the [Elements](https://www.historyofinformation.com/detail.php?entryid=382) was among the first books off the new presses at Venice in 1482, then ran to [more than a thousand editions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%27s_Elements). Newton later wrote his laws of motion in the same shape, rules first and proofs after, and school geometry still runs on it two thousand years on. Euclid gathered most of the proofs from [earlier Greeks](https://www.worldhistory.org/Euclid/) rather than finding them himself; what he made was the way of laying them out, and the method traveled better than any single result in the book.
