*Measure · Pisa, Italy · 1280s*
**Anonymous Pisan lens-grinders** · ground glass lenses worn on the face

**Front.** Around 1286, somewhere in northern Italy, a craftsman took two small disks of glass, ground each one thicker in the middle than at the edge, and set them in a frame that rested on the nose. The fat middle of the glass bent light inward, so an aging eye that could no longer focus up close could read again. We do not know his name; the maker kept his method secret. A Dominican friar in Pisa, [Alessandro della Spina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alessandro_della_Spina), worked out how to grind the same lenses himself and, a 1313 monastery chronicle says, taught anyone who asked. In 1306 another Pisan friar, [Giordano da Pisa](https://www.cabinet.ox.ac.uk/spectacles-technology-unknown-ancients), told a congregation that "it is not yet twenty years since the art of making eyeglasses was found," and said he had met the man who first made them.

**Back.** The new thing was not a discovery about light but a wearable tool, and a workshop skill: grinding a curved face onto glass and checking it by eye until it focused. That skill spread from Italy through the [glassmakers of Venice and Nuremberg](https://www.medievalists.net/2016/03/medieval-eyeglasses-wearable-technology-of-the-thirteenth-century/) over the next three centuries, and the trade stayed close to the craft. When [Galileo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galileo_Galilei) wanted lenses for his telescope in 1609, he was reaching into a craft that spectacle-makers had been refining for three hundred years. The lead held wherever the grinding skill lived, not where the first frame was riveted.

*Curator's note:* **Placement.** Measure cell, Goal Both, Convergence mechanism, Confidence Plausible: the 1286 date rests on Giordano's 1306 sermon, a strong primary source, but no inventor is named. **Analysis.** This is the head of the Precision Optics thread, the parade card that sits before [[Tooling Card - Galileo's Telescope]] and [[Tooling Card - Leeuwenhoek's Microscope]] (the microscope link is a downstream rhyme, left unwired). The deck-point: the design (a convex lens) was simple and public, but the hand-skill to grind one true was the thing that traveled. **Nuance.** Florence's Salvino degli Armati is the traditional "inventor," but that claim is a [1684 fabrication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salvino_D%27Armati) exposed in 1920, so I placed the card in Pisa, the city with documented early makers. The 1286 year and the ~20-years figure both come from Giordano's sermon; treat them as the best estimate, not a fixed fact.
