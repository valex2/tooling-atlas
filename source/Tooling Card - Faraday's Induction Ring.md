*Make · London, Britain · 1830s*
**Michael Faraday** · motion turned into current

**Front.** [Michael Faraday](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Michael-Faraday) had been a bookbinder's apprentice with almost no schooling in mathematics before a chemist took him on as a lab helper at London's Royal Institution. By 1831 he was chasing one question: a current was known to make magnetism, so could magnetism be made to give back a current? On [29 August 1831 he wound two separate coils of wire on opposite sides of an iron ring](https://www.aps.org/apsnews/2001/08/faraday-electromagnetism), connected one coil to a battery, and watched a [needle on the other coil twitch](https://www.aps.org/apsnews/2001/08/faraday-electromagnetism). The needle moved only at the instant he connected or broke the battery, never while the current sat steady. It was the *change* in the magnetism that pushed a current through a wire touching no battery at all. The whole thing was cheap apparatus and no mathematics, one man at a bench.

**Back.** Later that year Faraday spun a copper disc between the poles of a magnet and drew off a continuous current, the [first dynamo, the first generator](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/rsta/article/373/2039/20140208/114841/The-birth-of-the-electric-machines-a-commentary-on). Every generator since runs on that one logic, from a [bicycle dynamo to a hydroelectric dam](https://www.aps.org/apsnews/2001/08/faraday-electromagnetism), and so does every electric motor and every transformer that steps power up and down across the grid. This is the Little Science card at its purest: every power station on Earth is that one bench experiment scaled up.
