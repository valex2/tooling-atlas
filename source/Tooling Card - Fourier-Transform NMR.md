*Measure · Zurich, Switzerland · 1960s*
**Richard Ernst** · one radio pulse and a transform

**Front.** In 1966, a young Swiss chemist named [Richard Ernst](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/chemistry/1991/ernst/biographical/) was working at an instrument maker, [Varian Associates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Varian_Associates) in Palo Alto, trying to pull a signal out of samples too faint to read. The machines of the day swept a radio frequency slowly across the sample, one thin slice at a time, so a weak sample could take hours and still show nothing. With [Weston Anderson](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/Application-of-Fourier-Transform-Spectroscopy-to-Ernst-Anderson/bb3062e99e5fa319e208ee8941663147d7d87f47) he tried a blunter move: hit all the nuclei at once with one short, hard pulse of radio waves, catch the jumbled signal as it rang down, and hand it to a computer to Fourier-transform the tangle back into a clean row of peaks. One pulse now gave the whole spectrum in the time the old machine had spent on a single slice of it.

**Back.** The same spectrum came out in [a hundredth of the time](https://pubs.aip.org/aip/rsi/article/37/1/93/456193/Application-of-Fourier-Transform-Spectroscopy-to), or ten times cleaner in the hours you already had. Chemists could suddenly read faint nuclei like carbon-13 and work out the shape of a molecule from a few milligrams of it. The recipe was in print, so the machines spread fast: [Bruker](https://www.bruker.com/en/about/history-new.html) shipped the first commercial pulse-Fourier spectrometer in 1969, beating Varian, which held the patent. Ernst carried the method home to [ETH Zurich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ETH_Zurich), stacked pulses into two-dimensional NMR, and won the [1991 Nobel Prize in Chemistry](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/chemistry/1991/summary/) for it.
