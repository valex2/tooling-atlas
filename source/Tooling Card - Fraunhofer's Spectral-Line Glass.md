*Measure · Benediktbeuern, Bavaria · 1810s*
**Joseph von Fraunhofer** · glass measured to the line

**Front.** Even with [Guinand's](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39607049/) good glass, making an achromatic lens, one that does not fringe an image with color, meant trial and error, because no one could measure exactly how much a given glass bent each color of light. In [1814 Joseph von Fraunhofer](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1002/andp.201400807), an orphaned glazier's apprentice turned master optician, looked at sunlight through a prism and his own fine instruments and saw [hundreds of sharp dark lines at fixed colors](https://handwiki.org/wiki/Physics:Fraunhofer_lines). He realized each line was an exact, repeatable marker, a wavelength ruler, against which he could [measure a glass's refractive index and dispersion far more precisely than anyone before](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1002/andp.201400807). With those numbers he could *calculate* the perfect crown-and-flint pairing. The new thing was measuring the glass to the line, turning lens-making from guesswork into design.

**Back.** His telescopes and lenses became the best in the world, and Munich ruled precision optics. But he held his methods close, and [died at 39 in 1826](https://www.encyclopedia.com/science/dictionaries-thesauruses-pictures-and-press-releases/fraunhofer-joseph), likely poisoned by the heavy-metal fumes of his own glassworks. Much of the integrated craft went with him, and the lead faded until Abbe and Zeiss rebuilt it on open science decades later. Yet the measurement itself outlived the man: his three reference lines are [still the standard wavelengths optical glass is rated at, and the Abbe number is defined on them](https://www.rp-photonics.com/abbe_number.html). The craft died; the ruler did not.
