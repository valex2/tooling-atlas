*Measure · Padua, Italy · 1600s*
**Galileo Galilei** · ground lenses turned upward

**Front.** A math professor at Padua who was short of money, [Galileo Galilei](https://www.loc.gov/collections/finding-our-place-in-the-cosmos-with-carl-sagan/articles-and-essays/modeling-the-cosmos/galileo-and-the-telescope) heard in 1609 of a Dutch toy: a [spyglass that made far things look near](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sidereus_Nuncius). He never handled one, but reasoned out how it must work and ground his own lenses to do it better, pushing the magnification from about [8 times to 20, then near 30](https://www.loc.gov/collections/finding-our-place-in-the-cosmos-with-carl-sagan/articles-and-essays/modeling-the-cosmos/galileo-and-the-telescope), well past the toy. Then he did the thing no one had bothered to do: he pointed it at the night sky. He saw mountains and craters on a Moon that was supposed to be a perfect smooth ball, and [four little points of light circling Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galilean_moons) on 7 January 1610. The tube was Dutch; the lenses sharp enough to resolve those moons, and the nerve to aim them upward, were his alone.

**Back.** He rushed the findings into a thin book, [Sidereus Nuncius, 550 copies in March 1610](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sidereus_Nuncius), and the four moons circling Jupiter were proof that not everything turns around the Earth. This is one half of a pair. The same lone-bench seeing instinct, pointed out at the heavens, that a draper in Delft would soon turn the other way, down at a drop of water and the unseen things alive in it. The hand-ground lens of 1610 became the mountaintop observatory and, four centuries on, telescopes flown above the air looking back to the first galaxies.
