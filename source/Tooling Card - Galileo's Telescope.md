*Measure · Padua, Italy · 1600s*
**Galileo Galilei** · ground lenses turned upward

**Front.** In 1609 [Galileo Galilei](https://www.loc.gov/collections/finding-our-place-in-the-cosmos-with-carl-sagan/articles-and-essays/modeling-the-cosmos/galileo-and-the-telescope), a math professor at Padua who was short of money, heard of a Dutch toy: a [spyglass that made far things look near](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sidereus_Nuncius). He never handled one, but reasoned out how it must work and ground his own lenses to do it better, pushing the magnification from about [8 times to 20, then near 30](https://www.loc.gov/collections/finding-our-place-in-the-cosmos-with-carl-sagan/articles-and-essays/modeling-the-cosmos/galileo-and-the-telescope), well past the toy. Then he did the thing no one had bothered to do: he pointed it at the night sky. He saw mountains and craters on a Moon that was supposed to be a perfect smooth ball, and [four little points of light circling Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galilean_moons) on 7 January 1610. The new thing was not the tube, which was Dutch, but a lone man's sharper lenses aimed upward.

**Back.** He rushed the findings into a thin book, [Sidereus Nuncius, 550 copies in March 1610](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sidereus_Nuncius), and the four moons circling Jupiter were proof that not everything turns around the Earth. The lens telescope he made by hand grew, over four centuries, into mountaintop observatories and telescopes flown above the air. This is one half of a pair: the same lone-bench seeing instinct, pointed out at the heavens, that a draper in Delft would soon turn the other way, down at a drop of water and the unseen things alive in it. Two cheap glass instruments, two single minds, and the deep Measure tools that all the later nation-scale science was built to extend.
