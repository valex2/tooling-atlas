*Make · Zurich, Switzerland · 2000s*
**Ingo Potrykus & Peter Beyer** · genes that make grain golden

**Front.** In 1999, in a glasshouse at the technical university in Zurich, a plant scientist named [Ingo Potrykus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ingo_Potrykus) was thinking about children who eat little else but rice. Rice grain carries no vitamin A, so on a rice-only diet a small child can slowly go [blind](https://www.goldenrice.org/Content3-Why/why1_vad.php). With [Peter Beyer](https://embryo.asu.edu/pages/golden-rice) in Freiburg, he stitched three borrowed genes into rice cells, two taken from a [daffodil](https://www.goldenrice.org/Content2-How/how1_sci.php) and one from a soil bacterium. The genes switched on a chemical chain the grain had always kept shut, and the rice came out [yellow](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.287.5451.303), colored by the beta-carotene now packed inside, which a body turns into vitamin A.

**Back.** For the first time a staple crop grew its own provitamin A in the part people eat. Then golden rice sat for twenty years, held up by hard rules on engineered crops and by [protesters](https://www.science.org/content/article/activists-destroy-golden-rice-field-trial) who broke into a test plot in the Philippines and tore the plants out weeks before harvest. The [Philippines](https://www.irri.org/news-and-events/news/philippines-becomes-first-country-approve-nutrient-enriched-golden-rice) finally became the first country to clear it for planting, in 2021, more than two decades after the grain first turned yellow, years in which [hundreds of thousands](https://www.goldenrice.org/Content3-Why/why1_vad.php) of children went blind for want of the vitamin it carries.
