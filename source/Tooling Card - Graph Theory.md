*Model · Königsberg, Prussia (now Kaliningrad, Russia) · 1730s*
**Leonhard Euler** · dots and lines for a city's bridges

**Front.** In 1736 the people of [Königsberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seven_Bridges_of_K%C3%B6nigsberg) had a puzzle. Their town sat on two riverbanks and two islands, joined by seven bridges, and they wondered whether you could take a single walk that crossed each bridge once and only once. [Leonhard Euler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonhard_Euler) threw the map away. He drew each patch of land as one dot and each bridge as a line between dots, and saw that the distances and the shapes did not matter at all, only how many lines met at each dot. To enter and leave a dot on different bridges it needs an even count; with more than two dots holding an odd count, no such walk can exist. Königsberg had four. The answer was no, and the new thing was the move itself: keep only what connects to what.

**Back.** That short proof is counted as the [first theorem of graph theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_theory) and an early seed of topology. For two centuries it stayed a curiosity. Then dots-and-lines became the plain way to reason about every kind of network: circuits, molecules, roads, the telephone system, the web, and the ties between people. The idea is published and free to all, so it never handed one person a lasting lead. That went, every time, to whoever owned the biggest real graph.
