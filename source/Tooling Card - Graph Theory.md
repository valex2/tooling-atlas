*Model · Königsberg, Prussia (now Kaliningrad, Russia) · 1730s*
**Leonhard Euler** · dots and lines for a city's bridges

**Front.** In 1736 the people of [Königsberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seven_Bridges_of_K%C3%B6nigsberg) had a puzzle. Their town sat on two riverbanks and two islands, joined by seven bridges, and they wondered whether you could take a single walk that crossed each bridge once and only once. [Leonhard Euler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonhard_Euler) threw the map away. He drew each patch of land as one dot and each bridge as a line between dots, and saw that the distances and the shapes did not matter at all, only how many lines met at each dot. To enter and leave a dot on different bridges it needs an even count; with more than two dots holding an odd count, no such walk can exist. Königsberg had four. The answer was no. The lasting result was the move that got there: throw away the map and keep only what connects to what.

**Back.** That short proof is counted as the [first theorem of graph theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_theory) and an early seed of topology. For two centuries it stayed a curiosity. Then dots-and-lines became the plain way to reason about every kind of network: circuits, molecules, roads, the telephone system, the web, and the ties between people. Euler's proof sits under all of them and earned him nothing but the credit. The payoffs landed two centuries downstream, in the engines that ranked the web and the platforms that mapped a billion friendships.
