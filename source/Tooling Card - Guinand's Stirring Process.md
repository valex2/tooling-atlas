*Make · Les Brenets, Switzerland · 1800s*
**Pierre-Louis Guinand** · molten glass stirred until flawless

**Front.** Optical glass came out of the pot full of [striae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Striae_(glass)), faint swirls of slightly different density that bent light the wrong way and blurred any lens cut from the blank, so no one could make a large flawless lens. [Pierre-Louis Guinand](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39607049/), a Swiss cabinet-maker who taught himself glassmaking and began trial melts of flint glass around 1783, kept chasing the streaks out. By [1805 he found the trick: stir the molten glass with a fireclay rod as it cooled](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39607049/), folding the dense and thin parts together until the whole melt was even, and so he could pour blanks far larger and clearer than anyone's. The new thing was not a new glass but a way of *making* it even, a craft he guarded closely.

**Back.** His glass let opticians grind bigger, sharper lenses, and the [Munich optical firm that Fraunhofer worked for lured Guinand specifically to get it, and used his glass to overtake the London opticians](https://www.encyclopedia.com/science/dictionaries-thesauruses-pictures-and-press-releases/fraunhofer-joseph). The secret spread slowly and jealously through a few hands, his sons, then French and English makers, [later refined with platinum stirrers and vessels](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39607049/). It was the raw material under a century of better telescopes and microscopes, the craft Fraunhofer would measure to the line and Zeiss would one day industrialize.

Confidence Direct. **Analysis.** This is the material half of the optics gap, paired with [[Tooling Card - Fraunhofer's Spectral-Line Glass]] as make-the-glass and measure-the-glass. Together they fill the ~200-year hole between [[Tooling Card - Leeuwenhoek's Microscope]] and the Zeiss turn, and they sharpen the deck's optics lesson: leadership in lenses kept living inside guarded craft. Guinand is the gentler version of that, his secret did slowly transmit through his heirs and licensees, unlike Fraunhofer's, which mostly died with him, so the pair shows both how tacit craft can creep outward and how it can vanish. **Nuance.** Guinand began trials around 1783; the stirring breakthrough is dated to 1805. The fireclay (later platinum) stirrer is the instrument; the "process" is the stirring itself. He is sometimes credited jointly with the Munich firm, but the stirring method is squarely his, which is why the card centers him and not Fraunhofer for the glass.
