*Manufacture · Ludwigshafen, Germany · 1910s*
**Carl Bosch & Fritz Haber** · ammonia squeezed from the air

**Front.** Crops need nitrogen, and the air is [almost four-fifths nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haber_process), but in a locked form no plant can use. In 1909 [Fritz Haber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fritz_Haber) showed at the bench that you could force nitrogen from the air and hydrogen to join into ammonia, driven together under great pressure and heat over an iron catalyst. The trouble was doing it by the ton. [Carl Bosch](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/chemistry/1931/bosch/facts/) at BASF spent years building a vessel that could hold [roughly 200 atmospheres at 400 to 500 degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haber_process) without bursting or being eaten hollow from the inside, the first true high-pressure chemical plant, running at Oppau by 1913.

**Back.** Cheap ammonia became cheap fertilizer, and fertilizer fed the world. About [half the nitrogen in a human body today](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haber_process) has passed through one of these reactors, and the crops it grows keep billions of people alive. Its first use was darker: it let wartime Germany make explosives from the air after the British blockade cut off imported nitrates. The chemistry was published and won two Nobel prizes, but building and running a high-pressure plant was hard-won craft, and the lead stayed with the firms that mastered it.
