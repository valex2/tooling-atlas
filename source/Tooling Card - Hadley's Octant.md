*Measure · London, England · 1730s*
**John Hadley** · mirror tool that reads star height

**Front.** In May 1731, [John Hadley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Hadley) stood before the [Royal Society](https://www.lindahall.org/about/news/scientist-of-the-day/john-hadley/) in London and handed round a small brass instrument he had built. Sailors had long read their latitude by measuring how high the sun or a star rode above the sea, but on a rolling deck the old sighting sticks wobbled and a man could not hold his eye on the star and the horizon at once. Hadley fixed one mirror to a swinging arm and a second, half-silvered mirror at the eyepiece, so that as he tipped the arm he slid the star's reflection down until it sat right on the line of the horizon, then read the angle off the arc. The two mirrors were the trick: the star and the sea held still against each other in the same small view, so the pitch of the ship no longer spoiled the reading.

**Back.** The British Admiralty sent the instrument to sea for trials, and London workshops were soon turning them out for ships' officers, each one good to about [a minute of arc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octant_(instrument)) though its brass arc spanned only 45 degrees. A glazier in Philadelphia named [Thomas Godfrey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Godfrey_(inventor)) had built the same double-mirror device the winter before, and [Isaac Newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octant_(instrument)) had worked out the idea decades earlier and passed a description to Edmond Halley that stayed unpublished until 1742, yet Hadley's was the one the fleet took up and the name that stuck to it. Once a captain could pin his latitude from a heaving deck, the tool grew a longer arc and became the [sextant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sextant), which later joined the marine clock to fix longitude as well, and the lead in fine sea instruments sat with the London makers who kept building them.
