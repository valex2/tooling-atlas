*Manufacture · Meissen, Germany · 1700s*
**Johann Friedrich Böttger and Ehrenfried von Tschirnhaus** · kaolin and stone, fired white

**Front.** For centuries only China could make true porcelain: a body so thin and white you could see light through it, and it rang when you tapped it. The trick was the recipe and the heat. You mixed [kaolin, a soft white clay, with petuntse, a ground stone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Xavier_d'Entrecolles), and fired it near 1,400°C until the stone half-melted and locked the clay into a glassy, water-tight white. Europe shipped this "[white gold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meissen_porcelain)" home at great cost and could not copy it. Augustus the Strong of Saxony had locked up a young alchemist, [Johann Friedrich Böttger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Friedrich_B%C3%B6ttger), and ordered him to make gold; Böttger failed at gold and instead joined the porcelain work of the mathematician [Ehrenfried von Tschirnhaus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meissen_porcelain), who had found that kaolin was the key. On [15 January 1708](https://www.meissen.com/net/geschichte) their furnace turned out the first true hard-paste porcelain made outside Asia.

**Back.** Augustus registered the patent in 1709 and in 1710 moved the work behind the walls of [Albrechtsburg castle in Meissen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meissen_porcelain), where few workers were taught the whole recipe and each was sworn to keep the arcanum. China's centuries-old secret had been broken; now Saxony tried to lock its own. The lock held for less than a decade. A Vienna merchant won an imperial charter in 1718, hired away a Meissen arcanist, [Samuel Stöltzel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meissen_porcelain), and by 1719 a rival works was firing true porcelain in Vienna. From there the formula spread until [about thirty makers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meissen_porcelain) worked in Europe by 1760. A recipe is a thing you can write down or carry in your head, so a sworn oath could not pin it in place. The lead Meissen won by stealing China's secret it then lost the same way.

*Curator's note:* **Placement.** Manufacture, Goal Both, Fragility, Confidence Direct — a fired white body made at scale, and the lead sat on a recipe that walked out the door. **Analysis.** A clean Fragility card and the deck's sharpest secrecy story: the making-knowledge here is a written formula plus a body of ingredients, the kind of secret that travels, so it was stolen twice in two directions and no oath held it. Sits as a counter to the tacit-craft moats (casting, lithography) where the knowledge cannot be written down and stays put. **Nuance.** Tschirnhaus died in October 1708, the year of the first firing, and Böttger then carried the work forward and is usually named the founder, so the card credits both. Year 1708 marks the first true firing; the 1710 Albrechtsburg move marks the start of the guarded production. Meissen, Saxony is chosen over Vienna because that is where China's secret was first cracked (coordinates 51.16N, 13.47E). **Secrecy:** stolen (twice) — China's kaolin-and-petuntse recipe was reverse-engineered at Meissen, then Meissen's own arcanum was carried to Vienna by a fleeing arcanist, because a written, carryable recipe defeats any oath.
