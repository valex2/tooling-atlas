*Make · London, England · 1760s*
**John Harrison** · spring-driven sea clock for longitude

**Front.** In November 1761 the ship HMS Deptford [sailed from Portsmouth to Jamaica](https://www.rmg.co.uk/stories/time/harrisons-clocks-longitude-problem) carrying a small brass timekeeper, watched over by William Harrison, son of the man who built it. His father, [John Harrison](https://www.britannica.com/biography/John-Harrison), was a Yorkshire carpenter who had taught himself to make clocks, and for thirty years he had chased one thing: a sailor could find his east-west position if he knew the time back home, but no clock could keep that time at sea, where changing heat and the roll of the deck sent every one of them fast or slow. Harrison's fourth machine, called H4, was a spring-driven watch a little over five inches across, with a fast heavy balance that [beat five times a second](https://www.worldhistory.org/article/2197/harrisons-marine-chronometer/) so the ship's motion barely reached it. No timekeeper had ever held steady through months on the open ocean.

**Back.** When the Deptford reached Jamaica [81 days later](https://www.rmg.co.uk/stories/time/harrisons-clocks-longitude-problem), H4 had lost only about 5 seconds, close enough to place the ship within a mile or two. The Board of Longitude, which had put up a [£20,000 prize](https://www.worldhistory.org/article/2197/harrisons-marine-chronometer/) back in 1714, made Harrison repeat the trial and wait years, paying him near the end of his life. His single hand-built watch was too slow and costly to copy, so it fell to [Thomas Earnshaw](https://www.thenakedwatchmaker.com/blog/2021/9/14/thomas-earnshaw-was-an-english-watchmaker-who-simplified-the-process-of-making-marine-chronometers) and [John Arnold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Arnold_(watchmaker)), a generation on, to strip the design down to parts a workshop could turn out by the dozen. The sea clock then became standard issue on Royal Navy ships, and the London chronometer trade that grew up around it carried Britain's lead in precise timekeeping deep into the next century.
