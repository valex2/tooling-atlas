*Measure · Karlsruhe, Germany · 1880s*
**Heinrich Hertz** · spark-gap sender and ring catcher

**Front.** In the autumn of [1886](https://www.kit.edu/kit/english/pi_2011_8434.php), a 29-year-old physics professor in Karlsruhe named [Heinrich Hertz](https://nationalmaglab.org/magnet-academy/history-of-electricity-magnetism/pioneers/heinrich-hertz/) was trying out coils for a classroom demonstration when a spark jumping in one coil drew a faint answering spark from another across the bench, a hint of the invisible waves Maxwell's equations had promised twenty years before and no one had ever caught. So he built a catcher: an induction coil wired to a rod with a zinc sphere at each end and a cut in the middle where the spark snapped between two polished brass knobs, and across the room a plain [ring of copper wire](https://www.100objekte.kit.edu/en/object/022) whose ends a fine screw held a hair's width apart. In a darkened lecture hall the ring answered each snap with a spark of its own, [a fraction of a millimeter long](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00407-020-00260-1), so small he had to press his eye close in the dark to see it. Then he bounced the waves off a zinc sheet on the far wall and walked the ring down the hall; the little sparks swelled and died every few meters, and that spacing gave the wave's length, and the length gave its speed: the [speed of light](https://www.kit.edu/kit/english/pi_2011_8434.php).

**Back.** Hertz printed the whole recipe, drawings and measurements alike, and [died in 1894](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Heinrich-Hertz) at 36, before anyone had sent so much as a word with his waves. That same year a 20-year-old in Bologna named [Guglielmo Marconi](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Guglielmo-Marconi) read about them and began rebuilding the spark sender on his father's estate, stretching its reach a field at a time; on [12 December 1901](https://www.history.com/this-day-in-history/december-12/marconi-sends-first-atlantic-wireless-transmission) his spark carried the letter S across 2,000 miles of ocean to Newfoundland. The apparatus was public from the first paper and everyone copied it; the fortunes went to Marconi's company in London, and Karlsruhe kept the [lecture hall with Hertz's name on the door](https://www.kit.edu/kit/english/pi_2011_8434.php).
