*Model · Pisa, Italy · 1200s*
**Leonardo of Pisa (Fibonacci)** · place-value decimal notation with a zero

**Front.** As a boy [Leonardo of Pisa](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Fibonacci) was sent to join his father, a Pisan customs official posted to the busy port of [Bugia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liber_Abaci) on the North African coast. There the Arab merchants kept their accounts in a way no one back home used: ten little marks, with a value that changed depending on where it sat in the row. Back in Pisa in 1202 he wrote it all down in [Liber Abaci](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Liber-abaci). What he carried home was not a number but a way of writing them. "[The nine Indian figures are 9 8 7 6 5 4 3 2 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liber_Abaci)," he opened, "and with these nine figures, and with the sign 0 which the Arabs call zephir, any number whatsoever is written." A digit's worth now came from its slot in the line, and the [zero](https://www.britannica.com/science/zero-mathematics) held an empty slot open so the slots above it stayed in place. You could add and multiply on paper, in columns, instead of sliding beads on a board.

**Back.** The notation was old before Fibonacci touched it: the Indian astronomer [Brahmagupta had set down the rules for zero in 628](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brahmagupta), and Arab mathematicians carried it west over the next five centuries. Europe took its time. [Florence banned the figures from contracts in 1299](https://blog.nationalmuseum.ch/en/2020/03/the-mathematician-fibonacci-or-how-the-number-7-came-to-europe/), fearing a lone 0 could be doctored into a 6, and the old counting-board guilds fought to keep their boards. But the merchants ran the sums themselves, and the new way was simply faster, so the trading houses of Italy went over to it and the rest of Europe followed. Nobody owned the digits. Every later machine that runs on writing numbers down still rests on them: the slide rule, the ledger, the punch card, the computer.
