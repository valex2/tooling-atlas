*Model · Pisa, Italy · 1200s*
**Leonardo of Pisa (Fibonacci)** · place-value decimal notation with a zero

**Front.** As a boy [Leonardo of Pisa](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Fibonacci) was sent to join his father, a Pisan customs official posted to the busy port of [Bugia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liber_Abaci) on the North African coast. There the Arab merchants kept their accounts in a way no one back home used: ten little marks, with a value that changed depending on where it sat in the row. Back in Pisa in 1202 he wrote it all down in [Liber Abaci](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Liber-abaci), and the new thing was not a number but a way of writing them. "[The nine Indian figures are 9 8 7 6 5 4 3 2 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liber_Abaci)," he opened, "and with these nine figures, and with the sign 0 which the Arabs call zephir, any number whatsoever is written." A digit's worth now came from its slot in the line, and the [zero](https://www.britannica.com/science/zero-mathematics) held an empty slot open so the slots above it stayed in place. You could add and multiply on paper, in columns, instead of sliding beads on a board.

**Back.** The notation was old before Fibonacci touched it: the Indian astronomer [Brahmagupta had set down the rules for zero in 628](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brahmagupta), and Arab mathematicians carried it west over the next five centuries. Europe took its time. [Florence banned the figures from contracts in 1299](https://blog.nationalmuseum.ch/en/2020/03/the-mathematician-fibonacci-or-how-the-number-7-came-to-europe/), fearing a lone 0 could be doctored into a 6, and the old counting-board guilds fought to keep their boards. But the merchants ran the sums themselves, and the new way was simply faster, so the trading houses of Italy went over to it and the rest of Europe followed. Nobody owned the digits. They opened the way to every later machine that runs on writing numbers down: the slide rule, the ledger, the punch card, the computer.

Goal Both: it serves seeing-what-you-have (commerce, measurement) and reasoning, since it is the surface every later math tool is written on. Mechanism Convergence: digits, place value, and zero converge into one writing system for quantity. Confidence Direct on the dated facts (1202 Liber Abaci, the Bugia schooling, the opening line, Brahmagupta 628, the 1299 Florence ban). **Analysis.** A pure no-moat Model in the mold of [[Tooling Card - The Bit]]: a published notation is non-rival, so it confers no proprietary lead and instead becomes the ground floor everyone builds on. It is the oldest card in the deck and the root of [[Thread - Calculation]], sitting upstream of the slide rule (a sibling card in this batch, wired separately). Fibonacci is the carrier here, not the inventor, which is the honest framing: leadership in the notation never sat with him or with Pisa. Hero of its thread, parade elsewhere. **Nuance.** "Fibonacci introduced the numerals to Europe" is the popular line and roughly true at the level of the influential book, but the symbols had trickled in earlier (Gerbert of Aurillac, the 10th-century Codex Vigilanus) without catching on; Liber Abaci is the work that made them stick, so the card credits the book, not a first sighting. Geo is Pisa, where the book was written, though the learning happened in Bugia (Béjaïa, Algeria) and the math itself is Indian by way of the Arab world. A revised edition followed in 1228.
