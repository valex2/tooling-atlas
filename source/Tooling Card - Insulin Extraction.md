*Make · Toronto, Canada · 1920s*
**Frederick Banting & Charles Best** · islet hormone extracted from dogs

**Front.** In the summer of 1921, a young surgeon named [Frederick Banting](https://www.sciencehistory.org/education/scientific-biographies/frederick-banting-charles-best-james-collip-and-john-macleod/) and a fresh biochemistry graduate named Charles Best worked in a hot attic lab at the [University of Toronto](https://fisherdigitus.library.utoronto.ca/exhibits/show/insulin100/summerof1921). Doctors had known for thirty years that the pancreas held something that kept blood sugar down, but the gland's own digestive juices chewed that something to bits before anyone could collect it. So the two men [tied shut the ducts](https://www.jci.org/articles/view/142239) of living dogs and waited weeks for the outer gland to shrivel, then cut out the withered pancreas, ground it in cold salt water, and injected the liquid into a dog whose own pancreas they had removed. The dying dog's [blood sugar fell](https://www.umassmed.edu/dcoe/diabetes-education/patient-resources/banting-and-best-discover-insulin/) within hours.

**Back.** That winter the extract went into [Leonard Thompson](https://fisherdigitus.library.utoronto.ca/exhibits/show/insulin100/early-patients/leonard-thompson), a 14-year-old boy starving to death in a Toronto hospital; after a chemist named James Collip cleaned it up, the boy's blood sugar dropped to normal in a day. Toronto handed the patent to the university for [a dollar](https://www.acs.org/education/whatischemistry/landmarks/insulin.html), and Eli Lilly was mass-producing insulin within two years. Banting and John Macleod shared a [Nobel Prize in 1923](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/medicine/1923/summary/), and sixty years later the same hormone became the first drug grown in engineered bacteria, when [Humulin](https://www.acsh.org/news/2019/10/28/record-time-fda-approval-human-insulin-1982-when-genetic-engineering-came-age-14362) reached patients in 1982.
