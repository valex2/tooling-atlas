*Manufacture · East Hartford, USA · 1980s*
**Frank VerSnyder and the Pratt & Whitney foundry team** · growing a turbine blade as one crystal

**Front.** In September 1982 a Boeing 767 took off on Pratt & Whitney [JT9D-7R4 engines](https://www.asme.org/about-asme/engineering-history/landmarks/265-pratt-amp-whitney-single-crystal-turbine), the first airliner to fly on a new kind of turbine blade. The blade's problem was creep: hot, heavily loaded metal slowly stretches and tears along the seams where one crystal grain meets the next. The fix, worked out over two decades at the company's research lab by a team under [Frank VerSnyder](https://www.machinedesign.com/mechanical-motion-systems/article/21836518/single-crystal-turbine-blades-earn-asme-milestone-status), removed the seams: they grew the whole palm-sized blade as one continuous crystal. They poured molten nickel superalloy into a ceramic shell, started the freezing on a cold copper plate at the base, then drew the mold slowly up out of the heat through a tight corkscrew passage so narrow that only [one growing crystal could snake through](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Single_crystal) and fill the blade. What was new was a metal part with no grain boundaries at all, cast to shape.

**Back.** A single-crystal blade lasts [roughly nine times longer](https://www.machinedesign.com/mechanical-motion-systems/article/21836518/single-crystal-turbine-blades-earn-asme-milestone-status) under creep than ordinary cast metal, so the engine could run hotter and burn less fuel. The method spread to every Western engine maker and opened the way to the modern jet engine's hottest parts. The lead did not move. The casting is half a century of tacit foundry craft that no blueprint carries, so [Precision Castparts and Howmet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Howmet_Aerospace) still hold most of it, and China [needed about 35 years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xian_WS-9_Qinling) just to copy an older engine.
