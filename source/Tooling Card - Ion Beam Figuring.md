*Manufacture · Rochester, New York, USA · 1990s*
**Lynn N. Allen** · shave a mirror flat with an ion beam

**Front.** In 1991 an engineer at Eastman Kodak's optics shop in Rochester named Lynn Allen had a mirror on his hands that would not come right. It was a 1.8-metre hexagonal slab of Zerodur glass, [one of the 36 segments](https://www.spiedigitallibrary.org/conference-proceedings-of-spie/1531/1/Surface-error-correction-of-a-Keck-10-m-telescope-primary/10.1117/12.134862.short) that would make up the Keck telescope's ten-metre eye, and after polishing its shape was still off by 0.726 micrometres. More polishing was a bad bet: a pad presses on the glass, wears out as it goes, and rolls the edge down. So Allen and Robert Keim [put the segment in a vacuum chamber](http://spie.org/Publications/Proceedings/Paper/10.1117/12.962968) and pointed a beam of argon ions at it. The ions knocked atoms off the surface a few at a time, with nothing touching the glass, and because the beam always ate the same little dish-shaped hole they could map the error and then [compute how many seconds to hold the beam over every spot](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7235036/) to shave off exactly the high ground. Two passes took the segment from 0.726 micrometres of error down to [0.090](https://www.spiedigitallibrary.org/conference-proceedings-of-spie/1531/1/Surface-error-correction-of-a-Keck-10-m-telescope-primary/10.1117/12.134862.short). The last step of mirror-making had become arithmetic.

**Back.** [The idea had been printed in 1965](https://opg.optica.org/ao/abstract.cfm?uri=ao-4-12-1674) by three men at the University of Arizona and sat unused for a quarter century, because nobody could hold a beam steady enough or map an error finely enough to aim it. Once Kodak showed the loop working, fine-optics shops everywhere adopted it as the finishing step, and [beamline mirrors at Shanghai's synchrotron](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/physics/articles/10.3389/fphy.2022.865411/full) now come off it flat to about 1.2 nanometres. The shop that pushed it hardest was Zeiss in Oberkochen, which runs ion beam figuring together with computer-controlled polishing and a magnetic slurry to finish the mirrors inside every [EUV lithography machine](https://www.electrooptics.com/feature/mirror-semicon), grinding out the loop until, by the company's own measure, [a mirror blown up to the size of Germany would have its tallest bump standing 0.1 millimetres high](https://www.zeiss.com/semiconductor-manufacturing-technology/inspiring-technology/euv-lithography.html). Kodak's Rochester optics business was sold off and broken up. Zeiss has been finishing EUV mirrors for [thirty years](https://www.zeiss.com/semiconductor-manufacturing-technology/news-and-events/smt-press-releases/2025/euv-30-years.html), and no chip machine ships without a set of them.
