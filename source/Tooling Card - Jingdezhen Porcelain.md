*Manufacture · Jingdezhen, China · 1300s*
**Jingdezhen kiln towns** · a town-sized porcelain production line

**Front.** In [1351](https://artsandculture.google.com/asset/the-david-vases/dgEjTbxKC2D5mA), a man named Zhang Wenjin ordered a pair of tall vases from the kiln town of Jingdezhen as a gift for his temple, with a prayer for his family's peace written on their necks. No single potter would make them, because in Jingdezhen no single potter made anything whole. One shop mixed white kaolin clay with crushed porcelain stone; the next threw the shape; another trimmed it dry; a painter laid on dragons in [cobalt carried from Persia](https://www.koh-antique.com/yuan%20blue%20and%20white/yuan%20sumali.html); the kiln crews fired the load. The town had cut one craft into dozens of small steps, each done all day by one worker, filling whole [streets of workshops](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-06273-x) that did nothing but paint, and others that did nothing but fire.

**Back.** Zhang's vases sit in the [British Museum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Vases) today, carrying the earliest date known on blue-and-white ware, and the town that made them shipped its wares west for three more centuries: the Dutch East India Company alone carried [over three million pieces](https://www.essentialvermeer.com/dutch-painters/netherlands/delftware-01.html) to Holland between 1604 and 1657, while no potter in Europe could fire true porcelain at all. When a French priest finally walked the town's lanes and wrote home in [1712](http://ceramicstoday.glazy.org/articles/entrecolles_part1.html) that one fired piece "passed through the hands of seventy workmen," Saxony had just fired [Europe's first true porcelain](https://www.meissen.com/net/geschichte) at Meissen, 357 years after Zhang's vases left the kiln. Jingdezhen held the export trade for most of another century before Europe's own factories took it away.
