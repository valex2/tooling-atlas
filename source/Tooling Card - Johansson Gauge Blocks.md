*Measure · Eskilstuna, Sweden · 1890s*
**Carl Edvard Johansson** · steel blocks wrung to any length

**Front.** In 1896, [Carl Edvard Johansson](https://www.tekniskamuseet.se/en/learn-more/swedish-inventors/carl-edvard-johansson/), a gauge inspector at the state rifle factory in Eskilstuna, Sweden, rode the train home from the [Mauser works](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauge_block) in Germany, where his factory was getting ready to build the new army rifle under license. He had gone to study Mauser's measuring and found several thousand checking gauges, one for each dimension of each part, every one needing remaking whenever a design changed. On the train he worked out that [102 blocks](https://www.mitutoyo.com/webfoo/wp-content/uploads/E12016-History-of-The-Gage-Block.pdf) of hardened steel, in the right sizes, could stack into about 20,000 lengths, everything from 1 to 201 millimetres in steps of a hundredth; he rough-cut the blocks at the factory and lapped them flat at home, on a grinding wheel rigged to his wife Margareta's [sewing machine](https://www.lindahall.org/about/news/scientist-of-the-day/carl-edvard-johansson/). The lapping got so fine that two blocks slid face to face would grip as if glued, so a wrung stack measured like one solid bar, a length standard any machinist could hold.

**Back.** A factory with one flat box could now check every gauge it owned against the same millimetre, and by 1912 the weights bureau in Paris had measured his master blocks true to a [ten-thousandth of a millimetre](https://www.mitutoyo.com/webfoo/wp-content/uploads/E12016-History-of-The-Gage-Block.pdf). In the first world war [more than 450 companies](https://www.mitutoyo.com/webfoo/wp-content/uploads/E12016-History-of-The-Gage-Block.pdf) on both sides used his blocks, and when the war cut America off from Sweden, the National Bureau of Standards paid [William Hoke](https://emtoolbox.nist.gov/Publications/NISTMonograph180.pdf) to copy them. [Henry Ford](https://www.lindahall.org/about/news/scientist-of-the-day/carl-edvard-johansson/) bought Johansson's failing American company in 1923 and moved the inventor to Dearborn; by 1929 [a million blocks](https://www.mitutoyo.com/webfoo/wp-content/uploads/E12016-History-of-The-Gage-Block.pdf) had been made, and Japan's first maker, [Tsugami](https://www.mitutoyo.com/webfoo/wp-content/uploads/E12016-History-of-The-Gage-Block.pdf), proved his own blocks in the 1930s by checking them against a borrowed Johansson set.
