*Manufacture · Cheongju, Goryeo Korea · 1370s*
**Goryeo type-founders** · reusable letters cast in bronze

**Front.** In the summer of [1377](https://www.guinnessworldrecords.com/world-records/689333-oldest-book-printed-using-movable-metal-type), at a temple called Heungdeok-sa in the town of Cheongju, monks set out to print a book of Zen [teachings their late teacher Baegun had gathered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jikji). Cutting a whole page into one wood block was slow, the block wore out, and it could only ever print that same page. So the temple's founders cast each character on its own in bronze, [poured into a mold pressed from fine sand](https://artsandculture.google.com/story/the-invention-of-movable-type-goryeo-technology-and-wisdom-cheongju-early-printing-museum/zgXxwilonG6kIg?hl=en), then set the loose metal pieces into a frame to ink and print. Each little bronze letter was hard, and could be pulled back out and set again for the next book.

**Back.** The book they made, the [Jikji](https://www.bnf.fr/en/jikji-treasure-world-printing), still survives, and it is the [oldest book in the world](https://www.guinnessworldrecords.com/world-records/689333-oldest-book-printed-using-movable-metal-type) printed with metal type, [73 years](https://www.bnf.fr/en/jikji-treasure-world-printing) before [Gutenberg](https://www.history.com/articles/printing-press) cast his own letters in Mainz. But the lead did not follow the tool. Korea's writing needed thousands of different characters, the type foundries were run by the court for small royal print runs, and cheap books for everyone never came of it. Gutenberg's Germany had an alphabet of two dozen letters, so the same idea of reusable cast type set off a flood of books there, and the printing lead settled in Europe.
