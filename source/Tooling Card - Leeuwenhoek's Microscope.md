*Measure · Delft, Netherlands · 1670s*
**Antonie van Leeuwenhoek** · a single tiny glass-bead lens

**Front.** One day in 1676, a cloth merchant in Delft named [Antoni van Leeuwenhoek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antonie_van_Leeuwenhoek) left some pepper soaking in water, trying to find out why pepper tastes sharp. After the infusion had sat for about three weeks and turned cloudy, he held a drop up to one of the tiny glass beads he had [taught himself to grind](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8121416/) (each one no bigger than a pinhead, yet able to magnify as much as [266 times](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antonie_van_Leeuwenhoek), far past the bulky microscopes his rivals built), and saw the water was alive with thousands of little creatures swimming about. No one had seen them before. [Hooke had already printed how to draw glass into tiny bead lenses](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8121416/), and a year later, after another Royal Society man tried and failed, Hooke too managed to see the little creatures. Yet no one could match Leeuwenhoek's own lenses for a hundred and fifty years after he died.

**Back.** Those little creatures turned out, long after, to be the cause of many of the world's [deadliest diseases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germ_theory_of_disease), but no one knew that yet and no tool could reach them. Only in the 1800s, when other people built sharp lenses over again from scratch, could [Pasteur and Koch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germ_theory_of_disease) prove that these tiny things make us sick, opening the way to clean water, vaccines, and much of modern medicine. The way to make the bead had been printed all along; what could not be printed was the skill in his hands, so the lead it gave waited two hundred years for someone to grow it back.
