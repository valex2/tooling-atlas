*Make · Vancouver, Canada · 2010s*
**Pieter Cullis and colleagues** · fat bubble that smuggles RNA into cells

**Front.** From the late 1970s **Pieter Cullis** at the University of British Columbia studied the fats that make up the skin around our cells, and asked whether he could build tiny bubbles of that same fat to carry drugs. The hard part with RNA was simple and brutal: naked RNA is torn apart in the blood in minutes and never reaches a cell. The fix his group landed on was an [ionizable lipid](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8454296/) — a fat that has no charge in the blood, so the bubble is calm and slips past the body's defenses, but turns positive in the acid pocket inside a cell, where it grabs the cell's own wall and rips a hole so the RNA spills in. By 2010 his circle had tuned one such fat, [DLin-MC3-DMA](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8502116/), into a bubble about a hundred nanometres across that packed RNA, hid it, and dropped it inside on cue.

**Back.** That bubble carried the first RNA-interference drug, Alnylam's [patisiran](https://investors.alnylam.com/press-release?id=22946), to FDA approval in 2018. Two years later the same fat-bubble recipe, tuned for messenger RNA by the Cullis spin-out [Acuitas](https://www.med.ubc.ca/news/dr-pieter-cullis-awarded-2022-tang-prize-for-contributions-to-covid-19-mrna-vaccines/), became the envelope for the Pfizer-BioNTech and Moderna COVID vaccines, and Cullis took a share of the 2022 Tang Prize for it. Who owns the recipe is its own war. Vancouver's [Arbutus](https://www.genevant.com/genevant-sciences-and-arbutus-biopharma-initiate-international-patent-infringement-enforcement-actions-against-moderna/) and its partner Genevant sued Moderna for using their lipid-nanoparticle patents, and days before a Delaware jury trial Moderna [settled for $2.25 billion](https://ipwatchdog.com/2026/03/04/moderna-settles-genevant-arbutus-ending-lnp-patent-dispute/) in March 2026. You can write the mRNA message on a laptop in a day; the envelope took the Cullis labs, Arbutus, and Acuitas more than twenty years of lipid chemistry, and a quarter-billion-dollar check moved to Vancouver to settle who owned it.
