*Make · London, UK · 1830s*
**Joseph Jackson Lister** · spaced doublets that cancel blur

**Front.** In 1830, a London wine merchant named [Joseph Jackson Lister](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Joseph-Jackson-Lister) spent his evenings grinding and testing lenses after the shop closed. Every strong microscope he owned smeared its picture with colour and haze, so the compound microscope was still more toy than tool. Lister worked out that a single glued pair of crown and flint glass has [two exact spots](https://www.encyclopedia.com/science/dictionaries-thesauruses-pictures-and-press-releases/lister-joseph-jackson) where it bends light without adding blur, and that if he set a second such pair a measured gap behind the first, so the light left one clean spot and met the other, the errors of one lens undid the errors of the other. He [wrote it up for the Royal Society](https://www.jstor.org/stable/110225) that January: for the first time a high-power lens gave a sharp picture with no false colour.

**Back.** Instrument makers copied the spacing, and within a few years sharp achromatic objectives were sold across Europe. On them Theodor Schwann and Matthias Schleiden could at last [see cells clearly](https://hekint.org/2022/07/15/the-beginnings-of-cell-theory-schleiden-schwann-and-virchow/), and by 1839 they argued that every living thing is built from cells; decades later [Robert Koch](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Robert-Koch) used the same kind of lens to pick out the bacteria behind anthrax and tuberculosis. The lead compounded and then travelled: [Ernst Abbe](https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-94-009-1784-2_2) put the mathematics under Lister's rule at the Carl Zeiss works in Jena, and the making of fine microscopes settled in Germany for the rest of the century.
