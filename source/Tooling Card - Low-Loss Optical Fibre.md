*Model · Harlow, UK · 1960s*
**Charles Kao** · a purity target for glass fibre

**Front.** In 1966, an engineer at a telephone lab in Harlow, England, named [Charles Kao](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/physics/2009/kao/facts/) was trying to send phone calls down hair-thin fibres of glass as flashes of light. Every glass then made ate the light: after [twenty metres](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/physics/2009/press-release/) only one part in a hundred got through, and a phone line needs kilometres, so most labs had written glass off. Kao gathered the clearest glass samples he could find, measured how much each one dimmed a beam, and traced the dimming to specks of iron and other dirt in the melt, not to the glass itself. With [George Hockham](https://digital-library.theiet.org/content/journals/10.1049/piee.1966.0189) he published the answer as a number and a shape: make glass pure enough to lose under [20 decibels a kilometre](https://spectrum.ieee.org/how-charles-kao-beat-bell-labs-to-the-fiberoptic-revolution), wrap a thin core in a coat of slightly different glass, and light would go the distance.

**Back.** No glass on earth met the number in 1966, and [Bell Labs](https://spectrum.ieee.org/how-charles-kao-beat-bell-labs-to-the-fiberoptic-revolution) kept its money on hollow metal pipes instead. Four years later three researchers at [Corning](https://www.corning.com/worldwide/en/markets/Optical-Communications-Market/streamlined-connectivity/timeline-1970.html) in upstate New York melted a glass so pure that a 29-metre test fibre lost just [17 decibels a kilometre](https://ethw.org/Milestones:World%27s_First_Low-Loss_Optical_Fiber_for_Telecommunications,_1970), and by 1979 Japanese labs had pushed the loss down to [0.2](https://www.optica-opn.org/home/articles/volume_31/march_2020/features/the_breakthrough_birth_of_low-loss_fiber_optics/). The target was a printed page anyone could read, so the prize for hitting it went to the glassmakers; Kao's own share arrived in 2009, as [half a Nobel Prize](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/physics/2009/summary/), forty-three years after he named the number.
