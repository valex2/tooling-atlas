*Make · St John's, Canada · 1900s*
**Guglielmo Marconi** · long-wave wireless across the Atlantic

**Front.** In December 1901, [Guglielmo Marconi](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/physics/1909/marconi/biographical/) was camped in an old fever hospital on Signal Hill, above the harbour of St John's, Newfoundland, with a receiver on a table and a gale tearing his balloons off their ropes. The learned view held that radio waves fly straight, so the curve of the ocean between him and England should block them; his own best signals had gone a couple of hundred miles. On [12 December](https://ethw.org/Milestones:Reception_of_Transatlantic_Radio_Signals,_1901) he flew a kite trailing 500 feet of wire, joined it to the receiver, and pressed a telephone earpiece to his ear while his men at [Poldhu, Cornwall](https://marconi-centre-poldhu.org.uk/about/history/) fired a spark transmitter that sent the letter S (three short clicks) over and over. Just after half past twelve he heard the clicks: his big spark station and a wire in the sky had carried a signal [more than 2,000 miles](https://www.history.com/this-day-in-history/december-12/marconi-sends-first-atlantic-wireless-transmission), far past the horizon that was supposed to stop it.

**Back.** Doubters said he had heard nothing but the crackle of lightning, since the signal was too weak to move a recorder and the only other witness was his assistant [George Kemp](https://ethw.org/Milestones:Reception_of_Transatlantic_Radio_Signals,_1901); some historians [still say so](https://antiquewireless.org/wp-content/uploads/46-did_marconi_receive_transatlantic_radio_signals_in_1901.pdf). Two months later, aboard the liner Philadelphia, Poldhu's signals [printed themselves on paper tape](https://earlyradiohistory.us/1902phil.htm) 1,551 miles out with the ship's captain signing as witness, and from [October 1907](https://www.visitgalway.ie/explore/heritage-and-history/historic-buildings-and-places/the-marconi-station/) paid messages ran daily between Clifden in Ireland and Glace Bay in Nova Scotia. The cable company that owned Newfoundland's telegraph monopoly had chased him off the island within days, but the lead compounded anyway: when the Titanic went down in 1912, the calls that brought a rescue ship for [700 survivors](https://www.sciencemuseum.org.uk/objects-and-stories/titanic-marconi-and-wireless-telegraph) were sent and answered by operators the Marconi Company had trained and hired out.
