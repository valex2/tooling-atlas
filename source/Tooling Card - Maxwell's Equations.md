*Model · London, England, UK · 1860s*
**James Clerk Maxwell** · field equations that predict the wave

**Front.** In 1865 James Clerk Maxwell, then holding the physics chair at [King's College London](https://www.kcl.ac.uk/people/james-clerk-maxwell), had a working set of laws for electricity and magnetism but no way to say what they did over distance. Michael Faraday had shown a changing magnet makes a current, yet the equations stopped at the wire and said nothing about the empty space around it. Maxwell added one term, the [displacement current](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_Maxwell%27s_equations), which let a changing electric field act like a real current even in a vacuum, and with it the equations folded into a wave that carries itself through empty space. He then computed how fast that wave would travel from bench numbers for electricity and magnetism, and it came out at the [measured speed of light](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rsta.2014.0473), so the paper claimed light itself was one of these waves and that others could be made.

**Back.** The prediction sat on paper for [22 years](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC4360095/) with no device to show a wave existed. [Heinrich Hertz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heinrich_Hertz) in Karlsruhe finally made and caught one in 1887 with a spark gap and a loop of wire, and within fifteen years Guglielmo Marconi was pushing the same waves across the Atlantic. Maxwell's four equations were printed for anyone to read, so they gave Britain prestige and no monopoly. The money went to whoever could build the transmitters, the tuned antennas, and later the vacuum tubes that amplified the signal.
