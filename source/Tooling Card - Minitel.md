*Make · France · 1980s*
**French PTT / DGT** · free home online terminal network

**Front.** In 1982, France's state phone agency had spent a decade wiring millions of new telephone lines, and the paper directory listing them was [heavy, costly, and stale the day it was printed](https://www.universalis.fr/encyclopedie/minitel-et-annuaire-electronique/). [Gérard Théry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G%C3%A9rard_Th%C3%A9ry), the engineer who had run the network through the 1970s, proposed to stop printing the book and put the directory on a screen in every home. So the agency had Alcatel and Matra build a small box, [a screen with a keyboard that folds up over its face](https://collection.sciencemuseumgroup.org.uk/objects/co447955/alcatel-minitel-communication-terminal-france-1983-personal-computer), sending keystrokes up the phone line at [75 bits a second and pulling pages down at 1,200](https://hackaday.com/2022/02/16/teardown-alcatel-telic-1-minitel-terminal/), and began [handing it out free](https://spectrum.ieee.org/minitel-the-online-world-france-built-before-the-web) at the post office. A family plugged the box into the wall and dialed; the giveaway did the work, not the electronics.

**Back.** The free box became a marketplace: dial 3615 and a code, and you could book a train seat or flirt in a chat room, with the charge [collected on the phone bill](https://spectrum.ieee.org/minitel-the-online-world-france-built-before-the-web) and most of it passed to whoever ran the service. Small firms built [26,000 services by 1996](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minitel), and by 1999 nine million boxes served 25 million people, the biggest online public in the world while the Web was still small. But the box never learned new tricks; the Web passed it, the lead never left France, and when France Télécom [shut the network on 30 June 2012](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minitel), 810,000 boxes were still switched on.
