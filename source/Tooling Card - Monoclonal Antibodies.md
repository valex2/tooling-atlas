*Make · Cambridge, Britain · 1970s*
**Georges Köhler and César Milstein** · one exact antibody, made forever

**Front.** An antibody is a protein the immune system shapes to grab one specific target, which makes it a perfect tool, if only you could get a steady supply of a single kind. The trouble was that the body makes a swarm of different antibodies at once, and the cells that make them die within days in a dish. In [1975, at the MRC laboratory in Cambridge, Georges Köhler and César Milstein](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/medicine/1984/press-release/) found a trick: [fuse an antibody-making immune cell with an immortal cancer cell](https://resources.biomol.com/biomol-blog/invention-of-the-hybridoma-technology), a myeloma. The hybrid, a hybridoma, did both jobs of its parents at once: it [poured out one single, chosen antibody and it divided without end](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/medicine/1984/press-release/). The new thing was a living factory for one exact antibody, in any amount you liked.

**Back.** Monoclonal antibodies became one of medicine's largest tools. They are the [test strip in every pregnancy and rapid COVID test](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-025-02452-7), and they are a whole class of blockbuster drugs, the cancer and autoimmune treatments and the checkpoint inhibitors, a large share of the best-selling medicines in the world. Köhler and Milstein took the [Nobel Prize in 1984](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/medicine/1984/press-release/), and, famously, never patented the method, so the whole field grew on it freely.
