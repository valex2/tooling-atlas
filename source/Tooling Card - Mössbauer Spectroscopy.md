*Measure · Heidelberg, Germany · 1950s*
**Rudolf Mössbauer** · gamma rays fired with no recoil

**Front.** In 1958, a 29-year-old student named [Rudolf Mössbauer](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/physics/1961/mossbauer/biographical/) was finishing his thesis at a lab in [Heidelberg](https://edu.rsc.org/feature/the-beginnings-of-m%C3%B6ssbauer-spectroscopy/2020243.article), shooting gamma rays from one lump of [iridium](https://scihi.org/rudolf-mossbauer/) at another to watch the rays get soaked up. He thought that chilling the metal would blur the match and make fewer rays stick. So he cooled it with liquid nitrogen and, to tune the rays, spun the source on a small turntable he had [geared with toothed wheels from a child's construction set](https://edu.rsc.org/feature/the-beginnings-of-m%C3%B6ssbauer-spectroscopy/2020243.article). Chilling did the reverse of what he expected: more rays stuck, not fewer, because atoms held tight in the frozen crystal were firing their gamma rays with no kick back, so the rays came out at one exact pitch.

**Back.** A gamma ray with no kick has an almost perfect pitch, so fine that the tiniest nudge to its energy throws it out of tune, down to about [one part in a trillion](https://www.britannica.com/science/Mossbauer-effect). Word spread fast: within a week [260 people wrote in](https://edu.rsc.org/feature/the-beginnings-of-m%C3%B6ssbauer-spectroscopy/2020243.article) for copies of the short paper, and the [Nobel Prize](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/physics/1961/mossbauer/facts/) came in 1961, when he was 32. Barely a year after the paper, [two physicists at Harvard](https://www.nuclear-power.com/pound-rebka-experiment/) used the sharp pitch to catch the pull of gravity itself, measuring how a gamma ray reddened as it climbed a 22-metre tower, and the method has read the iron in steel and in rust ever since.
