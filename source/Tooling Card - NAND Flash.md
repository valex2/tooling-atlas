*Make · Tokyo, Japan · 1980s*
**Fujio Masuoka (Toshiba)** · memory cells strung in chains

**Front.** In 1987, an engineer at Toshiba named [Fujio Masuoka](https://global.honda/en/newsroom/news/2018/c180928beng.html) was still pushing a chip his bosses had left on the shelf: three years earlier he had [shown](https://computerhistory.org/blog/pioneers-of-semiconductor-non-volatile-memory-nvm-the-first-four-decades/) a memory that kept its data with the power off, and [Intel](https://spectrum.ieee.org/chip-hall-of-fame-toshiba-nand-flash-memory), not Toshiba, was preparing to sell it. His memory still cost too much per bit to chase the spinning hard disk, because every cell needed its own metal contact, and the contacts ate almost as much of the chip as the cells did. So he strung the cells in chains, transistors wired end to end like beads, one shared contact for each chain, and in [December 1987](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/1487443/) his team presented a cell of [6.43 square micrometers](https://rust-class.org/static/classes/class17/nandflash.pdf), 30 percent smaller per bit than a conventional chip drawn with the same-width lines. The chain made the chip slower, since a read had to pass through every other bead in the chain, but it bought room; the invention was the wiring plan, not a new transistor.

**Back.** Toshiba took four more years to [bring a chip to market](https://www.eweek.com/storage/1987-toshiba-launches-nand-flash/), and in late 1992, wanting allies against Intel's rival flash, it [licensed the design to Samsung](https://www.diskmfr.com/the-previous-and-present-life-of-nand-flash-after-35-years/). The chain cell went first into digital cameras, then into every phone and solid-state drive; by 2019 flash sold more than [$50 billion a year](https://computerhistory.org/blog/pioneers-of-semiconductor-non-volatile-memory-nvm-the-first-four-decades/), and Samsung, the licensee, held the largest share, having passed Toshiba in 2002. Masuoka's bonus for the invention was [a few hundred dollars](https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/news/historical-engineers-dr-fujio-masuoka-the-unsung-father-of-flash/); he sued and [settled in 2006 for $758,000](https://www.computerhistory.org/revolution/memory-storage/8/256), and in 2018 Toshiba [sold the memory business itself](https://www.baincapital.com/technology/case-studies/kioxia.html) for $18 billion.
