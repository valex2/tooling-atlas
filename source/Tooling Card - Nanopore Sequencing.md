*Measure · Oxford, UK · 2010s*
**Oxford Nanopore (Hagan Bayley)** · single-molecule pore reader

**Front.** In 2014, a company grown from [Hagan Bayley's chemistry lab](https://www.chem.ox.ac.uk/people/professor-hagan-bayley) at Oxford began shipping a [DNA reader the size of a thumb drive](https://genomebiology.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s13059-016-1103-0). The bench-sized machines that read DNA then could only spell out short snippets, about [150 letters at a time](https://www.nature.com/articles/nbt.4060). Oxford Nanopore set a [doughnut-shaped protein](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20704324/) from a bacterium into a thin film, pulled a current of salt ions through its hole, and threaded a single strand of DNA through the opening, where each letter of the strand pinched the hole a little differently and dipped the current. Reading the letter straight off that dip meant one molecule was enough, with no copying and no dye, and the reads ran tens of thousands of letters long.

**Back.** In April 2015, two workers packed one of these readers and its kit into [less than 50 kg of airline luggage](https://www.nature.com/articles/nature16996) and flew it to Guinea, where an Ebola outbreak was still spreading. In a field lab they sequenced the virus from a fresh sample and had an answer in [under 24 hours](https://nanoporetech.com/resource-centre/real-time-portable-genome-sequencing-ebola-surveillance), fast enough to track how the outbreak moved from town to town. The bench-sized machines never left the lab; the thumb-drive reader went on to sequence Zika in Brazil and later spelled out stretches of a human genome in single reads over [two million letters long](https://www.nature.com/articles/nbt.4060).
