*Model · Edinburgh, Scotland · 1610s*
**John Napier** · printed tables of logarithms

**Front.** In 1614 a Scottish laird named [John Napier](https://www.britannica.com/biography/John-Napier) printed a thin book of [tables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mirifici_Logarithmorum_Canonis_Descriptio) at Merchiston, near Edinburgh. He had watched astronomers and sailors lose whole days multiplying long strings of digits by hand, where one slip spoiled the answer. For about [twenty years](https://old.maa.org/press/periodicals/convergence/logarithms-the-early-history-of-a-familiar-function-john-napier-introduces-logarithms) he had worked out a matching number for every number, one that grows by adding while the first grows by multiplying, so that to multiply two big numbers you look up their matches, add those, and read the answer back. He called the match a logarithm, and he printed a full table of them so anyone could just look it up.

**Back.** Within three years [Henry Briggs](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Henry-Briggs) walked up from London and talked Napier into re-basing the tables on ten, which made them far easier to read, and he printed the base-ten version that every later table copied. Astronomers grabbed it at once; Kepler leaned on logarithms to grind through his planet math. Over in Switzerland, Joost Bürgi had worked out much the same idea [on his own](https://handwiki.org/wiki/History_of_logarithms) years earlier but sat on it until 1620, so the credit and the copies went to the man who printed first. The tables stayed the way people did hard sums for the next three hundred years, and a few years on someone carved a logarithm scale onto two sliding sticks so the lengths added themselves, the [slide rule](https://technologyuk.net/computing/history-of-computing/slide-rule.shtml).
