*Manufacture · Tokyo, Japan · 1980s*
**Nippon Kogaku (Nikon)** · chip printer with home-ground lens

**Front.** In 1976, Japan's [government chip program](https://www.asianometry.com/p/how-japan-won-the-lithography-industry) handed an odd job to [Nippon Kogaku](https://www.nikon.com/company/corporate/history/), the Tokyo firm that made Nikon cameras: build the machine that prints circuit patterns onto silicon, so Japan's chipmakers would not stand in line for the American one. The firm had melted its own optical glass since the 1920s, and its engineers ground a projection lens that could draw lines [one micrometer wide](https://www.lithoguru.com/scientist/litho_history/Kato_Litho_History.pdf), sharper than the lens in the American machine. They set it over a stage that slid the wafer along in small steps, printing the circuit one thumbnail-sized square at a time, and in [February 1980](https://www.nikon.com/company/corporate/history/) put the machine on sale as the NSR-1010G. [GCA](https://lithoguru.com/scientist/litho_history/milestones_tools.pdf), the Massachusetts firm that had built the first stepper, bought every lens from Zeiss across an ocean; Nikon's was ground in its own shops.

**Back.** [NEC and Toshiba bought the first machines](https://lithoguru.com/scientist/litho_history/milestones_tools.pdf), and in [1982 Nikon opened a base in Silicon Valley](https://www.lithoguru.com/scientist/litho_history/Kato_Litho_History.pdf) and began pulling away GCA's customers, IBM and Texas Instruments among the first. GCA waited on Zeiss for every lens and [could not get them fast enough](https://www.lithoguru.com/scientist/litho_history/Kato_Litho_History.pdf); Nikon [matched GCA's sales in 1984, passed it in 1985](https://lithoguru.com/scientist/litho_history/milestones_tools.pdf), and by [1990 stood first in the world](https://www.rieti.go.jp/jp/publications/dp/03e003.pdf). GCA had held [90 percent of the stepper market](https://www.rieti.go.jp/jp/publications/dp/03e003.pdf) when the NSR-1010G appeared; it lost [$100 million in two years](https://lithoguru.com/scientist/litho_history/milestones_tools.pdf) and shut its doors in 1993 when no buyer could be found.
