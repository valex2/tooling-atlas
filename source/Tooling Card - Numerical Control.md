*Make · Michigan and MIT, USA · 1940s-50s*
**John Parsons, MIT Servo Lab** · numerically controlled milling machine

**Front.** In 1949 [John Parsons](https://www.invent.org/inductees/john-t-parsons), who made helicopter rotor blades in Michigan, was tired of men hand-cutting curved blade templates point by slow point. His idea: feed the curve to a milling machine as a list of numbers on punched cards, and let the machine move its own cutter. The US Air Force liked it and paid [MIT's Servomechanisms Laboratory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_numerical_control) to build a real one. In September 1952 the lab showed it off: a borrowed [Cincinnati Hydrotel mill](http://museum.mit.edu/150/86) reading punched tape, the cutter guided by numbers instead of a man's hands.

**Back.** It worked, but the brain that ran it was [five refrigerator-sized cabinets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_numerical_control) of 250 vacuum tubes and 175 relays, and the bill came to [$360,000](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_numerical_control). So America built numbers-run machines only big, costly, and one-off, for aircraft and weapons, and never made a small cheap one a shop could buy. By 1968, after sixteen years, [only about a tenth](https://www.construction-physics.com/p/what-happened-to-the-us-machine-tool) of US metal shops owned even one. When the cheap version finally came in the 1970s, it came from [Japan and FANUC](https://www.fundinguniverse.com/company-histories/fanuc-ltd-history/), not from the country that invented it. The lead died on the table.
