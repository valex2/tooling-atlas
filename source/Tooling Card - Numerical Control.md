*Make · Michigan and MIT, USA · 1940s-50s*
**John Parsons, MIT Servo Lab** · numerically controlled milling machine

**Front.** In 1949 [John Parsons](https://www.invent.org/inductees/john-t-parsons), who made helicopter rotor blades in Michigan, was tired of men hand-cutting curved blade templates point by slow point. His idea: feed the curve to a milling machine as a list of numbers on punched cards, and let the machine move its own cutter. The US Air Force liked it and paid [MIT's Servomechanisms Laboratory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_numerical_control) to build a real one. In September 1952 the lab showed it off: a borrowed [Cincinnati Hydrotel mill](http://museum.mit.edu/150/86) reading punched tape, the cutter guided by numbers instead of a man's hands.

**Back.** It worked, but the brain that ran it was [five refrigerator-sized cabinets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_numerical_control) of 250 vacuum tubes and 175 relays, and the bill came to [$360,000](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_numerical_control). So America built numbers-run machines only big, costly, and one-off, for aircraft and weapons, and never made a small cheap one a shop could buy. By 1968, after sixteen years, [only about a tenth](https://www.construction-physics.com/p/what-happened-to-the-us-machine-tool) of US metal shops owned even one. When the cheap version finally came in the 1970s, it came from [Japan and FANUC](https://www.fundinguniverse.com/company-histories/fanuc-ltd-history/), not from the country that invented it. The lead died on the table.

*Curator's note:* (1) *Placement.* Goal 2 (the invention is not enough; it has to be driven into cheap manufacture), mechanism Convergence-by-omission. The cell: Make · Michigan & MIT · 1949–1952. Confidence in the back is **Direct** — the dates ($360,000 bill, Sept 1952 demo, July 1949 Air Force contract, ~10% of shops by 1968) are all corroborated across Wikipedia's NC history, the MIT 150 exhibition, and Construction Physics. One caveat: the exact year Parsons *first conceived* the idea is fuzzy (his by-the-numbers method dates to 1946–48; the funded contract is firmly July 1949). I dated the front to 1949 to anchor on the verifiable contract, not the murky spark.

(2) *Analysis.* This is the deck's clearest **invented-it-and-lost-it** card, and it is built to pair directly against "FANUC Makes the Brain Cheap." America is the hero of the front and the parade's no-show in the back: it holds the tool first and never industrializes it. Where the van Leeuwenhoek card shows a lead that *waited* two centuries and was then grown back by the same civilization, this shows a lead that was simply handed away — the tool existed, worked, and was left expensive on purpose. The contrast the deck wants is delay-then-revival vs. invention-then-abandonment.

(3) *Nuance.* The MIT machine froze Parsons himself out: MIT bought its own surplus Hydrotel in 1950 and contracted the Air Force directly, sidelining the inventor — a sub-story I left off the face because the card's point is national, not personal. The "refrigerator-sized cabinets" and tube/relay counts come from the Wikipedia NC history citing the original Air Force report; the $360,000 figure is from the same report (≈$2.6M in 2005 dollars). The 1968 ~10%-of-plants figure traces to Construction Physics summarizing NBER/census data. All load-bearing numbers cross-checked against a second source.
