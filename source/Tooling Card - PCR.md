*Make · California, USA · 1980s*
**Kary Mullis** · a photocopier for DNA

**Front.** One night in the spring of 1983, [Kary Mullis](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Kary-Mullis) was driving up [Highway 128](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kary_Mullis) toward his cabin in the Mendocino hills, his girlfriend asleep beside him. He worked at a company called Cetus, fiddling with tiny bits of DNA, the long string that spells out how a body is built. He wanted a way to pick one short stretch and make lots of it, because a single copy is far too little to see or test. The trick came to him on the road. You heat the DNA so its two paired strands let go of each other. Cool it, and a [tiny copying machine](https://www.genome.gov/about-genomics/fact-sheets/Polymerase-Chain-Reaction-Fact-Sheet) builds a fresh partner for each loose strand. Two where you had one. Heat, cool, repeat: each round doubles what you hold. After [thirty rounds](https://www.genome.gov/about-genomics/fact-sheets/Polymerase-Chain-Reaction-Fact-Sheet) one piece becomes [more than a billion](https://www.biology-pages.info/P/PCR.html). What was new was not the DNA. It was the machine that copies it on demand.

**Back.** At first the copying was a chore. The heating step killed the copier each round and you had to add more by hand. The fix came from a [bug that lives in a Yellowstone hot spring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taq_polymerase), happy in water near boiling. Its copier shrugs off the heat, so you add it [once and walk away](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kary_Mullis), and a small box does the rest. Police use it to match a smear of blood to a person, doctors use it to catch a virus from a faint trace, the teams who [first read out the whole human script](https://www.genome.gov/about-genomics/fact-sheets/Polymerase-Chain-Reaction-Fact-Sheet) leaned on it, and the [COVID test](https://daily.jstor.org/how-yellowstone-extremophile-bacteria-helped-with-covid-19-testing/) that swabbed the world is the same trick. Mullis took a [share of the 1993 Nobel Prize](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/chemistry/1993/mullis/facts/) for it. Cheap copying is the floor the rest of the DNA toolkit stands on, and the lead it opened only compounded.

*Curator's note:* **Placement.** Make cell, California, 1983. The "tool" is the copying method itself, a way to turn one chosen piece of DNA into billions, which is a Make capability (can you build one, here build many of one chosen thing) rather than a Measure or Model one. Serves Goal 1 (people already knew DNA could in principle be copied; having a cheap, repeatable bench method that anyone could run was a different thing, and that is what Mullis built) and Goal 2 (the copy tool is what made the read and write tools routine and cheap, so it seeded the whole molecular-biology capability that followed). Mechanism: Convergence, the copying step becomes the shared reservoir that every downstream DNA technique draws from. Confidence: Direct on the 1983 conception on Highway 128, the Cetus employment, the heat-cool doubling, the Taq fix, and the 1993 Nobel shared with Michael Smith, all multiply sourced. **Analysis.** This is the opening card on [[Thread - Reading & Writing DNA]] and the thread's "copy" layer. The thread is biology's read/write/edit stack, and PCR is the rung that made the others affordable: you cannot cheaply read or cut a piece of DNA you have only one faint copy of, so the photocopier comes first and feeds the rest. It is the wetware echo of the convergence cards on the silicon side (the purified crystal, the photolithography step) where one enabling tool pools and every later tool reaches into it. Cross-thread, it rhymes with [[Thread - Electronic Switching]] on a structural point the [[Thread - Reading & Writing DNA]] note already names: both stacks climb from a cheap enabling step toward an abstraction layer on top. It pairs, when those cards exist, against the read tool (sequencing) and the edit tool (the retypeable guide that cuts DNA at a chosen word), with PCR as the copy step beneath both. Hero, not parade: it is the thread's foundation card and carries real weight, so it should anchor the thread until the read/write/edit cards are written around it. **Nuance.** The exact night is given as spring 1983; the first successful bench demonstration is dated December 16, 1983, so the front says "spring 1983" for the idea and the back lets the payoff run later. The heat-proof copier is Taq, the DNA polymerase from the microbe Thermus aquaticus, which Thomas Brock first found in a Yellowstone hot spring in the 1960s and which Cetus (Randall Saiki and colleagues) brought into PCR around 1986; the card keeps all of that off the face and calls it "a bug that lives in a Yellowstone hot spring." Credit is genuinely contested: Mullis conceived the cycling idea and is the named "who," but the working method, the Taq step, and the patents came out of a Cetus team, and Mullis's later public life (HIV-AIDS denial, climate denial) is real but off-topic for a tooling card and left out. The ">a billion after thirty rounds" is 2^30, about 1.07 billion, the standard textbook figure for an ideal reaction, not a measured yield.
