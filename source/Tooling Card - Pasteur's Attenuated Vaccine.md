*Make · Paris, France · 1880s*
**Louis Pasteur** · weakening a germ on purpose

**Front.** In the summer of 1885, [Louis Pasteur](https://www.sciencehistory.org/education/scientific-biographies/louis-pasteur/) was a chemist in Paris who had spent six years teaching himself to make deadly germs weak. A nine-year-old boy, [Joseph Meister](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Meister), was carried into his lab, bitten fourteen times two days before by a rabid dog, and almost everyone who caught rabies died. Pasteur had hung the spinal cords of rabbits killed by rabies to dry in sealed jars, and the longer a cord dried the weaker its germ grew; starting [July 6](https://www.pasteur.fr/en/research-journal/news/history-first-rabies-vaccination-1885) he injected the boy with the oldest, weakest cord first, then a fresher one each day until the last was nearly full strength. He was running a recipe, not a lucky find: he had first watched a germ go weak on its own years earlier, when a forgotten [chicken-cholera culture](https://www.nature.com/articles/d42859-020-00008-5) went stale and stopped killing the birds it was fed to.

**Back.** Meister lived, and in 1886 alone Pasteur's lab treated more than [2,500 people](https://www.pasteur.fr/en/research-journal/news/history-first-rabies-vaccination-1885) bitten by rabid animals, who came to Paris from as far as Russia and America. Public money poured in to build the [Pasteur Institute](https://www.pasteur.fr/en/institut-pasteur/history/troisieme-epoque-1877-1887), opened in November 1888, which ran the same weakening method against germ after germ. The trick of tiring a known germ out on purpose became the standard way to grow a live vaccine, and the yellow-fever and measles shots given to children today are [made the same way](https://www.nature.com/articles/d42859-020-00008-5).
