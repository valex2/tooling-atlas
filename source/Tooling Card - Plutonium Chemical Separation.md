*Manufacture · Hanford, Washington, USA · 1940s*
**Glenn Seaborg and Stanley Thompson** · bismuth-phosphate carrier separation behind concrete

**Front.** A reactor breeds plutonium, but it breeds almost none. Irradiated uranium fuel holds the new metal at [250 to 300 parts per million](https://www.osti.gov/biblio/325360), buried in tons of uranium and in fission products so hot they would kill a man in the room. On 18 December 1942 [Stanley Thompson](https://ahf.nuclearmuseum.org/ahf/profile/stanley-g-thompson/), working for [Glenn Seaborg](https://ahf.nuclearmuseum.org/voices/oral-histories/glenn-seaborgs-interview/), found his answer in a powder: bismuth phosphate, dropped out of acid solution, dragged [98 percent of the plutonium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bismuth_phosphate_process) down with it and left the rest behind. The hard part was scale. The team had worked out the recipe on [40 micrograms](https://www.osti.gov/opennet/manhattan-project-history/Events/1942-1944_pu/seaborg_plutonium.htm) of plutonium, a speck too small to see, and had to prove the same chemistry would hold by the pound. At Hanford they ran it inside an [800-foot concrete canyon](https://atomicarchive.com/history/manhattan-project/p4s24.html), a row of forty cells each capped by removable blocks six feet thick weighing 35 tons, every step done by remote control while operators watched through periscopes from a shielded gallery above.

**Back.** It worked. The process pulled plutonium clean enough that the leftover fission products fell to less than [one part in ten million](https://www.osti.gov/biblio/325360), and the metal it harvested went into the [Trinity test and the Nagasaki bomb](https://www.atomicarchive.com/history/sites/t-plant.html). Both secrets held to the end of the war. Element 94 was so guarded that its name stayed classified and lab papers called it only ["49" or "product"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plutonium), and the discovery paper sat at *Physical Review* [until a year after the war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glenn_T._Seaborg). The chemistry never leaked because it was not a formula a spy could pocket. It was hands-on craft, learned by running tons of deadly fuel through concrete cells, and that opened the way to a plutonium bomb the United States built before anyone else could copy the harvesting.

*Curator's note:* **Placement.** Manufacture cell; Goal 2 (a weapons program, no civilian aim at this stage); Convergence (it joins ultramicrochemistry, carrier precipitation, and remote heavy shielding into one production line); Confidence Direct. **Analysis.** This is the harvest step in the bomb chain: the pile breeds plutonium, this process pulls it out, the implosion design fires it. It builds on [[Tooling Card - The Nuclear Pile]] and pairs with the enrichment and reactor cards as the other half of how the Manhattan Project made fissile material at scale. **Nuance.** The 18 December 1942 bench test, the 250 to 300 ppm feed, and the one-in-ten-million purity are all well-attested; I date the card 1945 to the production runs that fed Trinity and Nagasaki, not the 1942 lab moment. **Secrecy:** held — the United States guarded both the existence of element 94 and the bismuth-phosphate chemistry, and the secret held to the war's end because the making was tacit craft no notebook could carry off.
