*Make · United States (NASA and Bell Labs) · 1960s*
**John R. Pierce** · a giant shiny balloon in orbit

**Front.** [John Pierce](https://www.britannica.com/biography/John-Robinson-Pierce) at Bell Labs was the man who, years before, had picked the word [transistor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_R._Pierce) for the tiny new switch his colleagues had made. He kept turning over a simpler idea. A radio signal spreads out and fades as it travels, so to talk across an ocean you need something high up to throw the signal back down past the curve of the Earth. In a [1955 paper](https://www.satellitetoday.com/uncategorized/2010/03/11/sspi-timeline-1955-pierce/) he worked out that a shiny metal ball, [100 feet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project_Echo) wide and parked far overhead, would do it with no brains of its own at all. On [August 12, 1960](https://www.ebsco.com/research-starters/history/first-passive-communications-satellite-launched), NASA and Bell Labs put his ball in the sky: a [thin mylar balloon coated in aluminum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project_Echo), folded into a small canister, blown up to the size of a ten-story building once it reached orbit. It carried no radio, no power, nothing. It was just a mirror in space.

**Back.** That same day a recorded message from [President Eisenhower](https://www.history.com/speeches/first-speech-broadcast-by-satellite) was beamed up from California, glanced off the balloon, and came down clear in New Jersey, the [first voice ever relayed through space](https://www.nasa.gov/image-article/august-1960-project-echo-launched/). A dumb shiny ball had proved you could route a call by way of the sky. Within two years the same Bell Labs team, again led by Pierce, put a [real radio inside the satellite](https://www.ebsco.com/research-starters/history/first-passive-communications-satellite-launched) so it could catch a faint signal and send a strong one back. In July 1962 their [Telstar](https://www.britannica.com/biography/John-Robinson-Pierce) carried the first live television across the Atlantic, and every working communications satellite since has been an active repeater, not a mirror.
