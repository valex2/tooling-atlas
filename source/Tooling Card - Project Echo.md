*Make · United States (NASA and Bell Labs) · 1960s*
**John R. Pierce** · a giant shiny balloon in orbit

**Front.** [John Pierce](https://www.britannica.com/biography/John-Robinson-Pierce) at Bell Labs was the man who, years before, had picked the word [transistor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_R._Pierce) for the tiny new switch his colleagues had made. He kept turning over a simpler idea. A radio signal spreads out and fades as it travels, so to talk across an ocean you need something high up to throw the signal back down past the curve of the Earth. In a [1955 paper](https://www.satellitetoday.com/uncategorized/2010/03/11/sspi-timeline-1955-pierce/) he worked out that a shiny metal ball, [100 feet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project_Echo) wide and parked far overhead, would do it with no brains of its own at all. On [August 12, 1960](https://www.ebsco.com/research-starters/history/first-passive-communications-satellite-launched), NASA and Bell Labs put his ball in the sky: a [thin mylar balloon coated in aluminum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project_Echo), folded into a small canister, blown up to the size of a ten-story building once it reached orbit. It carried no radio, no power, nothing. It was just a mirror in space.

**Back.** That same day a recorded message from [President Eisenhower](https://www.history.com/speeches/first-speech-broadcast-by-satellite) was beamed up from California, glanced off the balloon, and came down clear in New Jersey, the [first voice ever relayed through space](https://www.nasa.gov/image-article/august-1960-project-echo-launched/). A dumb shiny ball had proved you could route a call by way of the sky. Within two years the same Bell Labs team, again led by Pierce, put a [real radio inside the satellite](https://www.ebsco.com/research-starters/history/first-passive-communications-satellite-launched) so it could catch a faint signal and send a strong one back, and [Telstar](https://www.britannica.com/biography/John-Robinson-Pierce) carried live television across the Atlantic. The mirror was a one-time proof; the lead compounded once the brain went on board.

*Curator's note:* **Placement.** Make cell, United States, 1960. Serves Goal 1, that closing the distance to orbit took a built object, not just the math, which Pierce had finished in 1955. Mechanism tagged Convergence: Echo is where the war-grown reservoir (large balloons, big tracking antennas, the maser receiver at Holmdel, launch vehicles) converges on one demonstration, though the convergence is thinner here than on a capstone like EUV, so the tag strains a little (see below). Confidence: Direct on the launch, the passive-reflector design, Pierce's authorship, and the Eisenhower bounce, all multiply sourced. **Analysis.** This is a Parade card, a clean early beat on [[Thread - Long-Distance Comms]] that carries the signal from wire and air up to orbit for the first time, and a [[Thread - Bell Labs]] entry showing patient monopoly capital funding a satellite with no product in sight. It is the first half of its pair: Echo (a passive mirror, no power, proof only) set against [[Tooling Card - Telstar]] (an active brain on board, the durable thing). That pairing is the card's real job, mirror versus repeater, dumb versus smart, so it should defer to Telstar as the hero of the two and stay parade. Pierce coining "transistor" and then needing a tube amplifier on Telstar (the transistors of 1962 could not yet make the power) is a nice irony to hold for the Telstar card, not this one. **Nuance.** The "100 feet" is the round design figure; the orbiting balloon is usually given as ~30.5 m / 98–100 ft. The 1955 paper ("Orbital Radio Relays," Jet Propulsion, April 1955) proposed both a passive sphere and an active repeater, so Echo and Telstar are both Pierce ideas built in sequence; Echo had an earlier failed launch (Echo on a Delta in May 1960 that did not reach orbit) before the August 12 success, which is the date pinned here. Pierce shares satellite paternity in the sources with Rudolf Kompfner and the wider NASA/JPL team; the card keeps Pierce as the named "who" per his authorship of the design and his lead on both Echo and Telstar. The [[Tooling Card - Telstar]] note does not exist yet; the pair is set one-sided here for it to be completed when that card is written.
