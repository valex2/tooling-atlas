*Measure · Stockholm, Sweden · 1990s*
**Pål Nyrén & Mostafa Ronaghi** · read DNA by watching light

**Front.** In 1996, a biochemistry teacher at KTH in Stockholm named [Pål Nyrén](https://link.springer.com/protocol/10.1385/1-59745-377-3:1) and his student [Mostafa Ronaghi](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.281.5375.363) were tired of reading DNA the slow way, by pulling cut-up strands through a jelly and reading the bands by hand. So they built a reaction that answers out loud. They pinned down a strand to be read, then fed it one kind of base at a time and let a copying enzyme try to join it on; each time a base clicked into place it threw off a scrap of waste, and two more enzymes turned that scrap into [a tiny flash of light](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/8923969/). Now you could read the letters by watching which base made the tube glow, in real time, with no jelly at all.

**Back.** The first runs read only about [fifteen letters](https://link.springer.com/protocol/10.1385/1-59745-377-3:1) before the signal muddied. But the reaction scaled. In 1997 the Stockholm group started a company to sell it, and in 2005 an American firm, [454 Life Sciences](https://ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC1464427), spread the same light-flash chemistry across [1.6 million tiny wells](https://www.nature.com/articles/nature03959) on one slide and read 25 million letters in a single four-hour run. That machine was the first of the next-generation sequencers, and the lead compounded: from fifteen letters in a Stockholm lab to millions in one run.
