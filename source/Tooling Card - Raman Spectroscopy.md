*Measure · Calcutta, India · 1920s*
**C. V. Raman** · light that comes back a different colour

**Front.** In 1928, a physicist in [Calcutta named C. V. Raman](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/physics/1930/raman/facts/) had spent years puzzling over why the sea looks blue. He wanted to know whether clear liquid, struck by a beam of light, sends back only the same colour or something else as well. So [on 28 February](https://www.acs.org/education/whatischemistry/landmarks/ramaneffect.html) he shone a narrow beam of blue light from [a mercury lamp](https://www.optica-opn.org/home/articles/volume_20/issue_3/features/c_v_raman_and_the_raman_effect/) into a bottle of pure liquid, set a green filter on the far side, and looked in from the side through a small spectrograph. A faint smear of green showed up where no green should be: [about one in ten million](https://www.mt.com/us/en/home/applications/L1_AutoChem_Applications/Raman-Spectroscopy/raman-scattering.html) of the light had come out shifted in colour, bent by the tiny shaking of the molecule it hit.

**Back.** That colour shift is a fingerprint. Each kind of bond shakes at its own rate, so the size of the shift tells you what a thing is made of. Raman won the [Nobel Prize in 1930](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/physics/1930/raman/facts/), the first person from Asia to win one in the sciences. But the effect was so weak that reading it meant hours of patient photography, and it sat as a lab curiosity for thirty years. Only when the [laser arrived](https://www.edinst.com/blog/the-life-of-c-v-raman/) in the 1960s, giving a bright steady beam, did the faint green streak become a fast instrument that now reads paint and medicine in seconds.
