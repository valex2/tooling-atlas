*Make · California, USA · 1970s*
**Stanley Cohen and Herbert Boyer** · cut a gene out and paste it in

**Front.** Late one night in [November 1972](https://www.sciencehistory.org/education/scientific-biographies/herbert-w-boyer-and-stanley-n-cohen/), two scientists at a meeting in Hawaii sat over sandwiches at a deli and talked shop. [Stanley Cohen](https://www.sciencehistory.org/education/scientific-biographies/herbert-w-boyer-and-stanley-n-cohen/) worked at Stanford on small rings of bacterial DNA that he could feed into a living cell. [Herbert Boyer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herbert_Boyer) at UCSF had a tiny protein, borrowed from bacteria, that worked like [scissors that cut DNA at a chosen short word](https://www.ebsco.com/research-starters/history/cohen-and-boyer-develop-recombinant-dna-technology). Each had half of a tool. In [the spring of 1973](https://www.sciencehistory.org/education/scientific-biographies/herbert-w-boyer-and-stanley-n-cohen/) they put the halves together: they cut a piece out of one ring of DNA, cut a gene out of another, joined the two cut ends, and dropped the new ring back into living bacteria. The new thing was not reading the code or even cutting it. It was pasting a chosen gene into a living cell and having it stick.

**Back.** The bacteria did something better than hold the new gene. Every time they split in two they copied it, so a single edited cell became a jar full of cells all carrying the same paste-in. By [1974](https://www.genome.gov/25520303/online-education-kit-1973-first-animal-gene-cloned) the pair had moved a gene from a frog into bacteria and watched the bacteria run it, proof you could write a gene from any creature into a living thing. Boyer carried the trick out of the lab and into a company, [Genentech, founded in 1976](https://www.genome.gov/25520305/online-education-kit-1976-first-genetic-engineering-company), which soon set bacteria to making [human insulin](https://www.genome.gov/25520305/online-education-kit-1976-first-genetic-engineering-company) instead of scraping it from the pancreas of pigs. The cut-and-paste did not stay one experiment. It became the floor that every later gene tool stands on, and the lead it opened in California has compounded ever since.

*Curator's note:* **Placement.** Make cell, California, 1973. The tool is the cut-and-paste itself: scissors that snip DNA at a chosen word (restriction enzymes, EcoRI in the actual work), a small carrier ring you can feed to a cell (a plasmid, pSC101), and a way to seal the join, used together to write a gene into a living thing. Serves Goal 1 (knowing life ran on a code, which the central dogma had already declared, was not the same as being able to *write* it, and the writing took this built tool) and Goal 2 (the cut-and-paste craft planted a deliberate-DNA-writing lead in the Bay Area that Genentech and the whole biotech industry cashed in). Mechanism: Convergence, two half-tools from two labs pooling into one method that then becomes the reservoir every later read/copy/edit tool draws from. Confidence: Direct, the November 1972 deli meeting, the spring 1973 experiment, the 1974 frog gene, and the 1976 Genentech founding are all multiply sourced (Science History Institute, NIH genome.gov, EBSCO). **Analysis.** This is the keystone Make card of [[Thread - Reading & Writing DNA]], the moment the thread's "write" layer turns on. The thread is a rising stack, read then copy then cut then edit then design, and this card is the first time anyone wrote a chosen gene into a cell on purpose, so it is the hinge the later layers stack on top of. It pairs against [[Tooling Card - CRISPR-Cas9]] across forty years on purpose: 1973 is crude cut-and-paste, you snip wherever the bacterial scissors happen to land and paste a whole gene in, while 2012 is precise find-and-replace, a retypeable guide that points the same scissors at any chosen spot. Same act, writing DNA, but blunt tool versus aimed tool, which is the cleanest way to show how far the craft climbed. It is a Hero, not Parade: it carries the thread's central claim that life became programmable, and it is the entry point for the whole write half of the stack. Cross-thread: its first product, bacteria grown to make human insulin, is the seed of the biologics-manufacturing story, so it feeds the same Make-to-Manufacture arc the chip and penicillin cards walk, growing a drug instead of extracting it. **Nuance.** The face compresses a real sequence. The 1973 experiment recombined two *bacterial* plasmids (Cohen, Chang, Boyer, Helling); the cross-*species* feat, a frog gene run in E. coli, came in 1974, so the front pins the cut-and-paste tool to 1973 and the back uses the frog as the 1974 proof, which is honest to both dates. Cohen and Boyer had two co-authors, Annie Chang at Stanford and Robert Helling at UCSF, folded here into "two scientists" for the face. The "scissors that cut at a chosen word" are restriction enzymes; EcoRI cut staggered ends that latched onto each other, which is why no glue-on was needed, a detail too fiddly for the front. Genentech's first protein made in bacteria was somatostatin in 1977; insulin followed and was the first marketed recombinant drug, by 1982, so the back says bacteria "soon" made insulin rather than dating it to 1976. The Mechanism tag leans Convergence cleanly here and does not strain; this is a tacit-craft Make tool, not a model, so the Models-are-non-rival caveat does not apply.
