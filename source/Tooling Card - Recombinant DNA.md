*Make · California, USA · 1970s*
**Stanley Cohen and Herbert Boyer** · cut a gene out and paste it in

**Front.** Late one night in [November 1972](https://www.sciencehistory.org/education/scientific-biographies/herbert-w-boyer-and-stanley-n-cohen/), two scientists at a meeting in Hawaii sat over sandwiches at a deli and talked shop. [Stanley Cohen](https://www.sciencehistory.org/education/scientific-biographies/herbert-w-boyer-and-stanley-n-cohen/) worked at Stanford on small rings of bacterial DNA that he could feed into a living cell. [Herbert Boyer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herbert_Boyer) at UCSF had a tiny protein, borrowed from bacteria, that worked like [scissors that cut DNA at a chosen short word](https://www.ebsco.com/research-starters/history/cohen-and-boyer-develop-recombinant-dna-technology). Each had half of a tool. In [the spring of 1973](https://www.sciencehistory.org/education/scientific-biographies/herbert-w-boyer-and-stanley-n-cohen/) they put the halves together: they cut a piece out of one ring of DNA, cut a gene out of another, joined the two cut ends, and dropped the new ring back into living bacteria. Reading the code was old, and so was cutting it; pasting a chosen gene into a living cell and having it stick was not.

**Back.** The bacteria did something better than hold the new gene. Every time they split in two they copied it, so a single edited cell became a jar full of cells all carrying the same paste-in. By [1974](https://www.genome.gov/25520303/online-education-kit-1973-first-animal-gene-cloned) the pair had moved a gene from a frog into bacteria and watched the bacteria run it, proof you could write a gene from any creature into a living thing. Boyer carried the trick out of the lab and into a company, [Genentech, founded in 1976](https://www.genome.gov/25520305/online-education-kit-1976-first-genetic-engineering-company), which soon set bacteria to making [human insulin](https://www.genome.gov/25520305/online-education-kit-1976-first-genetic-engineering-company) instead of scraping it from the pancreas of pigs. The cut-and-paste became the floor that every later gene tool stands on, and the firm Boyer cofounded still sits in South San Francisco at the head of an industry that grew up around it.
