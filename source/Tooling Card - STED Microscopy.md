*Measure · Heidelberg, Germany · 2000s*
**Stefan Hell** · a ring of light beats the blur

**Front.** In 2000, Stefan Hell, a physicist who had taken his doctorate in Heidelberg and now ran a small group at the [Max Planck Institute in Göttingen](https://www.mpinat.mpg.de/645046/research), was chasing a rule most people thought could not be broken. Since 1873 it had held that no [light microscope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optical_microscope) could tell apart two things closer together than about [200 nanometres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffraction-limited_system), roughly half the width of a light wave; anything smaller just blurred into one smear. Hell kept the old trick of making the sample glow, then added a second beam shaped like a [ring with a dark hole in its middle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/STED_microscopy) and laid it right on top of the first. Everywhere the ring's light landed it forced the glowing dye to switch off at once, so only the pinhole of dark at the centre still shone, a glowing spot far smaller than any beam of light can be.

**Back.** With the ring turned up bright the glowing spot shrank to about [20 nanometres](https://www.chemistryworld.com/news/the-million-dollar-microscope/3003963.article), ten times finer than any light microscope before it, sharp enough to pick out single clumps of protein inside a cell. The idea had been [in print since 1994](https://opg.optica.org/ol/abstract.cfm?uri=ol-19-11-780), yet it took six years of building to make one work, and seven more before [Leica sold](https://www.leica-microsystems.com/company/news/news-details/for-175-years-leica-microsystems-has-successfully-contributed-to-progress-with-groundbreaking-innovations/) the first one anyone could buy in 2007. In 2014 the Nobel committee split its chemistry prize between Hell and [two others](https://optics.org/news/5/10/13) who had each found a different route past Ernst Abbe's old wall.
