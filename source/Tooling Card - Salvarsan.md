*Make · Frankfurt, Germany · 1900s*
**Paul Ehrlich** · a numbered shelf of drug compounds

**Front.** By 1909, [Paul Ehrlich](https://www.smithsonianmag.com/smart-news/syphilis-cure-magic-bullet-180964644/) ran a lab in Frankfurt where he had spent years watching dyes stain some cells and leave others clean. Syphilis was everywhere, and the one common treatment was mercury, a poison that often hurt the patient about as much as the [spirochaete](https://www.jameslindlibrary.org/articles/the-introduction-of-chemotherapy-using-arsphenamine-the-first-magic-bullet/) it was meant to kill. Ehrlich's chemists cooked one [arsenic compound](https://cen.acs.org/articles/83/i25/Salvarsan.html) after another and gave each a number, and his assistant Sahachiro Hata injected them, one at a time, into rabbits sick with the disease. [Number 606](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arsphenamine) killed the microbe and spared the animal. It was a drug found not by luck but by working down a whole numbered shelf of compounds until one hit.

**Back.** It went on sale in [1910](https://www.ebsco.com/research-starters/history/ehrlich-introduces-salvarsan-cure-syphilis) as Salvarsan, and by the end of that year some [65,000 doses had reached 20,000 patients](https://www.jameslindlibrary.org/articles/the-introduction-of-chemotherapy-using-arsphenamine-the-first-magic-bullet/). Drug houses took up the way of working: make a long line of near-identical compounds, then test them one by one against the sickness and keep the winner. Salvarsan and its milder follow-up stayed the standard syphilis cure for about thirty years, until [penicillin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arsphenamine) pushed it aside in the 1940s.
