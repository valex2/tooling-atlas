*Manufacture · Jena, Germany · 1880s*
**Otto Schott** · optical glass melted to recipe

**Front.** In [1879](https://www.lichtgedanken.uni-jena.de/en/1672/the-history-of-glass-research-in-jena), [Otto Schott](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Friedrich-Otto-Schott), a young chemist in Witten whose father made window glass, wanted to know what lithium, a metal no glassmaker used, would do to light. Glassmakers then melted by the ton and cut lenses from whichever pieces set clear; Schott melted by the spoonful, in crucibles on a lab bench, changing one ingredient at a time and writing every recipe down, and that May he mailed a lump of his lithium glass to [Ernst Abbe](https://www.zeiss.com/vision-care/us/eye-health-and-care/understanding-vision/carl-zeiss-ernst-abbe-and-otto-schott-a-winning-team.html), the scientist behind the Zeiss microscope works, to have its light-bending measured. Abbe measured it, asked for more, and in [1884](https://www.schott.com/en-gb/about-us/company/history/corporate-history) he, Schott and Carl Zeiss opened a glassworks in Jena, backed with [Prussian state money](https://www.chemistryworld.com/opinion/schotts-glass/8480.article), to melt glass the same careful way at scale. Schott logged [more than a thousand](https://www.chemistryworld.com/opinion/schotts-glass/8480.article) trial melts by 1886, and the works' first [price list](https://belglas.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/01/perspectives_on_the_history_of_glass_com.pdf) that year offered 44 glasses, 19 of them never made before: for the first time, a lens maker could order glass by its numbers and get the same glass every time.

**Back.** Abbe folded the new glasses into the [apochromat](https://bitesizebio.com/44377/corrected-lenses-and-objectives/) in 1886, a microscope lens that performed at the theoretical limit, and within a decade lens makers across Europe and America were ordering from the Jena price list instead of gambling on their own pots. The lead compounded for thirty years, so completely that when war came in 1914 Britain was buying [60 percent](https://www.optica-opn.org/home/articles/volume_27/january_2016/features/how_the_great_war_changed_the_optics_industry/the_british_glass_scramble/) of its optical glass from Jena, and in 1915 it secretly offered Germany [rubber in exchange for binoculars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_World_War_glass%E2%80%93rubber_exchange); the German war office said it could deliver 30,000 pairs. The swap never went through, and Britain needed a state crash program and most of two years before, by [1916](https://edconway.substack.com/p/the-glass-famine), it could melt optical glass it trusted on its own.
