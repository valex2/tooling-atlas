*Model · Zurich, Switzerland · 1920s*
**Erwin Schrödinger** · one equation for electron waves

**Front.** At Christmas 1925, a Vienna-born physics professor named [Erwin Schrödinger](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/physics/1933/schrodinger/facts/) left Zurich for the mountain village of [Arosa](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.05337), carrying a jab from a seminar: a young Frenchman, [Louis de Broglie](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/physics/1929/broglie/facts/), had claimed every electron is a wave, and his colleague Peter Debye had scoffed that wave talk means nothing until someone writes the wave equation. Schrödinger spent the holiday with pen and paper and came back down in January with the equation: solve it for an atom and out come the standing waves an electron can settle into, the way a string can only ring at certain notes. He solved it for hydrogen, and the energy steps matched the [lines of light](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/andp.19263840404) hydrogen gives off. The first paper reached the journal on [January 27, 1926](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.05337), with three more inside five months; anyone who could do the mathematics now owned a machine for predicting atoms.

**Back.** The equation was printed for anyone to read, and at first almost no one could use it: [Dirac observed in 1929](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rspa.1929.0094) that the laws for the whole of chemistry were now known, and only too complicated to solve. [Douglas Hartree](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/mathematical-proceedings-of-the-cambridge-philosophical-society/article/abs/wave-mechanics-of-an-atom-with-a-noncoulomb-central-field-part-i-theory-and-methods/C9417AC1CEC84B934C1EA4C4B8401FEF) began grinding out approximate answers for single atoms on a desk calculator in 1928; whole molecules had to wait decades for electronic computers. In [1998](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/chemistry/1998/press-release/) the chemistry Nobel went to Walter Kohn and John Pople for the computer methods that solve it approximately, and by then the solving, and the lead that came with it, sat in American software and American machines, not in Zurich.
