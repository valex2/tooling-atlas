*Manufacture · Chang'an, China · 6th century*
**Two Nestorian monks (under Justinian I)** · raising silkworms and reeling cocoon filament

**Front.** For about [two thousand years](https://www.historicmysteries.com/history/silk/28329/) China alone knew how to make silk, and the [law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_silk) killed anyone who carried silkworms, their eggs, or mulberry seeds past the border. The craft was a chain of living steps: feed the moth's caterpillar on mulberry leaves, let it spin a cocoon, then drop the cocoons in hot water to soften the gum that holds them shut. A worker finds the loose end of one cocoon and unwinds a single thread that runs [around 900 meters](https://www.britannica.com/animal/silkworm-moth) long, thinner than a hair, and twists several together into one strong filament. None of this was written down for outsiders. The whole secret was the live worm, the mulberry leaf, and the hands that knew how to reel, and none of it could be faked from the outside.

**Back.** Around 552 the emperor [Justinian I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smuggling_of_silkworm_eggs_into_the_Byzantine_Empire) sent two monks back along the road they knew, and they hid silkworm eggs inside their [hollow bamboo canes](https://brewminate.com/stealing-the-worm-silk-production-in-the-byzantine-empire/), too small to spot and able to survive the trip if eggs went instead of fragile adults. The secret was stolen, not bought. Byzantium learned to feed the worms and reel the thread, and that one theft opened the way to a Roman silk industry that held its own [monopoly in Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smuggling_of_silkworm_eggs_into_the_Byzantine_Empire) for centuries. The craft was alive, so it could walk. A two-thousand-year lead ended once a few eggs fit inside a cane.
