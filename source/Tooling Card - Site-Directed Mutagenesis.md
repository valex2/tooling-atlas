*Make · Vancouver, Canada · 1970s*
**Michael Smith** · rewriting one chosen DNA letter

**Front.** In 1976, a chemist named [Michael Smith](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Smith_(chemist)) sat over coffee at Cambridge with a friend, Clyde Hutchison, chewing on a stubborn problem: you could now read a gene letter by letter, but you could not change one letter you picked. Back in his lab in Vancouver, Smith built [a short piece of DNA, just twelve letters long](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/681366/), that matched a stretch of a virus's gene everywhere but one spot. He let the little piece find its match and stick, then copied the whole ring of viral DNA off it. The copy carried the single wrong letter he had planted, the first time anyone had rewritten one chosen spot in a gene on purpose.

**Back.** At first the method spread slowly, because grinding out a clean twelve-letter primer by hand was hard work. Once machines could print short DNA to order cheaply, every lab that studied proteins picked it up, swapping one building block of a protein at a time to see which one mattered. Smith shared the [1993 Nobel Prize in Chemistry](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/chemistry/1993/press-release/) with Kary Mullis for the trick, and the same targeted rewrite now opens the way to tuned antibody drugs and gene editing, a first step so routine no one bothers to name it.
