*Measure · Jena, Germany · 1890s*
**Ernst Abbe** · scale-in-line length comparator

**Front.** In 1890, [Ernst Abbe](https://www.zeiss.com/corporate/en/about-zeiss/past/history/ernst-abbe.html), the physics professor who ran the Zeiss lens works in Jena, wanted a gauge his shop could trust to check the thickness of finished optics. He knew why the usual tools lied: a sliding caliper holds its ruled scale beside the piece, so any small tilt in the jaw gets multiplied by the gap between scale and piece and lands in the reading. He [built a thickness gauge](https://www.zeiss.com/corporate/en/about-zeiss/past/history/technological-milestones/industrial-metrology.html) laid out the other way, piece and ruled scale sitting on one straight line, nose to tail, so the same tilt now bends the reading by almost nothing. Then he wrote the layout down as a [three-page rule](https://link.springer.com/referenceworkentry/10.1007/978-3-642-35950-7_16793-1) for instrument builders: put the scale in line with the thing you measure.

**Back.** Abbe put [Carl Pulfrich](https://www.encyclopedia.com/science/dictionaries-thesauruses-pictures-and-press-releases/pulfrich-carl) in charge of a new measuring department at Zeiss that same year, and the firm turned the rule into a product line, up through a [full length-measuring machine in 1922](https://www.zeiss.com/corporate/en/about-zeiss/past/history/technological-milestones/industrial-metrology.html). The rule itself spread everywhere: a machinist's micrometer [obeys it and a sliding caliper does not](https://engineeringnotes.org/metrology/abbes-principle-of-alignment/), which is why the micrometer is the one trusted for fine work, and builders of today's coordinate measuring machines still count every sideways offset as [Abbe error](https://link.springer.com/referenceworkentry/10.1007/978-3-642-35950-7_16793-1). The lead compounded in the firm he [signed over to a foundation](https://www.zeiss.com/corporate/en/about-zeiss/past/history/ernst-abbe.html) rather than his heirs: Zeiss went from grinding lenses in Jena to selling the machines that check other machines' parts, and sells them still.
