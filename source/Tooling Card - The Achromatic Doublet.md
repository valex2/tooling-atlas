*Make · London, UK · 1750s*
**John Dollond & Chester Moor Hall** · crown-and-flint colour-cancelling lens pair

**Front.** In 1757, [John Dollond](https://www.britannica.com/biography/John-Dollond), a Spitalfields silk weaver's son who had worked the loom himself before [joining his son Peter's optics shop](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Dollond) at forty-six, kept running into the fault that ruined every telescope built from lenses: stars came ringed with colour fringes, and [Newton had concluded](https://www.encyclopedia.com/people/science-and-technology/technology-biographies/john-dollond) that no glass could cure it, so makers had stopped trying. Dollond decided to test the great man. He set a prism of common crown glass against a prism of flint glass, the heavy English glass made with lead, and [found](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rstl.1757.0088) that flint spread light into colours far more strongly than crown for the same bend; so he ground a convex lens of crown and a concave lens of flint and fitted them face to face, and the flint undid the crown's colours while most of its bending survived. The pair brought all the colours to one focus, the first lens that ever did, and on [19 April 1758](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Dollond) he took out a fourteen-year patent on it and won the Royal Society's [Copley Medal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Dollond) that same year.

**Back.** The doublet went to the front of nearly every refracting telescope made after it, and for fourteen years the Dollond shop held the only legal right in England to sell one. In [1766](https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Dictionary_of_National_Biography,_1885-1900/Dollond,_John) rival London makers fought the patent in court, showing that a barrister named [Chester Moor Hall](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Chester-Moor-Hall) had built the same lens pair back in 1733 and hidden it, splitting the crown and flint halves [between two different shops](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chester_Moore_Hall) so that no one would see the whole design; the judge let the Dollonds keep the patent anyway, because Hall had kept his lens from the public. In [1830](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Joseph-Jackson-Lister) Joseph Jackson Lister shrank the doublet down to microscope size, opening the way to the cell, and the shop Peter Dollond opened in 1750 went on selling optics under the Dollond name [until 2009](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dollond_%26_Aitchison), two hundred and fifty-nine years.
