*Measure · Baghdad, Abbasid Caliphate · 800s*
**Abbasid astronomers (al-Fazari)** · brass disc that models the sky

**Front.** Around the year 800, in the new round city of [Baghdad](https://www.encyclopedia.com/science/dictionaries-thesauruses-pictures-and-press-releases/al-fazar), the caliph's star-watchers needed the hour after dark, the way to Mecca, and how far north they stood, and they had no clock. The Greeks had long before worked out how to squash the round dome of the sky onto a flat plate, drawing each point as a line cast from one pole ([Hipparchus, about 150 BCE](https://www.worldhistory.org/Astrolabe/)). One Baghdad man, [al-Fazari](https://islamsci.mcgill.ca/RASI/BEA/Fazari_BEA.htm), is named as the first Muslim to cast that flattened sky into brass: a disc cut with the sky's grid, a fretwork star map that spun over it on a center pin, and a sighting bar hung on the back. Now the whole dome of the sky sat in the hand, and you read the time and your latitude by turning its parts.

**Back.** In Muslim workshops the tool grew deep. The 10th-century astronomer [al-Sufi listed more than a thousand](https://muslimheritage.com/article/star-finders-astrolabes) things you could do with one, and the oldest astrolabe still standing was cut in Baghdad in the 800s. For three hundred years the finest came from the Islamic world, each plate re-engraved for the latitude of a new city. Then the knowing crossed through Muslim Spain into Latin, European shops learned to file the brass, and by [1391 an English poet was writing a guide](https://muslimheritage.com/article/star-finders-astrolabes) so his ten-year-old son could read the sky by hand.
