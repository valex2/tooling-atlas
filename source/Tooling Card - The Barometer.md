*Measure · Florence, Italy · 1640s*
**Evangelista Torricelli** · inverted mercury tube reading air pressure

**Front.** In 1643 in Florence, [Evangelista Torricelli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evangelista_Torricelli) filled a glass tube about a meter long with mercury, sealed one end, and stood it upside down in a dish of more mercury. The column slid down but stopped at about [760 millimeters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barometer), leaving an empty space at the closed top. The mercury did not drain out because the weight of the air pressing on the open dish held it up, and the empty space above was a [vacuum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torricellian_vacuum), something many had argued could not exist. The height of that column was now a number for how hard the atmosphere pushes.

**Back.** Five years later [Blaise Pascal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blaise_Pascal) had the tube carried up the Puy de Dôme and watched the column drop as it climbed, proving it really was the weight of the air doing the work. Pressure was now something you could read off a scale, and that one number underwrote weather forecasting, the measure of altitude, and the finding that air thins with height. It also turned the vacuum from a philosophical fight into a tool: once you knew the atmosphere pushes with a measurable force, you could build a machine that lets it push a piston.
