*Make · Cambridge, Massachusetts · 2010s*
**Alexis Komor and David Liu** · change one DNA letter without cutting

**Front.** Most inherited disease comes down to one wrong letter in the DNA. In 2015 [Alexis Komor](https://www.broadinstitute.org/news/base-editing-enables-chemistry-individual-dna-bases) was working in David Liu's lab in Cambridge, Massachusetts, with no tool that could change one. CRISPR cuts clean through both strands of DNA and leaves the cell to patch the wound, which is a fine way to wreck a gene and a poor way to repair one. So Komor stopped cutting and started doing chemistry on the letter itself. She fused three pieces into a single protein: a Cas9 with its cutting jaws switched off, a [deaminase enzyme borrowed from a rat](https://escholarship.org/content/qt7pq224vk/qt7pq224vk.pdf) that strips a chemical group off a C and turns it into something the cell reads as a T, and a small protein from a [virus that infects bacteria](https://escholarship.org/content/qt7pq224vk/qt7pq224vk.pdf), whose only job is to jam the repair crew that would scrub the change back out. Point it with a guide and it parks on the chosen spot, and the rat enzyme reaches a window about five letters wide. The [2016 paper](https://www.nature.com/articles/nature17946) reported the letter changed in more than 30 percent of human cells, with the ragged insertions and deletions that plague cutting down near [1 percent](https://blog.addgene.org/single-base-editing-with-crispr). The double helix was never broken.

**Back.** A year later Nicole Gaudelli ran [seven rounds of directed evolution](https://blog.addgene.org/single-base-editing-with-crispr) on a bacterial enzyme that had never touched DNA until she made it, and the second editor could turn an A into a G. Between them the two editors cover the four letter swaps that account for [more than 60 percent](https://www.liugroup.us/research-overview/genome-editing/) of the point mutations known to cause human disease. Addgene has shipped the constructs [more than 9,000 times to over 3,000 labs](https://www.liugroup.us/research-overview/genome-editing/). In August 2024 a boy called KJ Muldoon was born in Philadelphia missing a working liver enzyme, a disease that turns up [once in 1.3 million births](https://erictopol.substack.com/p/the-first-human-to-undergo-in-vivo), and by five months he was on the transplant list. [Rebecca Ahrens-Nicklas and Kiran Musunuru](https://www.science.org/content/article/gene-editing-therapy-made-just-6-months-helps-baby-life-threatening-disease) built him an adenine base editor against his father's stop mutation and dosed him in [February 2025](https://www.nejm.org/doi/10.1056/NEJMe2505704), six months after they started. The editor was stock, the fatty bubble that carried it was stock, the factory was stock. The one part made for him was the guide sequence someone typed.
