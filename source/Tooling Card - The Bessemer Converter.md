*Manufacture · London, England · 1850s*
**Henry Bessemer** · air blown up through molten pig iron

**Front.** In January 1855 Henry Bessemer, a paint and type-metal man with no trade in iron, was renting a workshop in St Pancras and chasing a stronger gun metal, because the [spinning artillery shell he had shown to Napoleon III](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_Bessemer) would burst a cast-iron cannon. He was melting pig iron in a shallow furnace when he noticed two lumps sitting at the edge of the bath in the path of the air, [still unmelted in a fierce heat](https://www.gutenberg.org/files/29633/29633-h/29633-h.htm), and on breaking them open found thin shells of soft iron with the carbon burned clean out. So he built a vessel to do that on purpose: a clay-lined pot with holes in the bottom and a blast forced up through [eight hundred pounds of molten pig iron](https://www.gutenberg.org/files/29633/29633-h/29633-h.htm). He lit no fire under it. The carbon and silicon in the iron burned as the air went through, the charge heated itself past its own melting point, and after about half an hour he poured out steel. He read the method to the British Association [at Cheltenham in August 1856](https://www.gracesguide.co.uk/Henry_Bessemer) and ironmasters bought [£27,000 of licences](https://www.gracesguide.co.uk/Henry_Bessemer) inside a month.

**Back.** The licensees could not make sound metal and Bessemer [bought the licences back for £31,500](https://www.gracesguide.co.uk/Henry_Bessemer), because most British ore carried phosphorus, the blast left it sitting in the steel, and the bars cracked. Göran Göransson, an ironmaster at Edske in Sweden, ran the first steady industrial blow on [18 July 1858](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G%C3%B6ran_Fredrik_G%C3%B6ransson) on almost phosphorus-free Swedish charcoal pig iron, [driving the blast harder](https://www.tekniskamuseet.se/en/learn-more/swedish-inventors/goran-fredrik-goransson-steel-industry/) to hold the heat all the way through; Robert Mushet's habit of [stirring in manganese-rich spiegeleisen](https://www.britannica.com/technology/Bessemer-process) after the burn took the brittleness out. Alexander Holley then designed [eleven of the first thirteen American converter plants](https://www.construction-physics.com/p/the-rise-of-steel-part-ii), and a ton of steel rail that cost [$167 in 1867 cost $17.62 by 1898](https://www.construction-physics.com/p/the-rise-of-steel-part-ii). Sidney Gilchrist Thomas and Percy Gilchrist swapped the clay lining for burnt dolomite in [1878](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gilchrist%E2%80%93Thomas_process), which caught the phosphorus in the slag and opened the poor ore of Lorraine and the Ruhr to German steelmakers. The converter itself ran about fifty years before the open-hearth furnace, slower but easier to steer, took [two thirds of American output by 1911](https://www.construction-physics.com/p/the-rise-of-steel-part-ii). Nobody went back to the puddler's [eighty-pound batch](https://www.gutenberg.org/files/29633/29633-h/29633-h.htm).
