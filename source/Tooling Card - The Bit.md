*Model · Murray Hill, New Jersey · 1940s*
**Claude Shannon** · a mathematical theory of communication

**Front.** In 1948 a young mathematician at Bell Labs named [Claude Shannon](https://spectrum.ieee.org/claude-shannon-tinkerer-prankster-and-father-of-information-theory) was working on an old, stubborn problem. Every wire and every radio link adds a hiss of noise that garbles the message, and for a hundred years engineers had only two answers: shout louder, or send slower. They were sure a noisy line could never carry a message perfectly. Shannon threw out the meaning of the message and counted only the choices in it, measuring each one in a new unit: the [bit, a single yes-or-no](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bit) his colleague John Tukey had named the year before. [In one paper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A_Mathematical_Theory_of_Communication) he proved that every channel has an exact speed limit and that below it you can beat the noise with clever coding instead of brute force. He had not built a device. He had drawn the map: the [first exact math of how much a noisy line can carry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noisy-channel_coding_theorem).

**Back.** That map became the floor under everything that stores or sends information, from [ZIP files and JPEGs to the fiber under the sea and the link to every phone](https://www.nokia.com/bell-labs/about/history/innovation-stories/information-theory-turns-75/), each one creeping as close to Shannon's limit as the day's coding allows. The math ran in Bell Labs' own journal for any engineer to copy, so it could not be fenced. Shannon won every prize a field can give, the [National Medal of Science and the Kyoto Prize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claude_Shannon). The money settled instead with the firms that build the modems, the optical gear, and the phone chips chasing his limit, each new design measured by how close to Shannon's number it has crept.
