*Model · Murray Hill, New Jersey · 1940s*
**Claude Shannon** · a mathematical theory of communication

**Front.** In 1948 a young mathematician at Bell Labs named [Claude Shannon](https://spectrum.ieee.org/claude-shannon-tinkerer-prankster-and-father-of-information-theory) was working on an old, stubborn problem. Every wire and every radio link adds a hiss of noise that garbles the message, and for a hundred years engineers had only two answers: shout louder, or send slower. They were sure a noisy line could never carry a message perfectly. Shannon threw out the meaning of the message and counted only the choices in it, measuring each one in a new unit: the [bit, a single yes-or-no](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bit) his colleague John Tukey had named the year before. [In one paper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A_Mathematical_Theory_of_Communication) he proved that every channel has an exact speed limit and that below it you can beat the noise with clever coding instead of brute force. What was new was not a thing you could hold. It was the map: the [first exact math of how much a noisy line can carry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noisy-channel_coding_theorem).

**Back.** That map became the floor under everything that stores or sends information, from [ZIP files and JPEGs to the fiber under the sea and the link to every phone](https://www.nokia.com/bell-labs/about/history/innovation-stories/information-theory-turns-75/), each one creeping as close to Shannon's limit as the day's coding allows. The math was published in Bell Labs' own journal, free for anyone to read, so it could not be fenced. Shannon won every prize a field can give, the [National Medal of Science and the Kyoto Prize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claude_Shannon), while the lasting lead slid downstream to the people who could build the chips and the gear that race against his limit. The model gave the world its compass and kept no ground of its own.
