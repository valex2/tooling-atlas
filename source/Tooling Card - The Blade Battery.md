*Manufacture · Shenzhen, China · 2020s*
**BYD (Wang Chuanfu)** · long LFP cells, no module

**Front.** In [March 2020](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BYD_Blade_battery), BYD, a firm in Shenzhen that built cheap phone batteries before it built cars, called reporters in to watch a steel nail get driven through a car battery. The chemistry it wanted to sell, [lithium iron phosphate](https://evreporter.com/byd-blade-battery-what-makes-it-ultra-safe/), was cheap and hard to set on fire, but it held less energy than the hot nickel cells rivals used, and its stubby block cells packed loosely and wasted room. BYD's answer was to roll the iron chemistry into flat cells [96 centimeters long](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BYD_Blade_battery), stand them on edge, and lay them side by side straight into the floor of the car with no box around each group. Each thin cell was stiff enough to help hold up the pack, so the module frame could go and about [50% more cells](https://medium.com/batterybits/the-next-generation-battery-pack-design-from-the-byd-blade-cell-to-module-free-battery-pack-2b507d4746d1) fit in the same space.

**Back.** The nail went through and the blade cell sat at just [30 to 60°C](https://evreporter.com/byd-blade-battery-what-makes-it-ultra-safe/) with no smoke or fire, while a nickel cell in the same test shot past 500°C and burned. The gain was less about the chemistry, which was old, than about the shape: packing the cells as their own beams clawed back the room that iron chemistry gives up, so a safe, cheap pack no longer meant a short-range car. Tesla and others soon switched their standard-range cars back to iron cells, and China, which kept betting on this chemistry while the West chased nickel, now makes [more than three-quarters](https://www.iea.org/commentaries/the-battery-industry-has-entered-a-new-phase) of the world's batteries.
