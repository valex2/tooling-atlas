*Manufacture · Norberg, Sweden · 1150s*
**The Bergslagen ironmasters of Lapphyttan** · tall furnace, water-driven blast, melted iron

**Front.** For most of history a smith got iron by heating ore in a low hearth, a bloomery, until it formed a spongy clot. He then hammered it for hours to drive out the dirt. The metal never melted, because pure iron only goes liquid near [1,538°C](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron) and a hand-pumped fire could not hold that heat. The ironmasters working the ore hills of [Bergslagen in central Sweden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lapphyttan), around [AD 1150](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S2352409X1530170X), built something taller: a stone stack several meters high, packed with charcoal and ore, with leather bellows driven by a water wheel forcing a hard, steady blast of air through the bottom. The fast air made the charcoal burn far hotter. As the gas climbed it fed carbon into the iron, which dropped the melting point enough that the metal ran out the base as a glowing liquid. They had stopped making a lump to hammer and started pouring iron.

**Back.** The liquid iron, cast into rough bars, was carbon-rich and brittle, so the Swedes reheated and stirred it in a second hearth to burn the carbon back out and make workable iron for trade. China had run blast furnaces on the same principle [centuries earlier, by the Han period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_metallurgy_in_China), but in Europe the Lapphyttan stack is among the [oldest known](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blast_furnace), and the design spread west and south through the next two centuries until iron stopped being scarce. Once iron came by the ton, the way was open to cheap bulk steel and the rails, beams, and machines built from it. That is why this furnace heads the thread.

*Curator's note:* **Placement.** Manufacture, Goal Both, Convergence, Confidence Plausible — it makes iron at scale, and the lead pools where the heat, the water, the ore, and the bellows craft all come together in one stack. **Analysis.** Head of the Iron & Steel thread and the deepest Manufacture card in the deck: every later metal-shaping tool assumes iron is already a bulk material, which this furnace is what made true. Parade card, not a hero portrait; no single named inventor, so it carries the "tool, not person" weight by standing for a whole region's practice. **Nuance.** No inventor and no one date: China had cast iron from roughly the [5th century BCE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blast_furnace) and blast furnaces by the Han era, so Europe's version is an independent or transmitted re-creation, not a first. Lapphyttan in Norberg is chosen as the representative European site because it is radiocarbon-dated and well excavated (coordinates 60.0757N, 16.0708E); the ~1150 date is from charcoal and carries the usual old-wood uncertainty. Year is set to 1400 to mark the mature, spreading European furnace rather than the single Swedish stack.
