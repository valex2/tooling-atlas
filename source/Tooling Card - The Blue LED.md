*Make · Anan, Japan · 1990s*
**Shuji Nakamura** · a chip that glows blue

**Front.** A diode that gives off light was old news by the 1960s. [Nick Holonyak built the first red one at General Electric in 1962](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Nick-Holonyak-Jr), and green and yellow followed in the 1970s. But a bright blue diode would not come, and without blue you cannot mix white light or paint a full-color screen, so the problem sat unsolved for thirty years. The right stuff to try was [gallium nitride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shuji_Nakamura), a hard crystal that should glow blue, but no one could grow it clean. At a [small chemical company in Anan called Nichia](https://www.invent.org/inductees/shuji-nakamura), an engineer named Shuji Nakamura spent years rebuilding the oven that lays the crystal down. He [reworked the gas-flow reactor so two streams met the hot sapphire at once](https://grokipedia.com/page/Shuji_Nakamura), which laid down far fewer cracks and flaws. Then he cracked the second wall: to make the crystal carry current the right way you dope it with magnesium, but the gas left hydrogen behind that choked the dopant, so Nakamura [baked the finished crystal in clean nitrogen near 700°C to drive the hydrogen out](https://grokipedia.com/page/Shuji_Nakamura) and got working p-type gallium nitride. By [1993 Nichia was selling a blue diode many times brighter than anything before](https://www.invent.org/inductees/shuji-nakamura). The new thing was not light from a diode. It was the hands-on craft of growing the crystal and making it carry current.

**Back.** Coat that blue chip with a yellow [phosphor](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/physics/2014/nakamura/facts/) and the mix reads as white, so the blue diode opened the way to the white lamp on the wall and the screen in your pocket. A white LED [runs near 100,000 hours and turns far more of its power into light than the old bulb that gave back about 16 lumens per watt](https://www.azom.com/article.aspx?ArticleID=11451), and lighting eats [roughly a fifth to a third of the world's electricity](https://www.azom.com/article.aspx?ArticleID=11451), so the saving is large. Akasaki, Amano, and Nakamura shared the [2014 Nobel Prize in Physics](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/physics/2014/nakamura/facts/) for it. The lead settled with the firms that had the growth recipe in hand, not with the idea on the page; the recipe was tacit, learned at the reactor, and slow to copy.

*Curator's note:* **Placement.** Make cell, Anan, 1993, the Japan beat in the optoelectronics arc. Serves Both goals: the white lamp is a mass good, the bright blue chip a strategic one (displays, signage, lasers). Mechanism: Convergence, the red/green lineage and the GaN crystal craft meeting in one device that finally closes white. Confidence: Direct, the names, the 1993 date, the two-flow reactor, the nitrogen-anneal p-type fix, and the Nobel are all primary or institution-sourced. **Analysis.** This is the inverse device to [[Tooling Card - The Silicon Solar Cell]]: the solar cell turns light into electricity, the blue LED turns electricity into light, and both rest on the same doped-junction craft that [[Tooling Card - The Transistor]] established, which is why the BuildsOn points there. The moat is the same shape the deck keeps finding, the un-copyable process held by a few, echoing [[Tooling Card - The EUV Machine]] and the fermentation cards: the diode design is public, the way to grow the crystal is not. **Nuance.** Credit splits cleanly: [Akasaki and Amano at Nagoya did the late-1980s groundwork](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/andp.201500803), high-quality GaN and the first p-type by electron-beam treatment and a 1989 p-n junction; Nakamura at Nichia made the bright, manufacturable blue LED and the simpler thermal route to p-type. Sources scatter the "bright blue" date across 1992–1996 depending on which device they count; I use 1993 for Nichia's high-brightness product and flag the spread. The lifetime and lumen figures vary by source and improve every year, so they are order-of-magnitude. The [Nichia lawsuit](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Shuji-Nakamura) is the vivid proof of how much the process was worth: Nakamura's original bonus was about 20,000 yen, a Tokyo court first valued his share near 20 billion yen, and the parties settled in 2005 for [840 million yen, about $8.1 million](https://patentbaristas.com/archives/2005/01/14/ex-employee-settles-patent-dispute-with-japanese-company-for-81-million/), then the largest such payout in Japan.
