*Manufacture · Germany · 1990s*
**Robert Bosch GmbH (Laermer & Schilp)** · etch-and-coat deep silicon carving

**Front.** In 1992, a physicist named [Franz Laermer](https://ethw.org/Franz_Laermer) and a process engineer named Andrea Schilp were working in the research labs of Robert Bosch, the car-parts company near Stuttgart, on a sensor small enough and cheap enough to fire a car's airbag. The sensor was a tiny moving part carved out of silicon, and carving was the problem: the standard trick, soaking the wafer in hot lye, could only cut along the crystal's own slanted planes, shallow and wide. Laermer and Schilp put the wafer in a plasma chamber and made the gases take turns, a few seconds of a [fluorine gas that eats silicon](https://www.samcointl.com/news-events/tutorials/what-is-the-bosch-process/) straight down, then a few seconds of a gas that lines the fresh hole with a Teflon-like coat, over and over, hundreds of rounds. Ions raining straight down knock the coat off the floor each round but spare the walls, so the hole keeps its sides dressed and only deepens — a way of carving that ignores the crystal and follows the mask, [patented in 1994](https://patents.google.com/patent/US5501893A/en).

**Back.** Bosch licensed the recipe to the firms that build etching machines, [Surface Technology Systems in Wales and Alcatel in France](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/ppap.201800207), so within a few years any fab could buy a chamber with the trick built in, and to this day everyone calls it the Bosch process. Deep straight walls opened the way to mass-producing the tiny silicon machines called MEMS: [airbag sensors in 1996](https://www.bosch.com/stories/mems-25-years/), then the motion sensors in every phone. Bosch kept the product lead it had etched for itself; its Reutlingen fab has turned out more than [15 billion sensors since 1995](https://www.bosch.com/stories/mems-25-years/), about [four million a day](https://www.businesswire.com/news/home/20181214005005/en/Christmas-goes-digital-why-mini-Bosch-components-will-feature-in-so-many-gifts-this-Christmas), and more than half the smartphones on Earth carry at least one.
