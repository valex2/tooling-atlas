*Measure · Strasbourg, Germany · 1890s*
**Ferdinand Braun** · electron-beam tube that draws currents

**Front.** In [1897](https://www.crtsite.com/Annalen-der-Physik.html), [Ferdinand Braun](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/physics/1909/braun/biographical/), a physics professor in Strasbourg, wanted to watch an alternating current change, and no meter could show him: the current flipped direction faster than any needle could swing. So he had a long glass tube pumped empty, fired electrons off a cold metal plate at one end, and squeezed them through a [hole two millimetres wide](https://www.crtsite.com/page3.html) so a thin beam flew down the tube and lit a spot on a mica screen painted with glow-paint. He fed the current he wanted to study into a coil at the tube's neck; the beam bent as the current changed, the spot danced, and a spinning mirror in front of the screen spread the dance into a curve of light. An electron beam weighs next to nothing, so the spot could follow wiggles no metal pointer ever could, and the current drew its own picture.

**Back.** Braun printed the design in [Annalen der Physik](https://www.crtsite.com/Annalen-der-Physik.html) that February, and his assistant [Jonathan Zenneck](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1002/phbl.19980541117) added a circuit in 1899 that swept the spot sideways at a steady rate, turning the tube into the oscilloscope, the instrument on every electrical workbench of the next century. In 1907 [Boris Rosing](https://ethw.org/Milestones:Development_of_Electronic_Television,_1924-1941) in St. Petersburg put the first crude picture on one, and on [Christmas Day 1926](https://www.titech.ac.jp/english/public-relations/about/stories/kenjiro-takayanagi) Kenjiro Takayanagi in Hamamatsu made a Braun tube show a single Japanese letter, the start of electronic television. Germany published the tube and lost it: the picture tubes in the world's living rooms came from RCA in America and then from Japan, where the everyday word for one is still [buraun-kan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cathode-ray_tube), Braun's tube.
