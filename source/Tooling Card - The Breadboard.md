*Make · Derby, USA · 1970s*
**Ronald Portugal** · push-in board, no solder

**Front.** [Ronald Portugal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Breadboard), an engineer at E&L Instruments, was tired of how slow it was to test a circuit. To try an idea you soldered each part down, and to change it you unsoldered and started over. In 1971 he made a small plastic block full of spring-clip holes set [one tenth of an inch apart, the same spacing as the legs of a computer chip](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Breadboard). You pushed a wire or a part into a hole and it gripped and connected, no solder and no heat. Now a working circuit could be built, changed, and rebuilt in minutes by hand.

**Back.** The solderless [breadboard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Breadboard) became the first place almost every electronic circuit gets tried, on the benches of engineers, in school labs, and in garages. It made the step from an idea to a circuit you can poke at almost free. Half a century on it is still made and used the same way, the same plastic block with the same tenth-inch holes.
