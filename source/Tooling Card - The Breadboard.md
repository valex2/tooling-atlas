*Make · Derby, USA · 1970s*
**Ronald Portugal** · push-in board, no solder

**Front.** In 1971 an engineer named [Ronald Portugal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Breadboard), working at E&L Instruments, was tired of how slow it was to test a circuit. To try an idea you soldered each part down, and to change it you unsoldered and started over. He made a small plastic block full of spring-clip holes set [one tenth of an inch apart, the same spacing as the legs of a computer chip](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Breadboard). You pushed a wire or a part into a hole and it gripped and connected, no solder and no heat. Now you could build a working circuit, change it, and rebuild it in minutes with your hands.

**Back.** The solderless [breadboard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Breadboard) became the first place almost every electronic circuit gets tried, on the benches of engineers, in school labs, and in garages. It made the step from an idea to a circuit you can poke at almost free, and that cheap first step is where a lot of invention starts. It is still made and used the same way half a century on, a small tool that quietly stayed.

Analysis: this is the simple, human end of [[Thread - Prototyping & CAD]] and the sibling to [[Tooling Card - The FPGA]]: the breadboard keeps the first physical try cheap, the FPGA keeps the first digital-logic try cheap, and both feed the same point, that a country which makes prototyping fast keeps more of its inventing at home. It earns its place as the plainest example of "lower the cost of the first attempt" in the deck. Not a moat card; it confers no lasting lead by itself, which is why it pairs upward to the FPGA where the prototyping advantage did harden into an industry.
