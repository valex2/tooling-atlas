*Measure · Hayes, England · 1970s*
**Godfrey Hounsfield** · spin an X-ray around the body, rebuild a slice

**Front.** In 1971 Godfrey Hounsfield was an engineer at [EMI](https://www.smithsonianmag.com/innovation/fifty-years-ago-the-first-ct-scan-let-doctors-see-inside-a-living-skull-180978792/), the British firm that recorded the Beatles. A plain X-ray squashes the whole body onto one sheet, so a deep tumor hides behind the bone and tissue in front of it. His machine slowly turned an [X-ray tube and a detector](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/medicine/1979/press-release/) in a ring around the head and read how much the beam dimmed from each angle. A computer then took those hundreds of angle-readings and [solved backward](https://www.acr.org/News-and-Publications/Nobel-Prize-for-Computed-Tomography) for the density at every point, drawing a single flat slice through the skull. The first patient scan, on [1 October 1971](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11892408/), showed a dark cyst in a woman's brain on a picture only 80 dots across.

**Back.** The math for turning many angled shadows into a clean picture was the hard part, and the same kind of digital image work was being built through the 1960s at [NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory](https://www.spacefoundation.org/space_technology_hal/digital-image-processing-medical-applications/), where Robert Nathan wrote the first working software to clean up the smeared photos coming back from the Ranger and Mariner probes; histories of that work list CT and MRI among the medical tools that grew from it. Hounsfield and the physicist [Allan Cormack](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/medicine/1979/press-release/), who had worked out the reconstruction math years earlier, shared the 1979 Nobel Prize in Medicine. The idea of building an image from many angled readings carried straight into [MRI](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/medicine/2003/summary/), where Paul Lauterbur and Peter Mansfield used magnetic fields instead of X-rays, and the slice became the standard way doctors look inside a living body.
