*Model · Cambridge, England · 1680s*
**Isaac Newton & Gottfried Leibniz** · method of rates and areas

**Front.** In October 1684, a Leipzig journal ran [six crowded pages](https://www.historyofinformation.com/detail.php?id=1723) by [Gottfried Leibniz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova_Methodus_pro_Maximis_et_Minimis) that laid out a general way to find the slope of any curve and the area under it, using small marks he wrote as dx and ∫. People before him could crack one such problem at a time, each by its own trick, but no one had a single recipe that worked for all of them. Leibniz gave the recipe, and the marks that made it easy to write down. Eighteen years earlier, and unknown to him, [Isaac Newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Method_of_Fluxions) had built the same method in a private notebook and called a rate of change a "fluxion," but he sat on it until [1687](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophi%C3%A6_Naturalis_Principia_Mathematica).

**Back.** With one general method, motion and change became equations a person could actually solve. The Bernoulli family in Basel and then [Leonhard Euler](https://mathshistory.st-andrews.ac.uk/Biographies/Euler/) took Leibniz's clean marks and built most of modern analysis on top of them, while English mathematicians stayed loyal to Newton's clumsier dots and [fell behind for a century](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leibniz%E2%80%93Newton_calculus_controversy). The notation is what survived: every model that predicts how a fluid flows or a rocket climbs is still written in Leibniz's dx and ∫.
