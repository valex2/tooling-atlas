*Manufacture · Oak Ridge, Tennessee, USA · 1940s*
**Ernest Lawrence** · bend a uranium beam to sort isotopes

**Front.** In 1943 a new plant called [Y-12](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Y-12_National_Security_Complex) rose in the Tennessee hills around a machine named for Lawrence's Berkeley lab, the **CAL**ifornia **U**niversity cyclo**TRON**. It did one plain thing: heat uranium to a charged gas, fire the ions in a beam, and bend that beam through a huge magnet so the lighter U-235 swung a hair tighter than the heavier U-238 and landed in its own collector, scraped out by hand. Aston had done this on a benchtop in 1919 to weigh single atoms; here the same trick ran in oval "racetracks" the size of a factory, the dials tended by some [10,000 young women](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calutron_Girls) who were told nothing of what they made. Wartime copper was scarce, so the magnet coils were wound from [14,700 tons of U.S. Treasury silver](https://www.americanscientist.org/article/from-treasury-vault-to-the-manhattan-project), about 395 million troy ounces borrowed under armed guard.

**Back.** Over two years the calutrons delivered most of the [64 kilograms of U-235](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Y-12_National_Security_Complex) that went into the Hiroshima bomb, the late batches topped up by enriched feed from the gaseous-diffusion plant next door. The method was open physics, printed in journals since the 1920s, so what the guards protected was not how it worked but that it worked at this scale and how much metal it took; the silver came home with [less than 0.04% lost](https://www.americanscientist.org/article/from-treasury-vault-to-the-manhattan-project). Cheaper diffusion and centrifuges soon made the calutron obsolete, so the U.S. declassified it, and in the 1980s Iraq used that public design to build [hundreds of its own calutrons](https://nuclearweaponarchive.org/Iraq/Calutron.html) toward a bomb, found only by inspectors after the 1991 Gulf War.

*Curator's note:* **Placement.** Manufacture cell, Goal 1, Convergence, Direct confidence: a measuring instrument grown into a production plant. **Analysis.** The clean Measure-becomes-Manufacture hand-off in the deck. Aston's [[Tooling Card - The Mass Spectrograph]] weighed atoms one at a time as a balance for the unseen; the calutron is that same beam-bending physics scaled until it produces a war-ending quantity, which is why it sits beside the rival enrichment routes in [[Tooling Card - The Gas Centrifuge]] and [[Tooling Card - The Solvent-Extraction Cascade]]. **Nuance.** Y-12 did not work alone in 1945; K-25's diffusion plant pre-enriched the feed, so I say "most of" the U-235, not all. The silver figure is firm (14,700 tons / ~395M oz, returned nearly intact). The Iraq revival is the card's spine: a written method cannot be locked up, only the tacit scale-craft can, and once that craft was declassified as outdated the door stood open. **Secrecy:** held briefly then obsolete — the open mass-spec method was never secret, only the proof that it scaled and the silver it ate; declassified as outdated, the public design was copied wholesale by Iraq decades later.
