*Model · Cambridge, UK · 1960s*
**Olga Kennard** · one file of every crystal structure

**Front.** In [1965](https://www.ccdc.cam.ac.uk/discover/news/celebrating-dr-olga-kennard-1924-2023), a crystallographer in Cambridge named [Olga Kennard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olga_Kennard) set out to gather something no one had gathered before. Every year chemists worked out the shapes of hundreds of small molecules, atom by atom, from how the crystals bent X-rays, but each result sat alone in its own paper with no way to line it up against the rest. Kennard and a young helper named [David Watson](https://www.iucr.org/news/newsletter/volume-31/number-1/olga-kennard-obe-frs-19242023) began copying the numbers out of each paper onto [edge-punched cards](https://journals.iucr.org/b/issues/2016/02/00/bm5086/index.html), then onto magnetic tape, writing down where every atom sat in a form a machine could read. For the first time all those structures lived in [one file](https://www.iucr.org/news/newsletter/volume-13/number-1/cambridge-data-centre) that a computer could search at once.

**Back.** At first the file went out as fat reels of magnetic tape and a run of printed books called [Molecular Structures and Dimensions](https://journals.iucr.org/b/issues/2016/02/00/bm5086/index.html). But once a chemist could ask a computer to pull every molecule with a certain bond or ring, people started seeing [patterns across thousands of structures](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC11378158/) that no single experiment could ever show. The file never stopped filling; it now holds more than [1.2 million](https://www.ccdc.cam.ac.uk/discover/news/celebrating-dr-olga-kennard-1924-2023) structures, and it showed the way for the shared data banks that came after it, from [protein shapes](https://www.rcsb.org/news/640a3e37e0a2cc03bf31545b) to the gene libraries of the 1980s.
