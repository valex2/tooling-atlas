*Make · Birmingham, England · 1940s*
**John Randall and Harry Boot** · a palm-sized copper block that makes microwaves

**Front.** On [21 February 1940](https://www.historic-uk.com/HistoryUK/HistoryofBritain/Cavity-Magnetron/), two young physicists at the University of Birmingham, [John Randall and Harry Boot](https://spectrum.ieee.org/magnetron), were trying to do something the textbooks said was very hard: make radio waves short enough to bounce off a plane and aim them with a small dish. Working under [Mark Oliphant](https://spectrum.ieee.org/magnetron), they took a solid lump of copper, drilled a ring of round holes through it, and ran a hot wire down the middle. Switch it on, and electrons spun past the holes and set them singing, the way blowing across a bottle makes a note. The first one they fired gave [waves about 10 centimetres long at hundreds of watts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cavity_magnetron), far more power at that tiny wavelength than anyone had wrung from short waves before, out of a block you could [hold in your hand](https://www.historic-uk.com/HistoryUK/HistoryofBritain/Cavity-Magnetron/). What was new was not the radio wave. It was the little copper block that poured it out.

**Back.** Short, strong waves meant radar small enough to ride in a plane or a ship and sharp enough to spot a surfaced U-boat in the dark, which helped turn the [Battle of the Atlantic](https://blog.oup.com/2019/07/how-microwaves-changed-the-course-of-the-battle-of-the-atlantic/). Britain made the thing but could not build it fast enough, so in September 1940 the [Tizard Mission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tizard_Mission) carried one across the ocean and handed it to the Americans, who set up the [MIT Radiation Laboratory](https://news.mit.edu/2015/how-tizard-mission-paved-way-for-MIT-research-1123) and had [Bell Labs](https://spectrum.ieee.org/magnetron) turn out over a million by war's end. One American historian called it [the most valuable cargo ever brought to our shores](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tizard_Mission). The lead did not stay in Birmingham; it crossed the water to whoever could make the thing by the million.

Parade, not hero: the migration is real but the back leans on a handoff and a quote more than on a single physical moment. **Nuance.** The 21 February 1940 first-run date is from Historic UK and the magnetron histories; some sources date the first powered test a day or two on either side, and the very first figures are usually given as "hundreds of watts at ~9.8–10 cm," rising to ~1 kW within a week and 10–25 kW within months, so the front's "by the kilowatt" is the near-term figure, not the 21 February number. The unit carried by the Tizard Mission was not the Birmingham original but an improved GEC build (E1189, by E.C.S. Megaw), a detail too fiddly for the face. "Over a million" Bell magnetrons and the "most valuable cargo" line trace to James Phinney Baxter's *Scientists Against Time* (1946); Bell's first contracted run was 30 units in October 1940. Mechanism strain for the framework: the live force here is *the lead follows the manufacturing*, the same migration shape as the gauge card, not true convergence into an unbuyable moat. Convergence is the closest of the three labels but it does not fit cleanly; this is a second card reaching for a migration mechanism the model doesn't name, worth tracking as standing drift rather than a one-off.
