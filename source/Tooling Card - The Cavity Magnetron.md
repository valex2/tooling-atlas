*Make · Birmingham, England · 1940s*
**John Randall and Harry Boot** · a palm-sized copper block that makes microwaves

**Front.** On [21 February 1940](https://www.historic-uk.com/HistoryUK/HistoryofBritain/Cavity-Magnetron/), two young physicists at the University of Birmingham, [John Randall and Harry Boot](https://spectrum.ieee.org/magnetron), were trying to do something the textbooks said was very hard: make radio waves short enough to bounce off a plane and aim them with a small dish. Working under [Mark Oliphant](https://spectrum.ieee.org/magnetron), they took a solid lump of copper, drilled a ring of round holes through it, and ran a hot wire down the middle. Switch it on, and electrons spun past the holes and set them singing, the way blowing across a bottle makes a note. The first one they fired gave [waves about 10 centimetres long at hundreds of watts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cavity_magnetron), far more power at that tiny wavelength than anyone had wrung from short waves before, out of a block you could [hold in your hand](https://www.historic-uk.com/HistoryUK/HistoryofBritain/Cavity-Magnetron/). The wonder was not the radio wave but the little copper block that poured it out.

**Back.** Short, strong waves meant radar small enough to ride in a plane or a ship and sharp enough to spot a surfaced U-boat in the dark, which helped turn the [Battle of the Atlantic](https://blog.oup.com/2019/07/how-microwaves-changed-the-course-of-the-battle-of-the-atlantic/). Britain made the thing but could not build it fast enough, so in September 1940 the [Tizard Mission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tizard_Mission) carried one across the ocean and handed it to the Americans, who set up the [MIT Radiation Laboratory](https://news.mit.edu/2015/how-tizard-mission-paved-way-for-MIT-research-1123) and had [Bell Labs](https://spectrum.ieee.org/magnetron) turn out over a million by war's end. One American historian called it [the most valuable cargo ever brought to our shores](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tizard_Mission). Birmingham invented it; Bell Labs turned out over a million, and the radar lead crossed the Atlantic with them.
