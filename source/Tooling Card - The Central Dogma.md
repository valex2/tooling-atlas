*Model · Cambridge, England · 1950s*
**Francis Crick** · life as a code

**Front.** In 1953 [Francis Crick and James Watson worked out the shape of DNA](https://profiles.nlm.nih.gov/spotlight/sc/feature/doublehelix), the long molecule a cell uses to pass its traits to its young. It was a twisted ladder spelled in four chemical letters. The shape did not say what the letters were *for*, or which way the message ran. A few years later Crick stood up at a London meeting and gave the rule. [In a 1957 lecture, printed the next year](https://journals.plos.org/plosbiology/article?id=10.1371/journal.pbio.2003243), he said the message in a cell only flows one way: DNA holds the master copy, RNA carries a working copy out, and a protein gets built from that. [Once the message reaches the protein it can never travel back](https://www.dna.caltech.edu/courses/cs191/paperscs191/CrickCentralDogma1970.pdf). What was new was not a thing in his hand. It was a way of seeing — stop fighting the cell's chemistry and treat the gene as plain text, a tape of letters you can read.

**Back.** Once the gene was just text, you could build tools to handle it without touching the chemistry, and within twenty years biologists had three: a way to [read the tape](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC431765/), a way to [copy it](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1523369/), and a way to [cut and paste it](https://www.gene.com/about-us/leadership/our-founders). Crick printed the rule and patented nothing, because an idea, once written, belongs to everyone. The money never sat with the idea. It went to the people who built the machines that read and copy and cut, the cutting trick alone covering [more than $35 billion of products](https://ipmall.law.unh.edu/sites/default/files/hosted_resources/IP_handbook/ch17/ipHandbook-Ch%2017%2022%20Feldman-Colaianni0Liu%20Cohen-Boyer%20Patents%20and%20Licenses.pdf). Crick won every prize there is, including a [Nobel](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/medicine/1962/summary/), and held no ground of his own. The rule he set is the floor every later tool still stands on, the way a computer's whole stack rests on one set of instructions underneath.

Hero, because it carries the Model argument for the whole biology thread on its own. **Nuance.** The dogma was stated in Crick's lecture to the Society for Experimental Biology on 19 September 1957 and printed in 1958 as "On Protein Synthesis"; the face rounds to "a 1957 lecture, printed the next year." The popular line that the 1970 discovery of reverse transcriptase (RNA copied back to DNA) "overturned" the dogma is a misreading Crick corrected the same year in Nature: the rule only ever forbade flow *out of* a protein, never RNA-to-DNA, so reverse transcription fit it cleanly. That beat is kept off the face. The computer parallel (a one-way set of instructions underneath everything) is the long-form note's analogy, not Crick's own words, used here only to picture the abstraction. The $35 billion is the Cohen-Boyer licensing total; "first biotech company" (Genentech, built on that cutting trick) is contestable and left off the face. Crick shares the 1962 Nobel with Watson and Wilkins, for the helix, not the dogma; the face says only "a Nobel."
