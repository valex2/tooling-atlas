*Manufacture · Whitehall, Michigan, USA · 1950s*
**Theodore Operhall and the Misco foundry team** · a mold built by dipping wax in slurry and grit

**Front.** Goldsmiths had been melting wax out of clay molds for four thousand years, and in 1907 a Chicago dentist named [William Taggart](https://americanhistory.si.edu/collections/object/nmah_725507) turned the trick into a bench process for casting gold fillings. From late 1941 [Austenal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vitallium), a firm that cast false teeth out of a cobalt-chrome alloy, was casting turbosupercharger blades the same way. The method still worked as it always had: bury the wax in a steel flask packed with a plaster-like investment, bake the flask overnight, pour, and hope. At Misco Precision Casting, a [26-person shop in Whitehall, Michigan](https://www.developmuskegon.org/case-study-industries/case-study-howmet-aerospace/), the men were losing [forty to fifty of every hundred castings](https://patents.google.com/patent/US2961751A/en) to flaws they could not see until the metal was already in. In January 1958 [Theodore Operhall, Charles Schwartz and Dickson Van Schoik](https://patents.google.com/patent/US2961751A/en) filed on a mold with no flask at all. They dipped a tree of wax parts into a slurry of watery silica and ground zircon, lifted it dripping, and rained coarse alumina grit onto the wet skin. Dry it, dip it, stucco it again, five to ten times over, until a crust a quarter inch thick stood around the wax. The last coats carried a trace of feldspar, which softens in the fire and closes the shell's own cracks. Then the wax ran out and the shell went into the furnace on its own.

**Back.** Scrap fell to a few percent, because a shell that stands by itself can be looked over before anything is poured into it. Firing that had taken sixteen hours took minutes, and the ceramic in a cluster cost [90 cents instead of $2.50](https://patents.google.com/patent/US2961751A/en). Howe Sound bought Austenal in 1958 and Misco the year after, renamed itself [Howmet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Howmet_Aerospace) in 1965, and sold the shell into jet engines. Every hot part in a modern engine is still poured into a dipped shell, and the airfoil and its hollow cooling passages come out at size, so the machinist grinds a few faces instead of carving a blade from a solid billet. Howmet put [$92 million](https://www.developmuskegon.org/case-study-industries/case-study-howmet-aerospace/) into the same Whitehall site in 2018 for fifteen more casting furnaces and 22 percent more blades.
