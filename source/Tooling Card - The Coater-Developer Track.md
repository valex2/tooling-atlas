*Manufacture · Tokyo, Japan · 1980s*
**Tokyo Electron (TEL)** · sealed robot-arm resist track

**Front.** In 1988, engineers at [Tokyo Electron](https://www.tel.com/about/milestones/index.html), a Tokyo company that had spent its first years importing other makers' machines, took on the least glamorous step in chip-making. Before a stepper can print anything, the wafer must be spun with photoresist, the light-sensitive goo that catches the pattern, and the goo was trouble: since the 1970s wafers had ridden [belt conveyors](https://www.chiphistory.org/272-tel-clean-track-mark-ii) through the coating and baking stations, and the belts jammed and shed dirt onto the wet film. So they threw the belt out. They stacked the spin cups and hotplates into towers and set a robot arm in the middle to lift each wafer from chamber to chamber inside one sealed cabinet, the [CLEAN TRACK Mark V](https://www.shmj.or.jp/english/pdf/em/exhibi2455E.pdf); the whole messy resist line was now a single clean machine that bolts to the stepper's side.

**Back.** When [chemically amplified resists](https://www.shmj.or.jp/english/pdf/em/exhibi2455E.pdf) arrived in the 1990s, resists so touchy that a bake run a little hot spoils the pattern, only the sealed track could hold them steady, and the belt machines vanished. TEL shipped its [thousandth](https://www.tel.com/about/milestones/index.html) CLEAN TRACK ACT 8 in 2000 and today holds about [90 percent](https://newsletter.semianalysis.com/p/lam-research-tokyo-electron-jsr-battle) of the world's track market, and nearly all of the tracks feeding EUV scanners. Nikon lost the stepper war to ASML, but when ASML stood up its first High-NA EUV scanner at imec [in 2023](https://aei.dempa.net/archives/1131), the machine bolted to its side was a TEL track.
