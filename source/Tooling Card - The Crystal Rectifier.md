*Make · United States (Purdue, Bell, DuPont) · 1940s*
**the wartime crystal-rectifier program** · pure silicon and germanium crystals

**Front.** In 1942 the war needed radar small enough to ride on a plane, and to send a tight beam you need very short radio waves, [only a few centimeters long](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystal_detector). The hot glass tubes that caught ordinary radio were [too slow to catch waves that fast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystal_detector), so the radar could not hear its own echo. The fix was an old trick from the early days of radio: a thin metal whisker touching a [tiny chip of crystal](https://www.computerhistory.org/siliconengine/semiconductor-diode-rectifiers-serve-in-ww-ii/), which could follow the fast wave where a tube could not. But a plain crystal is full of dirt, so it hissed and behaved differently every time. The new thing was learning to make the crystal [pure](https://www.physics.purdue.edu/about/history/war_period.html). At [Purdue, under Karl Lark-Horovitz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Lark-Horovitz), they cleaned germanium until it behaved the same way every time, and DuPont pushed silicon to [99.999 percent pure](https://www.computerhistory.org/siliconengine/semiconductor-diode-rectifiers-serve-in-ww-ii/) by the end of the war.

**Back.** When the war ended and the work [came out of secrecy](https://www.physics.purdue.edu/about/history/war_period.html), the clean crystal it had perfected was waiting. In 1947 [Walter Brattain built the first transistor](https://www.computerhistory.org/siliconengine/semiconductor-diode-rectifiers-serve-in-ww-ii/) on a slab of high-purity germanium, the very kind of clean crystal this wartime work had taught the country to grow. The skill of making a crystal pure, forced by the need to hear a faint echo, became the ground the whole chip age stands on, picked up by Bell Labs and then by every chipmaker after. The craft only grew finer: the silicon in a chip today is so clean that one stray atom in it is like [a single grain of salt in a railroad car of sugar](https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/semiconductors/chpt-2/electrons-and-holes/). It did not die with the war, because once you know how to clean a crystal, you can keep doing it.

It pairs against [[Tooling Card - The Cavity Magnetron]] (the cavity magnetron the British carried to America in 1940): two halves of the same radar set, the transmitter that makes the beam and the crystal that hears it back, both forced by the same war, with the lead settling in American labs either way. Against the deck's fragility cards (Leeuwenhoek's one pair of hands) this is the opposite outcome on purpose, craft that spread and compounded instead of dying, which is why the back lands on "a thing you can keep making." It is a clean Convergence example but a feeder, not the capstone, so it should defer to [[Tooling Card - The EUV Machine]] as the deck's heaviest convergence case. **Nuance.** The home-doc write-up #5 covers the *post-war* purification leap (DuPont's 1956 Brevard plant, zone refining 1951, 9N-11N silicon); this card is the *wartime* program that came before and forced it, so the 99.999 percent (5N) figure here is the wartime end-state from the Computer History Museum, not the later 9N-11N. The grain-of-salt-in-a-railroad-car-of-sugar image on the back is the All About Circuits textbook's analogy for about one impurity in ten billion atoms, today's 9N-11N chip silicon, so the back attributes it to "a chip today," not to the wartime 5N. "Millions" of rectifiers is CHM and Bell Labs' own account; an exact count is not given. The high-back-voltage germanium result (up to ~150 V, reported October 1943) and the GeO2-in-hydrogen reduction are from Purdue's history page. The card credits a program, not a person, so the front has no single bench protagonist, which is the standard strain on war-funded ecosystem cards rather than a defect. Silicon purification ran mainly at DuPont and the University of Pennsylvania; germanium ran at Purdue; the place field compresses this to "Purdue, Bell, DuPont."
