*Make · United States (Purdue, Bell, DuPont) · 1940s*
**the wartime crystal-rectifier program** · pure silicon and germanium crystals

**Front.** Radar small enough to ride on a plane needed very short radio waves, [only a few centimeters long](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystal_detector), and in 1942 the war wanted exactly that. The hot glass tubes that caught ordinary radio were [too slow to catch waves that fast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystal_detector), so the radar could not hear its own echo. The fix was an old trick from the early days of radio: a thin metal whisker touching a [tiny chip of crystal](https://www.computerhistory.org/siliconengine/semiconductor-diode-rectifiers-serve-in-ww-ii/), which could follow the fast wave where a tube could not. But a plain crystal is full of dirt, so it hissed and behaved differently every time, and the whole effort turned on [cleaning that dirt out](https://www.physics.purdue.edu/about/history/war_period.html). At [Purdue, under Karl Lark-Horovitz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Lark-Horovitz), they cleaned germanium until it behaved the same way every time, and DuPont pushed silicon to [99.999 percent pure](https://www.computerhistory.org/siliconengine/semiconductor-diode-rectifiers-serve-in-ww-ii/) by the end of the war.

**Back.** When the war ended and the work [came out of secrecy](https://www.physics.purdue.edu/about/history/war_period.html), the clean crystal it had perfected was waiting. In 1947 [Walter Brattain built the first transistor](https://www.computerhistory.org/siliconengine/semiconductor-diode-rectifiers-serve-in-ww-ii/) on a slab of high-purity germanium, the very kind of clean crystal this wartime work had taught the country to grow. The skill of making a crystal pure, forced by the need to hear a faint echo, became the ground the whole chip age stands on, picked up by Bell Labs and then by every chipmaker after. The craft only grew finer, each gain in cleanliness making the next one reachable: the silicon in a chip today is so clean that one stray atom in it is like [a single grain of salt in a railroad car of sugar](https://www.allaboutcircuits.com/textbook/semiconductors/chpt-2/electrons-and-holes/).
