*Make · Berkeley, California, USA · 1930s*
**Ernest Lawrence and M. Stanley Livingston** · spiralling particles past one repeated push

**Front.** In 1930 a graduate student at Berkeley named [M. Stanley Livingston](https://www.britannica.com/biography/M-Stanley-Livingston) needed a thesis, and his supervisor Ernest Lawrence handed him one. To break open a nucleus you needed particles carrying a million volts, and a straight accelerating tube that long sparked over before it got there. Lawrence had read [a sketch by the Norwegian engineer Rolf Widerøe](https://history.aip.org/history/exhibits/lawrence/first_text.htm) showing a particle pushed twice by the same voltage, and had seen that a magnetic field would curl the particle back for another push, and another. Livingston built the thing out of [brass, glass, red sealing wax and copper](https://collection.sciencemuseumgroup.org.uk/objects/co5324/vacuum-chamber-of-the-original-cyclotron-1931-replica-cyclotrons): a vacuum chamber four and a half inches wide holding one hollow D-shaped electrode, clamped between the poles of a magnet. On [2 January 1931 he switched it on](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclotron), fed it about 1,800 volts of radio-frequency, and read protons coming off at 80,000 electron volts, better than forty times the push he had put in. The particles spiralled outward and took a little more energy every half turn.

**Back.** Lawrence went straight to bigger magnets. On [9 January 1932 an eleven-inch machine in LeConte Hall](https://www2.lbl.gov/Science-Articles/Research-Review/Magazine/1981/81fchp2.html) delivered a billionth of an amp of protons at 1.22 MeV, the first particles above a million volts anyone had made, and eight years later Berkeley's 60-inch machine bombarded uranium with deuterons and [made the first plutonium on 14 December 1940](https://ahf.nuclearmuseum.org/ahf/history/plutonium/). Medicine came off the same magnets: Ernest's brother John gave a leukemia patient [cyclotron-made phosphorus-32 in 1936](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_H._Lawrence), the first time an isotope from the machine was used to treat a person. Other labs copied the design, some of them staffed by men sent to Berkeley to learn it, and Yoshio Nishina's team at Riken in Tokyo drew first beam from a 26-inch cyclotron on [2 April 1937](https://www.nishina.riken.jp/about/history_e.html), the first built outside the United States; American occupation troops broke Riken's machines up after the war and dumped the pieces in Tokyo Bay. The design kept compounding anyway. Roughly [1,500 cyclotrons run today](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclotron), most of them in hospital basements, baking the fluorine-18 that shows up a tumour on a PET scan.
