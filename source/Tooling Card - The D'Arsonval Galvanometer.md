*Measure · Paris, France · 1880s*
**Jacques-Arsène d'Arsonval** · a coil hung in a magnet's gap

**Front.** In 1882 a Paris doctor named [Jacques-Arsène d'Arsonval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacques-Ars%C3%A8ne_d%27Arsonval), who ran a lab studying how electricity moves through living tissue, was tired of galvanometers that would not hold still. The kind everyone used swung a little magnetized needle, and the needle twitched at every bit of iron or stray magnet nearby, so you read it by bouncing a light beam off a mirror in a dark room. Working with the engineer [Marcel Deprez](https://www.whonamedit.com/synd.cfm/1899.html), he turned the thing inside out: he bolted the magnet down and [hung a coil of fine wire](https://physics.kenyon.edu/EarlyApparatus/Electrical_Measurements/DArsonval_Galvanometer/DArsonval_Galvanometer.html) in the gap between its poles, so current through the coil twisted it against a light spring and stopped where the twist balanced the pull. He shaped the pole faces around a soft iron core to keep the field even, and now the same current swung the needle the same amount every time, along a plain evenly-spaced scale.

**Back.** The little instrument could pick up about [ten millionths of an amp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galvanometer), and because the magnet did the holding it stayed steady when you carried it across the room. In 1888 the American maker [Edward Weston](https://ethw.org/Milestones:Weston_Meters,_1887-1893) took the design, worked out iron alloys that held their magnetism for good, and sold the first accurate portable voltmeter you could just look at and trust. From there the moving coil went into nearly every ammeter and voltmeter on every panel and dashboard for the next hundred years; the swinging needle on an old radio or a car gauge is this same movement, shrunk down.
