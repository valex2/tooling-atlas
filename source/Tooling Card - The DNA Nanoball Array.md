*Measure · Mountain View, California, USA · 2000s*
**Radoje Drmanac** · balls of coiled DNA landed one per printed spot

**Front.** [Radoje Drmanac](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2924307/) had been trying to read DNA by sticking short probes to it since 1988, when the idea was turned down by three journals before *Genomics* took it. By 2009 he was running a quiet company in Mountain View and had a duller problem in front of him: the reading machines of the day grew their DNA in clumps on a glass slide, the clumps landed wherever they liked, and the empty glass between them still had to be photographed and flooded with costly chemicals. A colleague, Matt Callow, suggested letting an enzyme run in circles. So Drmanac cut a genome into short pieces, joined each piece into a small ring, and set a phi29 enzyme going round the ring hundreds of times, spooling out one long thread carrying [about 500 copies](https://grokipedia.com/page/DNA_nanoball_sequencing) of the same ring. He wrote short back-to-front stretches into the ring so the thread stuck to itself and folded up into a ball roughly 200 nanometres wide, packed with about a hundred thousand negative charges. Billions of those balls were poured over a silicon chip printed with a grid of sticky dots, and because two balls that close repel each other, [more than 90 percent of a billion dots held exactly one ball](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.1181498). Every copy inside a ball is made off the original ring, so a copying slip stays in one copy instead of doubling every round the way it does in clumps grown by PCR. Three human genomes came off the chip in the [Science paper of November 2009](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19892942/), at $4,400 of chemicals each.

**Back.** Complete Genomics sold finished genomes as a service and never sold the chip or the machine to anyone, so its best customer bought the whole company instead: [BGI paid $118 million for it in 2013](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complete_Genomics). BGI spun out [MGI in Shenzhen in 2016](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MGI_(company)) to build instruments around the array, and a pair of its big machines now claims [50,000 human genomes a year at under $100 each](https://global-mgitech.com/complete-genomics-agbt-2023-dnbseq-t20x2-g99/). When Beijing [barred imports of Illumina's sequencers on 4 March 2025](https://www.medtechdive.com/news/Illumina-China-sequencer-import-ban/741773/), Chinese labs had a homegrown machine to buy, and MGI with Berry Genomics took [59.3 percent of Chinese sequencer purchases](https://www.yicaiglobal.com/news/chinese-market-share-of-domestic-gene-sequencers-rise-in-first-half-amid-illumina-import-ban) in the first half of the year. The ban came off in November. The chip is still printed in Shenzhen.
