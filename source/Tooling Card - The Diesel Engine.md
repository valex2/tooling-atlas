*Make · Augsburg, Germany · 1890s*
**Rudolf Diesel** · oil engine lit by squeezed air

**Front.** In the winter of [1897](https://dieselnet.com/tech/diesel_history.php), an engineer named [Rudolf Diesel](https://lemelson.mit.edu/resources/rudolf-diesel) was four years into an engine that had so far run only in fits, in a test room at the Maschinenfabrik in Augsburg. The steam engines of his day threw away about nine-tenths of the heat in their coal, and Diesel had promised in print a machine that would not. He built an iron cylinder that squeezed plain air to more than thirty times its normal pressure, so hard the air grew hotter than a match flame, then blew a spray of oil into it; the oil lit itself, with no spark anywhere in the machine. On 17 February 1897 a Munich professor, [Moritz Schröter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motor_250/400), ran the formal test and measured [26 percent](https://spectrum.ieee.org/transportation/advanced-cars/the-diesel-engine-at-120) of the fuel's heat coming out as work, roughly twice what a good steam engine gave.

**Back.** For fifteen years the engine mostly stayed on land, because the only way to get oil into that hot squeezed air was to blast it in with a heavy air pump, which kept the machines big and slow. [Sulzer](https://dieselnet.com/tech/diesel_history.php) in Switzerland had bought rights as early as 1893, and in 1912 the Copenhagen yard [Burmeister & Wain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MS_Selandia) fitted diesels into the *Selandia*, the first ocean-going motor ship, which sailed from Copenhagen to Bangkok with no funnel and no coal crew. Diesel drowned in the English Channel in 1913, but the lead he planted compounded: the Augsburg firm's successor, [MAN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MAN_Energy_Solutions), took over Burmeister & Wain's engine line and still designs the giant two-stroke engines that move about half of world trade.
