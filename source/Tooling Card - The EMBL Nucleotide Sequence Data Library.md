*Model · Heidelberg, Germany · 1980s*
**EMBL (Heidelberg)** · one shared file of DNA letters

**Front.** In 1980, inside a young research lab in [Heidelberg](https://www.embl.org/news/science-technology/sparking-a-data-revolution/), a few staff set out to fix a dull, growing problem: the letters of DNA that scientists were reading off their gels were printed in hundreds of separate journal papers, so to line up two sequences you first had to track down both papers and copy the letters by hand. [Greg Hamm and his colleagues](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/3945550/) began typing those letters, the long strings of A, C, G and T, into one shared computer file, keyed straight off the printed pages and written out to reels of magnetic tape. Now the sequences no longer sat locked inside separate papers. They lived in [one file anyone could ask for a copy of](https://www.embl.org/about/history/timeline/).

**Back.** The [first public release came in April 1982](https://encyclopedia.pub/entry/36852): 568 entries, about 500,000 letters of DNA on a single reel of tape. Groups in the United States and Japan were stacking up their own piles at the same time, so the three [agreed to swap everything](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Nucleotide_Archive), and a sequence typed in one place soon showed up in all three. That shared pile never stopped growing. It became the [European Nucleotide Archive](https://www.ebi.ac.uk/about/our-story), now holding trillions of letters, and it is the open reservoir that today's protein-folding models learn from.
