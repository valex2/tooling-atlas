*Make · London, England · 1830s*
**William Fothergill Cooke and Charles Wheatstone** · coded pulses sent down a wire

**Front.** The railways were spreading across Britain in the 1830s and no one could tell a station down the line that a train was coming. [William Cooke](https://ethw.org/William_Fothergill_Cooke), a former army officer, teamed up with the King's College physicist [Charles Wheatstone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cooke_and_Wheatstone_telegraph), and in 1837 the two patented a box of magnetic needles wired into a diamond on a lettered board. A clerk pressed keys to push current down the wires, and at the far end two needles swung to point at a letter. On [25 July 1837](https://ageofrevolution.org/200-object/cooke-and-wheatstone-5-needle-telegraph-1837/) they strung a four-needle line between Euston and Camden Town in London and spelled out words over a mile of wire. Electricity was decades old; the instrument that turned a coded pulse into a letter you could read was not.

**Back.** Across the Atlantic [Samuel Morse](https://www.senate.gov/artandhistory/senate-stories/morses-telegraph-in-the-capitol.htm) cut the needles down to a single wire and a code of dots and dashes; on [24 May 1844](https://americanhistory.si.edu/collections/object/nmah_713485) he tapped "What hath God wrought" from the Capitol to Alfred Vail in Baltimore, [thirty-eight miles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baltimore%E2%80%93Washington_telegraph_line) away, in seconds. Until that line a message could go [no faster than the person carrying it](https://www.history.com/articles/telegraph); now it ran ahead of the mail by days. Wires followed the rails and then the coastlines, and when engineers tried to push a current under the sea the signal came back so weak that reading it became its own problem, the one [Lord Kelvin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mirror_galvanometer) solved with a mirror hung on a thread.
