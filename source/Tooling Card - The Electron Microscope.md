*Measure · Berlin, Germany · 1931*
**Ernst Ruska** · the electron microscope

**Front.** In 1931, two engineers at the Technical University in [Berlin](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ernst-Ruska), Max Knoll and his student Ernst Ruska, went after a microscope that used no light at all. De Broglie had just shown that electrons travel as [waves far shorter than light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter_wave), so a beam of them ought to see far finer, if you could focus it the way glass focuses a sunbeam. Ruska found the trick: a coil of wire carrying a current [bends a beam of electrons just as a lens bends light](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ernst-Ruska), and on [9 March 1931](http://scihi.org/ernst-ruska-electron-microscope/) he and Knoll stacked two such coils and caught a focused picture made of electrons. It was a lens with no glass in it, and their first machine magnified the specimen only [about sixteen times](https://www.news-medical.net/life-sciences/History-of-the-Electron-Microscope.aspx), weaker than a child's microscope.

**Back.** By 1933 the tool had already passed the best light microscope, and in 1939 Ruska's employer [Siemens sold the first one](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ernst-Ruska), resolving detail down to about [10 nanometres](https://tutorials.al.ucsd.edu/index.php/History_of_the_Electron_Microscope), twenty times finer than light can ever reach. It took the first pictures of a virus. Pushed further over the decades, the electron beam reached the atom itself, and the electron microscope became the eye of virology, materials science, structural biology, and chipmaking: you cannot design or inspect a modern microchip without it. Germany built it first and Siemens sold it, but the electron eye spread into every science, and the instrument that began weaker than a toy now sees a single atom.

That arc deserves its own card on the Electron Eye thread; flagged, and the specific present-day market shares need their own sourcing pass before they go on a card face. **Nuance.** The 1931 prototype's magnification is cited variously (~14–16×); it was below the light microscope until the 1933 instrument surpassed it. The "first electron microscope" credit is shared by Knoll and Ruska; Ruska took the 1986 Nobel (with Binnig and Rohrer, who won for the scanning tunnelling microscope). Cryo-EM, the branch that images frozen proteins, took its own Nobel in 2017.
