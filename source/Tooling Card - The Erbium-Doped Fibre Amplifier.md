*Make · Southampton, UK · 1980s*
**David Payne (Southampton)** · fibre that amplifies its own light

**Front.** In 1987, at the University of Southampton, a professor named [David Payne](https://spie.org/news/photonics-focus/janfeb-2021/amp-it-up-david-payne) had been pulling glass into hair-thin fibre for twenty years. Light fades as it travels down fibre, so on the new ocean cables the signal had to stop [about every fifty kilometres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TAT-8) at a box that turned it into electricity and back, one channel at a time. Payne's group slipped a trace of erbium, a rare metal, into the glass core as they made the fibre; his student [Robert Mears](https://spectrum.ieee.org/edfa-robert-mears-inventor) spliced a three-metre length into the line and shone a laser down it, and the erbium atoms soaked up the laser's energy and fed it to the signal passing through, leaving it [six hundred times stronger](https://digital-library.theiet.org/doi/abs/10.1049/el:19870719) with almost no added hiss. No box, no electricity: three metres of doped glass was itself the amplifier.

**Back.** [Bell Labs](https://subtelforum.com/erbium-doped-fiber-amplifiers-bell-labs/) had a rival version working within the year, and once [small diode lasers](https://www.eduard-rhein-stiftung.de/en/erfindung-des-erbium-dotierten-faserverstarkers-edfa/) replaced the bench-top pump, the amplifier shrank to a coil that could be sealed in a capsule and sunk to the sea floor. In 1996 [TAT-12/13](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TAT-12/13), the first Atlantic cable built around erbium amplifiers, carried [5 billion bits a second](https://opg.optica.org/abstract.cfm?uri=OFC-1996-TuN2) per fibre pair where the 1988 cable had managed 280 million; and because the doped glass boosts many colours of light at once, one fibre soon carried dozens of channels side by side, the room the young internet grew into. Southampton's lab grew into a world centre for fibre research, but AT&T's factories and the cable firms built the amplifiers that filled the oceans, and every long undersea cable laid since has erbium glass spliced through it.
