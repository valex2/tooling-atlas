*Measure · Salisbury, England · 1290s*
**anonymous European clockmakers** · a catch that releases gears one tooth at a time

**Front.** In 1271 the astronomer [Robertus Anglicus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Verge_escapement) wrote that clockmakers were trying to build a wheel that turned once a day, "but they cannot quite complete their task." A hanging weight pulls hard and then faster and faster, so a wheel it drives just spins away. Within a generation, somewhere in monastery and town workshops, someone added a vertical rod with two small flags, the verge, set against a toothed crown wheel. The flags catch one tooth, swing a weighted bar (the foliot) one way, let that tooth slip, then catch the next, so the whole train moves in even steps and the weight falls in a slow, counted drip.

**Back.** The catch turned a falling weight into a steady tick, and that tick is what every later clock refined rather than replaced. The best verge-and-foliot clocks still drifted about [15 minutes a day](https://www.mdpi.com/2673-3161/1/2/8), until [Christiaan Huygens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendulum_clock) swapped the swinging bar for a pendulum in the 1650s and cut the error to seconds. The same idea of a regular beat gating a count runs from the pendulum to the marine chronometer to the atomic clock. It opened the way to time you could carry, sell, and trust.

*Curator's note:* **Placement.** Measure cell, Goal Both, Pace-layers, Confidence Plausible. The escapement does not measure a quantity in the world; it manufactures a unit and then counts it, the deepest "see what's there" move in the Timekeeping thread. **Analysis.** Hero of the medieval end of the thread and the parent of the pendulum clock, which sits as its own card; this card holds the root mechanism, the pendulum card holds the precision leap. It reads as the crude first member of a long family of beat-keepers that ends in quartz and atomic clocks. **Nuance.** No named inventor and no surviving original site, so the Place is representative, not literal: Salisbury Cathedral's iron tower clock (commonly dated ~1386) is the best-preserved early verge-and-foliot machine and stands in for the lost 1270s-1300s workshops; the 1386 attribution is itself contested, with a minority of horologists dating the surviving mechanism centuries later. Year 1300 marks the window when such clocks are first solidly attested, after Robertus Anglicus's 1271 note that the trick was not yet solved.
