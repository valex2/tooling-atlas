*Make · Hawthorne, California · 2010s*
**SpaceX** · a rocket booster that flies home and lands

**Front.** For sixty years every rocket worked the same way. You burned the big bottom stage to get up to speed, then dropped it in the sea and it was gone. [SpaceX](https://www.space.com/31420-spacex-rocket-landing-success.html), a young rocket company in Hawthorne, California, thought that was insane. It was like throwing away the plane after one flight. On the night of [December 21, 2015](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Falcon_9_flight_20), a [Falcon 9](https://www.space.com/31420-spacex-rocket-landing-success.html) lifted eleven small satellites toward orbit, and then the bottom stage did something no rocket had ever done. It turned around in the dark, fell back toward the coast, lit its engines again to slow itself, and set itself down upright on a pad near the launch site. It stood there on its legs, whole. The new thing was not the trip up. It was a booster you could fly and then [get back](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Falcon_9_first-stage_landing_tests).

**Back.** Once you can land a booster, you can clean it up and fly it again, and again. One single booster has now flown more than [thirty times](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Falcon_9_first-stage_boosters). That dropped the price of carrying a kilogram to orbit from the Space Shuttle's [tens of thousands of dollars](https://theconversation.com/how-spacex-lowered-costs-and-reduced-barriers-to-space-112586) down toward a few thousand, roughly twenty times cheaper. The real tool underneath was the way they worked: build a thing fast, fly it, let it blow up, learn from the wreck, build a better one, and make the engines [in their own factory](https://theconversation.com/how-spacex-lowered-costs-and-reduced-barriers-to-space-112586). In [May 2020](https://www.nasa.gov/news-release/nasa-astronauts-launch-from-america-in-historic-test-flight-of-spacex-crew-dragon/) the same company carried two NASA astronauts up from American soil, the first time since 2011 the country had not been buying seats from Russia. The lead in getting to space came back, and this time it sat in private hands.

*Curator's note:* **Placement.** Make cell, Hawthorne, 2015. Serves Goal 1, since the launch lead rides directly on owning one tool nobody else had, the booster you get back, and Goal 2, since the lead in cheap launch came home to America and landed in a private company rather than NASA, then stayed there because SpaceX kept pouring money into it. Confidence Direct on the December 21 2015 landing, the >30-flight booster, the cost drop, and the May 2020 crewed flight, all multiply sourced (Space.com, the Falcon 9 flight-20 and booster-list pages, The Conversation, NASA). The "what was new" lands on the tool (a recoverable booster) and the back lands on the deeper craft (build-fly-break-learn, engines made in-house), per the skill. **Analysis.** This is the modern beat on [[Thread - Reaching Orbit]]: the thread runs from the V-2 and the first throwaway stages up to the moment launch stops being a one-shot waste. It pairs head-on with [[Tooling Card - The Space Shuttle]] (the Space Shuttle), which promised reuse and made it more expensive, not less: same dream, opposite outcome, which is the cleanest way to show that reuse alone is not the win, cheap fast reuse is. It also rhymes back along the Forced-by-War / Carrier material in the deck as the inverse: where most launch capability was born under government war money (the V-2, the Shuttle), here the lead migrated *out* of the state and into a firm, driven by manufacturing discipline rather than secrecy or a smuggled craft. **Mechanism.** Tagged Convergence, but read it as the reservoir filling inside one company: the iterate-and-blow-things-up loop plus in-house engine-building is a compounding engine, each crash feeding the next build. It strains a little, since classic Convergence is a capability pooling across a whole machine-tool trade, and here the pooling happens inside one shop rather than across an industry; if more SpaceX-style cards keep showing a single firm internalizing the reservoir, that is a model-check signal worth tracking. **Nuance.** Only the first stage is reused; the upper stage is still expended, so "reusable rocket" is shorthand for "reusable booster," which the face keeps honest by saying booster. Cost-per-kilogram figures vary widely by source and by how you count refurbishment and overhead; the Shuttle is often given around $54,500/kg and a reused Falcon 9 around $2,700/kg, hence "roughly twenty times," stated as a range rather than a single hard number. The single-booster flight count keeps rising (past 30, with fleet leaders cited at 32 to 35 in late 2025 and into 2026), so the card says "more than thirty" to avoid going stale. SpaceX was founded in 2002; the December 2015 landing followed several failed attempts, including two earlier tries at landing on a drone ship at sea. Crew Dragon Demo-2 (May 30 2020) carried Doug Hurley and Bob Behnken and ended nine years of buying Soyuz seats.
