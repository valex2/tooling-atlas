*Model · Germany · 1920s*
**Julius Edgar Lilienfeld** · a solid-state switch, on paper

**Front.** In 1925, [Julius Edgar Lilienfeld](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julius_Edgar_Lilienfeld), a physics professor at Leipzig who had spent twenty years on vacuum and X-ray tubes, cared more about his patents than his chair; within a year he would give the chair up and move to America for good. The amplifier of his day was a glass vacuum tube with a hot filament, and the filament drank power and burned out. That October he [filed a patent in Canada](https://patentimages.storage.googleapis.com/fa/5d/33/ed2769d48fac4d/US1745175.pdf) for a replacement: a sliver of [copper sulfide](https://www.computerhistory.org/siliconengine/field-effect-semiconductor-device-concepts-patented/) carrying current between two contacts, with a third contact beside the film whose voltage would squeeze the current up and down, the way the tube's grid did, inside a cold solid. The patent set the whole idea down, current in a solid steered by voltage alone, a switch that nobody on earth could yet build.

**Back.** Twenty years later [Shockley at Bell Labs](https://ipwatchdog.com/2017/04/03/transistor-shockley-bardeen-brattain-modern-electronics/) tried the same solid amplifier and failed the same way; that failure pushed Bardeen and Brattain sideways into the point-contact transistor of 1947, and when Bell's lawyers went to patent it they found [Lilienfeld's claims already covering the field effect](https://www.eejournal.com/article/a-brief-history-of-the-mos-transistor-part-1-early-visionaries/), wrote around them, and left Shockley's name off the application. Not until [1960](https://engineering.purdue.edu/150/Consequential-Stories/150th-john-atalla) did Atalla and Kahng at Bell, with silicon grown pure and its surface sealed under a skin of oxide, build the insulated-gate switch Lilienfeld had drawn in a [1928 follow-on patent](https://www.computerhistory.org/siliconengine/field-effect-semiconductor-device-concepts-patented/): the MOSFET, of which factories have since made about [13 sextillion](https://computerhistory.org/blog/13-sextillion-counting-the-long-winding-road-to-the-most-frequently-manufactured-human-artifact-in-history/), more copies than of anything else people make. Lilienfeld got nothing from the wait; his patent lived its seventeen years and ran out in January 1947, and the first transistor of any kind worked that December.
