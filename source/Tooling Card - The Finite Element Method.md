*Model · UK · 1960s*
**engineers incl. Ray Clough & John Argyris** · cut structures into solvable pieces

**Front.** In the autumn of [1954](https://www.emerald.com/aeat/article/26/10/347/21955), a professor in London named [John Argyris](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/cnm.709) began filling a trade magazine, *Aircraft Engineering*, with a new kind of arithmetic: he wrote the forces and bends of a whole jet wing as tables of numbers a computer could grind through. Engineers at [Boeing](https://arc.aiaa.org/doi/10.2514/8.3664) had hit the same wall (the delta wing of a new jet was too oddly shaped for any beam formula), so over two summers Ray Clough and Jon Turner cut the wing, on paper, into small triangles, each simple enough to solve, and let the machine add them all back together. In [1960](https://senate.universityofcalifornia.edu/in-memoriam/files/ray-clough.html) Clough gave the trick its name, the finite element method. Any shape a formula could not touch could now be broken into pieces a computer could.

**Back.** For years the method lived in a handful of aircraft-company and university machines; then [Olgierd Zienkiewicz](https://www.swansea.ac.uk/centenary2020/century-of-inspiring-people/olek-zienkiewicz/) at Swansea wrote the [first textbook](https://www.swansea.ac.uk/science-and-engineering/research/zienkiewicz-institute-for-modelling-data-ai/about/history/finite-element-method/) on it in 1967, and NASA spent three years building it into a general program, [NASTRAN](https://spinoff.nasa.gov/spinoff1998/ct9.htm), which it handed to the public in 1971. After that, anyone with a computer could test a bridge or a car body in numbers before cutting metal. The method itself, printed in journals, belonged to everyone; the money settled in the software, and a small Los Angeles firm that helped write NASTRAN, [MacNeal-Schwendler](https://spinoff.nasa.gov/spinoff1998/ct9.htm), sold its polished copy to industry for the next fifty years.
