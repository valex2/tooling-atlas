*Measure · Stanford, California · 2014*
**Manu Prakash** · a paper microscope you fold yourself

**Front.** In 2014, a scientist at Stanford named [Manu Prakash](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0098781), tired of seeing clinics and schools that could never afford a microscope, [folded one out of a single flat sheet of paper](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4062392/) with one tiny glass ball set inside. It cost about a dollar in parts, weighed almost nothing, magnified a sample more than [a hundred times](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4062392/), and his lab [gave the plans away for free](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foldscope). Same trick as the old Delft bead, three centuries on, but handed to everyone. This time it spread instead of dying with one pair of hands.

**Back.** For three hundred years, to look at the hidden world you needed money: a lab, a heavy brass instrument, a city. The [paper microscope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foldscope) broke that. A health worker can check a drop of water for parasites in a village with no electricity, and a child anywhere can find the same swimming creatures that once needed a genius's hand-made glass. When the tool costs about a dollar and the plans are free, the number of people who might find something stops being a handful and becomes almost anyone.
