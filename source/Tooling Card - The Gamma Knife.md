*Make · Stockholm, Sweden · 1960s*
**Lars Leksell** · weak beams that cross deep in the brain

**Front.** By 1967, [Lars Leksell](https://www.elekta.com/company/about-us/story/surgeon-and-inventor/) was a neurosurgeon in Stockholm who hated what his work made him do. To kill a bad spot deep in the brain, he had to saw open the skull and push past healthy tissue to reach it. So with the physicist [Börje Larsson](https://www.elekta.com/company/about-us/story/the-early-years/) he packed [179 lumps of radioactive cobalt](https://www.butterflyavmcharity.org.uk/wp-content/uploads/bsk-pdf-manager/2022/02/1.Lundsford_chapter.pdf) into a heavy steel helmet, each one drilled to send its own thin beam of gamma rays at [a single point in the middle](https://www.neurosurgery.pitt.edu/centers/image-guided-neurosurgery/gamma-knife/technology-development). Any one beam was too weak to harm the brain it passed through, but where all of them crossed, the dose could burn away a target the size of a pea, with no cut and no bone taken out.

**Back.** In May 1968 they aimed the helmet at a [21-year-old woman](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38690888/) with a tumor behind her eyes, gave it one dose, and sent her home; more than fifty years later she was still alive and being checked. For years almost no one else copied the machine. It was strange and costly, and no one could aim it well until sharp pictures of the living brain arrived with the CT scanner in the 1970s. Leksell's family turned the helmet into a company, [Elekta](https://www.elekta.com/company/about-us/story/a-company-grows-up/), still based in Stockholm; the first American unit reached [Pittsburgh in 1987](https://www.neurosurgery.pitt.edu/news/gamma-knife-marks-32-years-upmc), and by the machine's fiftieth year Gamma Knives had treated [about 1.1 million people](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/gamma-knife-radiosurgery-hits-half-century-mark-with-over-a-million-patients-treated-300588723.html).
