*Manufacture · Sukhumi, Georgia · 1950s*
**Gernot Zippe** · spin uranium gas to sort it by isotope

**Front.** In the early 1950s the Austrian engineer [Gernot Zippe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gernot_Zippe) was a war prisoner in a Soviet lab at Sukhumi, set to a hard problem with a team under Max Steenbeck. Natural uranium is almost all the wrong kind: only [about 0.72%](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zippe-type_centrifuge) is U-235, the isotope that splits and powers a reactor or a bomb, and the rest is U-238, which is [just 1.26% heavier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zippe-type_centrifuge). Aston's [mass spectrograph could pull those two apart one atom at a time](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/chemistry/1922/aston/biographical/), but no one could do it by the ton. Zippe's team built a tall thin rotor that turned the uranium to gas and spun it at [hundreds of meters a second](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zippe-type_centrifuge), so the faintly heavier U-238 crowded to the outer wall and the lighter U-235 stayed nearer the middle. The new trick was the rotor itself: it stood on the tip of a needle and rode loose top bearings, so instead of shaking itself apart at high speed it [settled and ran for years](https://www.asimov.press/p/centrifuge). One machine barely sorts the gas, so you pipe it through thousands in a chain, each one nudging the mix a little richer, from 0.7% up to the [3%–5% a reactor burns or the ~90% a weapon needs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zippe-type_centrifuge).

**Back.** Released in 1956, Zippe [rebuilt the machine from memory at the University of Virginia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zippe-type_centrifuge) and published the unclassified parts, and the design spread west into the Urenco consortium of Britain, the Netherlands, and Germany. It also leaked: [A.Q. Khan copied Urenco's drawings in the 1970s](https://ahf.nuclearmuseum.org/ahf/profile/q-khan/), carried them to Pakistan, and sold the design on to [Iran, Libya, and North Korea](https://isis-online.org/publications/southasia/nuclear_black_market.html), so the same rotor now turns under inspectors' seals and under sanctions. Enrichment is still a tight club: in 2023 Russia's Rosatom held [roughly 40% of the world market](https://www.enerdata.net/publications/executive-briefing/uranium-enrichment.html), Urenco about 30%, France's Orano around 14%, and China roughly 12%. The rotor compounded for whoever kept building it. The US let its own cascades wither and now [leans on Russian spinning](https://www.thirdway.org/memo/western-reliance-on-russian-fuel-a-dangerous-game) for a fifth of its reactor fuel, while China keeps adding centrifuges, so the tool that decides who gets the fuel sits mostly in Moscow and Beijing.

*Curator's note:* **Placement.** Manufacture cell, Sukhumi, 1956, on [[Thread - Critical Materials]]. Goal Both; Mechanism Pace-layers. Confidence Direct. **Analysis.** This is the industrial twin of any near-1 separation cascade: a single stage barely separates, so you chain thousands to climb the curve, the same logic as solvent-extraction tanks but with rotors instead of vessels. It rests on [[Tooling Card - The Mass Spectrograph]], which first split the two uranium isotopes by mass at the atomic scale, and it feeds [[Tooling Card - The Nuclear Pile]] the enriched fuel a reactor or weapon runs on. **Nuance.** Credit is partly contested: the type carries Zippe's name because he rebuilt and described it in the West, but Steenbeck and a 60-strong German-Austrian team did the Sukhumi work, and the Soviet record disputes who contributed what. The moat is rebuildable rather than durable. Zippe proved you can copy it from memory, Khan proved you can steal it, so the barrier is now precision machining, materials like maraging steel and carbon-fiber rotors, and export control, not secrecy.
