*Manufacture · Oak Ridge, Tennessee, USA · 1940s*
**Clarence Johnson** · porous nickel barrier membrane

**Front.** To make the bomb, the United States had to pull apart two kinds of uranium atom that weigh almost the same, and the plan was to push uranium turned into a gas through a metal sheet full of tiny holes. The lighter atom slips through a hair faster, so each pass leaves the gas a touch richer; the [physics gives a separation factor of about 1.0043 per stage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaseous_diffusion), which is why the K-25 plant chained thousands of stages into a cascade. The open part was the physics. The wall was making the sheet: a [nickel membrane with holes 10 to 25 nanometers wide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaseous_diffusion) that would not clog and would not be eaten by uranium hexafluoride, a gas so corrosive it attacks most metals. After [Edward Norris and Edward Adler's electroplated mesh proved too brittle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K-25), Groves switched in January 1944 to a sintered nickel-powder barrier that **Clarence Johnson** had pushed past both earlier designs.

**Back.** The [Houdaille-Hershey plant in Decatur, Illinois](https://ahf.nuclearmuseum.org/ahf/location/decatur-il/) was stripped of its just-installed machinery and rebuilt to plate the barrier, and by summer 1944 the material was shipping to Oak Ridge; K-25 made the enriched uranium that went into the Hiroshima bomb. No country wrote the recipe down for anyone to copy. The know-how for [sintering the nickel powder to the right pore structure stayed classified](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaseous_diffusion), held as process craft rather than a published method, and the guard held long enough that gaseous diffusion stayed the main US and Western way to enrich uranium into the 1980s, when the gas centrifuge finally undercut it. The secret never leaked because the hard part was not a formula but the feel for making the membrane, and that opened the way to a four-decade lead.
