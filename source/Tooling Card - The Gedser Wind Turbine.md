*Make · Gedser, Denmark · 1950s*
**Johannes Juul** · three-blade grid-fed wind turbine

**Front.** In [1957](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gedser_wind_turbine), on Denmark's flat south coast, a utility engineer named [Johannes Juul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johannes_Juul), nearly seventy, was finishing the biggest wind turbine he had ever built. Denmark burned imported coal for its power, and his company SEAS wanted to know whether wind could feed the grid like an ordinary power plant. At Gedser he raised a concrete tower, set on it [three fixed blades](https://www.gedserwindturbine.com/danishconceptuk.html) of wood and steel spanning [24 metres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gedser_wind_turbine), and wired the rotor to a plain induction generator, the same rugged kind that runs a factory motor, pushing [200 kilowatts](https://www.osti.gov/etdeweb/biblio/8141196) straight into the wires. Because the blades could not twist, strong wind simply stalled over them and held the power down; if the rotor ever ran away, the blade tips swung out on hinges and braked it in the air.

**Back.** The turbine ran ten years on the grid and made [2.2 million kilowatt-hours](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gedser_wind_turbine), then cheap oil idled it in 1967. Ten years later, with oil suddenly scarce, the American energy agency [paid to fit new blades](https://gedserwindturbine.com/NASAtestprogramdk.html) on the twenty-year-old machine so its engineers could [measure](https://www.osti.gov/etdeweb/biblio/8141196) why the simple Danish turbine had held together while their own big two-blade designs struggled. Danish workshops copied Juul's layout at farm size, [won the California wind boom](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0309524X241260061) of the 1980s, and the lead compounded: almost every large turbine on earth today keeps his shape, and the biggest builder of them, [Vestas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vestas), is still Danish.
