*Measure · Vincennes, France · 1780s-1820s*
**Honore Blanc** · paired pass/fail steel gauge

**Front.** In [1785](https://www.allaboutlean.com/230-years-interchangeability/) a French gunsmith named [Honoré Blanc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Honor%C3%A9_Blanc) had a hard problem. To make musket locks whose parts could swap, every part had to come out the same size, but no two filers' hands work the same way. So he made his workers stop trusting their eyes and use a pair of hardened steel checks instead. One check the part had to slide into; the other it must not enter. The trick came from the army's old way of sizing cannon balls: a ball that [passed the "go" check but stuck at the "no-go" check](https://www.allaboutlean.com/230-years-interchangeability/) was the right size, and anything else was scrap. A worker who could not measure at all could now hold a part to two steel ends and know pass or fail, so parts from many hands came out alike.

**Back.** Blanc could soon turn out lock parts that fit any lock at only [10% more cost](https://www.allaboutlean.com/230-years-interchangeability/), but the French state stopped paying and he [died in 1801](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Honor%C3%A9_Blanc) with the work dropped. The idea crossed the ocean with [Thomas Jefferson](https://www.americanheritage.com/eli-whitneys-other-talent), who had watched Blanc fit random parts together. The American armories took the paired check and ran with it: at Harpers Ferry [John Hall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_H._Hall_(gunsmith)) built [63 gauges](https://www.allaboutlean.com/230-years-interchangeability/) to size one lock, and in 1826 a commission [tore down 100 of his rifles, mixed the parts, and built them back](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M1819_Hall_rifle). The gauge opened the way to making the same part in many places and trusting it to fit.

*Curator's note:* **Placement.** Cell Measure, Goal 1 (the necessity case). Mechanism is Convergence: the whole American System pools on top of the gauge, because uniform parts are only possible once size can be checked the same way by everyone. Confidence is **Direct** on the core chain. Blanc used gauges and filing jigs to bring parts to interchangeability; the go/no-go pair is documented as the army's cannon-ball sizing method (Gribeauval's system) that Blanc carried into small arms; the 1785 Vincennes demonstration, Jefferson's witnessing, the 10% cost figure, Hall's 63 gauges, and the 1826 mix-and-rebuild test are all sourced.

**Analysis.** This card is the reframe of the old "interchangeable parts" card onto the actual instrument. Interchangeability is the *result*; the gauge is the *tool*. Filing under Measure, not Make, is the point: the gauge does not shape the part, it judges it, and that judgment is what lets unskilled labor hit a fixed size. It pairs against the long-form **Gauge → Interchangeable Parts** entry (which owns the "leadership follows the toolmaking and migrates both ways" thesis) and sits beside the armory carrier cards the user is merging; this card stays narrowly on the gauge as instrument and leaves the carrier story to those.

**Nuance.** The go/no-go pair predates Blanc as a cannon-gauging practice (attributed to Gribeauval's artillery reforms), so Blanc is best framed as the man who carried the paired check into small-arms manufacture, not its sole inventor; the front says "came from the army's old way" to keep this honest. The 10% cost figure is single-sourced to AllAboutLean. Springfield only reached full interchangeability around 1849, so the 1826 Hall test is the cleaner early proof and is the one cited here.
