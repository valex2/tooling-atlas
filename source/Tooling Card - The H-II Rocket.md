*Make · Tanegashima, Japan · 1990s*
**NASDA / Mitsubishi (LE-7)** · home-built staged-combustion hydrogen launcher

**Front.** In 1984, engineers at [NASDA](https://global.jaxa.jp/projects/rockets/h2/index.html), Japan's space agency, flew rockets whose main engines were [built from American drawings under license](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/H-II). Borrowed designs came with borrowed permission, and Japan wanted a heavy rocket that was wholly its own. So [Mitsubishi](https://www.mhi.com/products/space/first_stage_engine_le-7_le-7a.html) machined and test-fired the [LE-7](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LE-7), a first-stage engine that burns liquid hydrogen the hard way: the hydrogen is burned once, fuel-rich, to spin the pumps, then piped into the main chamber and burned again, so no fuel is thrown overboard. Only America and the Soviet Union had ever made a hydrogen engine run that way, and ten years and one fatal test-stand explosion later, the engine's [110 tons of thrust](https://global.jaxa.jp/projects/rockets/h2/index.html) lifted the first all-Japanese [H-II off Tanegashima island on February 4, 1994](https://global.jaxa.jp/projects/rockets/h2/index.html).

**Back.** Each H-II launch cost about [19 billion yen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/H-II), near $190 million, too dear to win foreign customers, and after engine failures sank two flights in 1998 and 1999 Japan retired it at seven flights. The engine outlived the rocket. Mitsubishi rebuilt the LE-7 simpler and cheaper, and the [H-IIA](https://www.nasaspaceflight.com/2025/06/gosat-gw-launch/) built around it flew [fifty times with one failure](https://www.space.com/space-exploration/launches-spacecraft/japan-launching-of-gosat-gw-on-50th-and-final-liftoff-of-the-h-2a-rocket); when the last one climbed off the same pad in June 2025, Japan had been launching its own satellites on its own engines for thirty-one years.
