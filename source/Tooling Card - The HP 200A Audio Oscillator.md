*Measure · Palo Alto, California, USA · 1930s*
**William Hewlett** · a light bulb that holds a tone steady

**Front.** To find out whether a microphone or an amplifier is any good, you feed it one clean tone at a pitch you have chosen and listen to what comes back out. In 1938 [Bill Hewlett](https://www.hpe.com/us/en/about/history/innovation-gallery/007-product.html) was a graduate student at Stanford looking for a thesis, and the machines that made that tone were the problem. They were heavy, they drifted, they had to be reset to stay accurate, and they [cost between $200 and $600](https://www.hpmemoryproject.org/timeline/john_minck/inside_hp_04.htm). Hewlett wanted the tone to come from plain resistors and capacitors instead of coils, so the pitch could be set by turning a dial. A loop like that will sing, but the sound swells until it goes ragged, and holding it at one steady loudness is the hard part. In the summer of 1938 he wired a [three-watt light bulb](https://www.hpmemoryproject.org/wa_pages/wall_a_page_01.htm) into the path that feeds the loop's output back to its input. When the tone grows, more current runs through the bulb, the tungsten wire inside gets hotter, its resistance climbs, and it holds the loop back. The tone sat still, [bent by less than one part in a hundred](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HP_200A) across the range a person can hear. The next year Hewlett and Dave Packard were building the boxes by hand in [a rented garage at 367 Addison Avenue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HP_Garage) and selling them for [$54.40](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HP_Garage).

**Back.** A sound engineer from Walt Disney Studios saw one working, asked for a wider dial, and [bought eight of the modified model at $71.50 each](https://www.hewlettpackardhistory.com/item/a-deal-with-disney/); Disney's crew used them to check the channels, recorders, and speakers in the [twelve theatres wired for Fantasound](https://www.hewlettpackardhistory.com/item/a-deal-with-disney/) when *Fantasia* opened in 1940. Their old professor [Frederick Terman](https://www.referenceforbusiness.com/history2/20/Hewlett-Packard-Company.html), by then running anti-radar work at Harvard, put the pair to building microwave signal generators for the war, and the garage shop came out of it with 200 people and about a million dollars a year in sales. [General Radio](https://www.hpmemoryproject.org/timeline/john_minck/inside_hp_04.htm), which had owned the bench-oscillator trade, did not answer with a resistance-tuned instrument of its own until 1948, and priced it at $365.
