*Manufacture · Hsinchu, Taiwan · 1980s onward*
**Morris Chang and TSMC's process engineers** · a tuned thousand-step flow run at high yield

**Front.** In 1987 a chip veteran named [Morris Chang](https://spectrum.ieee.org/morris-chang-foundry-father) opened a plant in [Hsinchu, Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TSMC) that would build chips for anyone and design none of its own. The hard part was not the promise. It was the doing. To turn a blank silicon wafer into a working chip you run it through [more than a thousand exact steps](https://english.cw.com.tw/article/article.action?id=2272), each one laying down, etching, or doping a layer thinner than a virus, and a single speck of dust can kill the whole wafer. The engineers wrote down every step as a recipe and then tuned it, run after run, by reading the [millions of measurements](https://english.cw.com.tw/article/article.action?id=2272) a fab spits out each day and nudging the parameters until fewer wafers came out broken. That tuned recipe was the tool. It lived in no single head.

**Back.** The payoff shows up at each new node. When TSMC started its 5-nanometer line it was already losing [fewer chips to defects than its older 7-nanometer line had](https://ljdevice.com.tw/en/better-yield-5nm-7nm-tsmc-update-defect-rates-n5/), and within months it was making good chips on [about 80 percent of every wafer](https://fuse.wikichip.org/news/3398/tsmc-details-5-nm/). Samsung and Intel buy the [same ASML machines](https://patentpc.com/blog/samsung-vs-tsmc-vs-intel-whos-winning-the-foundry-market), yet at 3 nanometers TSMC was getting [good chips off more than 90 percent of a wafer while Samsung was stuck near 50](https://www.trendforce.com/news/2025/05/29/news-samsungs-3nm-yield-reportedly-stuck-at-50-far-behind-tsmcs-90/). The machine prints the chip; the recipe decides how many survive, and that recipe is craft that does not travel. Today the world's [most advanced chips are made on one island](https://wccftech.com/tsmc-dominates-global-foundry-market-with-a-jaw-dropping-share/), inside one company's walls.

*Curator's note:* **Placement.** Manufacture cell, Hsinchu, dated to TSMC's 1987 founding, the present-day end of [[Thread - Chip Lithography]]. Serves Goal 1 (leading-edge chip leadership rides on this one recipe) and Goal 2 (the lead in actually making chips migrated to Taiwan). Mechanism on the face is Convergence, where the industry's designs come to be built, but the durable layer underneath is pace-layers: the recipe is tacit, spread across thousands of engineers and millions of logged runs, and cannot be bought or regrown on a rival's schedule, the same "institutionalized craft is unbuyable" point as the Zeiss and EUV cards. Confidence: direct. **Analysis.** Reframed off the foundry business model (an institution) onto the thing the model's moat actually is: the high-yield process flow itself, a method held as tacit craft. This is the "after ASML" card and pairs with [[Tooling Card - The EUV Machine]]: that card is the machine no one else can build, this is the recipe no one else can run. Together they state the deck's thesis at its sharpest, design is public, the moat is in the doing, and they are the cleanest manufacture-vs-machine contrast in the deck. The yield gap is the cleanest example of tacit process craft beating identical equipment. **Nuance.** The 90%-vs-50% 3 nm figure is a mid-2020s [TrendForce report](https://www.trendforce.com/news/2025/05/29/news-samsungs-3nm-yield-reportedly-stuck-at-50-far-behind-tsmcs-90/); exact yields are closely held and vendor-reported, so treat them as well-sourced estimates, not audited numbers. The N5 "better than N7" defect claim is TSMC's own 2020 disclosure. TSMC was founded as a joint venture with the Taiwanese government and Philips; "build anyone's chip, compete with no one" is Chang's framing. The one-island line refers to Taiwan's location and chip-supply geopolitics, not any claim of imminent action.
