*Make · United States (TI and Fairchild) · 1950s*
**Jack Kilby and Robert Noyce** · many switches on one chip

**Front.** By the late 1950s a computer needed thousands of tiny parts, and someone had to wire every one to its neighbors by hand. With that many joints a few were always wrong, and one bad joint among thousands killed the whole machine. A Bell Labs man named [Jack Morton called it the tyranny of numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tyranny_of_numbers). In the summer of 1958 [Jack Kilby](https://nationalmaglab.org/magnet-academy/history-of-electricity-magnetism/pioneers/jack-kilby/), too new at Texas Instruments to have earned the holiday, sat alone in an empty lab and saw the fix: make all the parts from one piece of crystal, so there is nothing left to wire. On [September 12, 1958](https://www.computerhistory.org/siliconengine/all-semiconductor-solid-circuit-is-demonstrated/) he showed a working circuit cut from a single sliver of germanium, its few parts still joined by little gold wires. A year later at Fairchild, [Robert Noyce](https://www.computerhistory.org/siliconengine/practical-monolithic-integrated-circuit-concept-patented/) built the version you could make by the thousand, laying the parts flat in silicon and printing the wires on top as thin metal lines, with no hand-soldering at all.

**Back.** Noyce's flat chip used a new trick from his colleague Jean Hoerni, the [planar process](https://www.computerhistory.org/siliconengine/invention-of-the-planar-manufacturing-process/): print the parts and the wires onto the silicon with light, instead of placing them by hand. That seed grew straight into the [machines that print every chip today](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extreme_ultraviolet_lithography). The lead did not die. It pooled in the United States, in the Texas desert at TI and in the orchards south of San Francisco that the chip soon renamed Silicon Valley, and it has compounded for sixty years.
