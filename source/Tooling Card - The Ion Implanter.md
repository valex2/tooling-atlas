*Manufacture · Peabody, Massachusetts, USA · 1970s*
**Peter H. Rose** · a counted beam of dopant ions

**Front.** In early 1969 the money for big physics machines was drying up at [High Voltage Engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High_Voltage_Engineering_Corporation) in Burlington, Massachusetts, so Peter Rose, who ran the accelerator research lab there, took a small contract from Fairchild Semiconductor to build a little one. His group put it together [in six weeks, mostly out of parts lifted from the stockroom](https://www.axcelis.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/05/Father_of_Ion_Implantation.pdf). It stood thirty feet long and threw 20 microamperes of argon ions at a target at 100 keV. Nobody on the team had ever seen a silicon wafer, so they shot aluminum disks instead. Rose wanted to sell chipmakers a rugged version for the production floor, his president said no, and in 1971 he walked out with five colleagues and rented a disused leather factory in Peabody with a stream running a foot beneath the floor and a working tannery next door. Older accelerators bent the beam through a magnet after speeding it up, which stretched the machine across a room; Rose put the magnet first, at the slow end, and folded the whole beamline down to something a fab could fit on its floor. Most people in the field told him it would not work. The boron ion source came from a sketch [Bob McPherson of National Semiconductor drew on a paper napkin over dinner](https://www.axcelis.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/05/Father_of_Ion_Implantation.pdf), copied to its exact dimensions the next day. At the far end the beam landed in a metal cup that counted the charge arriving, and the machine shut the beam off when the count hit the number asked for.

**Back.** The old way to dope silicon was to bake it in a hot gas and let the dopant soak in, which meant junction depth, peak concentration and total amount all came as one package, so MOS transistors came off the line with scattered turn-on voltages and had to be [sorted into bins with only one bin usable](https://www.axcelis.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/05/Father_of_Ion_Implantation.pdf). Chipmakers wanted the yield for hand-held calculators running off a 9-volt battery, and a counted dose in the channel set the turn-on voltage directly. Extrion shipped its first machine eighteen months in, to National Semiconductor, a ton of steel that two forklifts borrowed from the tannery next door hoisted onto a flatbed, priced at $64,000. Implantation [displaced thermal diffusion across the industry](https://www.axcelis.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/02/Ion_Implantation_in_Silicon_Technology.pdf), Varian bought Extrion [in 1975](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/0001079023/000092701601504410/d10k.txt), and Applied Materials paid [$4.9 billion for that business in 2011](https://ir.appliedmaterials.com/news-releases/news-release-details/applied-materials-acquire-varian-semiconductor-equipment). Shockley had [patented the idea of doping by ion bombardment in 1954](https://patents.google.com/patent/US2787564A/en); the patent ran out in 1974, just as the market for the machine opened, and earned him almost nothing. A 1970s NMOS chip took six to eight implants, a modern logic chip with memory on it takes [up to thirty-five](https://www.axcelis.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/02/Ion_Implantation_in_Silicon_Technology.pdf), and a large fab runs about twenty implanters to keep up.
