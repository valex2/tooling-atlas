*Make · Rugby, England · 1930s*
**Frank Whittle** · a gas turbine that pushes a plane

**Front.** In April 1937, a 29-year-old Royal Air Force officer named Frank Whittle stood in a works at Rugby, England, waiting to light an engine no one had run before. A propeller pulls a plane by clawing at the air, and it runs out of bite as the air thins high up and the plane goes fast. Whittle's machine did something else. It [sucked in air, squeezed it, sprayed in fuel and burned it](https://www.geaerospace.com/news/articles/100-year-anniversary-technology/fight-or-flight-startling-start-whittles-first-jet-engine), fired the hot gas out the back to push, and bled off some of that spin to drive the squeezer at the front. On [12 April 1937 it ran](https://www.thisdayinaviation.com/12-april-1937/), screamed up to 8,000 rpm, and kept accelerating after the fuel was cut because fuel had pooled inside, a runaway that sent the crew scattering. Here was an engine with almost no parts that go back and forth, one that ran better the faster and higher the plane flew.

**Back.** A German engineer, Hans von Ohain, had built the same idea on his own, and his engine flew first. The [Heinkel He 178 took off on 27 August 1939](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heinkel_He_178), the first plane carried by a turbojet, nearly two years before [Whittle's engine flew the Gloster E.28/39 in 1941](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frank_Whittle). Both designs were picked up fast, by the German jets of the war and then by [General Electric, which built engines from Whittle's design](https://www.geaerospace.com/news/articles/100-year-anniversary-technology/fight-or-flight-startling-start-whittles-first-jet-engine). The turbojet gave the world airliners that cross oceans high and quick. But it ran straight into one wall: the blades just behind the fire spin in gas hotter than the metal can take, [today around 0.85 to 0.9 of the metal's melting point](https://www.americanscientist.org/article/each-blade-a-single-crystal). Whittle held the patent and von Ohain flew first, yet neither country kept the lead; it settled with the few firms that learned to cast a blade which does not soften in the fire.
