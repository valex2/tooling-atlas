*Make · Rugby, England · 1930s*
**Frank Whittle** · a gas turbine that pushes a plane

**Front.** In April 1937, a 29-year-old Royal Air Force officer named Frank Whittle stood in a works at Rugby, England, waiting to light an engine no one had run before. A propeller pulls a plane by clawing at the air, and it runs out of bite as the air thins high up and the plane goes fast. Whittle's machine did something else: it [sucked in air, squeezed it, sprayed in fuel and burned it](https://www.geaerospace.com/news/articles/100-year-anniversary-technology/fight-or-flight-startling-start-whittles-first-jet-engine), then fired the hot gas out the back to push, and bled off some of that spin to drive the squeezer at the front. On [12 April 1937 it ran](https://www.thisdayinaviation.com/12-april-1937/), screamed up to 8,000 rpm, and kept accelerating after the fuel was cut because fuel had pooled inside, a runaway that sent the crew scattering. What was new was an engine with almost no parts that go back and forth, one that ran better the faster and higher the plane flew.

**Back.** A German engineer, Hans von Ohain, had built the same idea on his own, and his engine flew first: the [Heinkel He 178 took off on 27 August 1939](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heinkel_He_178), the first plane carried by a turbojet, nearly two years before [Whittle's engine flew the Gloster E.28/39 in 1941](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frank_Whittle). Both designs were picked up fast, by the German jets of the war and then by [General Electric, which built engines from Whittle's design](https://www.geaerospace.com/news/articles/100-year-anniversary-technology/fight-or-flight-startling-start-whittles-first-jet-engine). The turbojet opened the way to airliners that cross oceans high and quick, but it ran straight into one wall: the blades just behind the fire spin in gas hotter than the metal can take, [today around 0.85 to 0.9 of the metal's melting point](https://www.americanscientist.org/article/each-blade-a-single-crystal), so the lead moved on to whoever could make a blade that does not soften.

*Curator's note:* **Placement.** Make cell, Rugby, England, 1937, sitting on both [[Thread - Energy]] and [[Thread - Flight]]. Goal tagged Both: a turbojet is a power source and the thing that made modern flight, so it serves the "lead in the tool, lead in the field" claim on two axes at once. Mechanism Convergence, two reservoirs (turbomachinery and heat-resistant metal) meeting in one machine; the moat does not sit in the cycle, which is public and was invented twice, it sits downstream in the blade. Confidence Direct: the 12 April 1937 bench run, the runaway, the 27 August 1939 He 178 flight, the 1941 Gloster flight, and the GE pickup are all cross-checked across This Day in Aviation, GE Aerospace, and Wikipedia. **Analysis.** The card's real work is the last sentence: the thermodynamic cycle was discovered independently by two men and is written in any textbook, so it is no one's moat, the moat is the blade that survives the fire. That hands off cleanly to an investment-casting / single-crystal-turbine-blade card whenever one is written, and it pairs against [[Tooling Card - The F-1 Engine]] (the other "engine you cannot calculate, only build and test" card, rocket rather than air-breathing). Independent dual invention also makes it a quiet companion to The Bit and other non-rival-idea cards: the *idea* travels free, the *tool* that realizes it at the limit does not. **Nuance.** Dual attribution is the honest hard part. Whittle patented the concept in 1930 (granted 1932) and ran a bench engine first, in 1937; von Ohain patented in 1936 and flew first, in 1939; the two worked with no knowledge of each other, so the card credits both and pins the dated moment and the map coordinates to Whittle's 1937 run while naming von Ohain's 1939 flight in the body. The "8,000 rpm and runaway" detail is from GE Aerospace and This Day in Aviation. The blade-temperature figure (0.85 to 0.9 of melting point) is the modern superalloy limit from American Scientist, used to mark where the wall is, not a 1937 number; Whittle's first engines ran far cooler and the heat ceiling tightened as engineers chased more thrust.
