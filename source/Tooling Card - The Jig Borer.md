*Make · Geneva, Switzerland · 1910s*
**Société Genevoise (SIP)** · machine that places holes by number

**Front.** In [1912](https://collection.sciencemuseumgroup.org.uk/objects/co46470/first-jig-boring-machine), the [Société Genevoise d'Instruments de Physique](https://gus-sip.ch/sip-history) was a Geneva firm that engraved standard metre scales for national measuring bureaus, and the watch trade next door brought it a problem. A watch plate carries dozens of tiny holes that must each sit exactly where the drawing says, and a shop could only get them there by scribing layout lines by hand or by first hand-fitting a hardened steel drilling guide, a jig, for every new design. SIP's machinists built a [pointing machine](https://www.lathes.co.uk/sip/): a punch over a table that slid on a fine screw, checked against the firm's own ruled scales read through a microscope, graduated to [five thousandths of a millimetre](https://www.lathes.co.uk/sip/) on the small models. A worker dialed the plate to two numbers counted from one corner and marked the hole there, with no jig and no layout lines.

**Back.** SIP grew the pointing machine into a two-ton [industrial jig borer](https://collection.sciencemuseumgroup.org.uk/objects/co46470/first-jig-boring-machine) by 1921 and sold the first one to the British rifle works at Enfield, a machine so fussy about heat that the makers said to keep it in an air-conditioned room. [Pratt & Whitney](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jig_borer) in Connecticut had built a jig borer of its own by 1917, yet Geneva stayed ahead on accuracy for decades; the 1934 SIP [Hydroptic](https://www.lathes.co.uk/sip/) stopped trusting the drive screw altogether and read the table's true position off glass scales inside the machine. Placing a cutter by two numbers became the habit that numerical control automated after the war, on machines Geneva did not build; SIP [went bankrupt in 1987](https://gus-sip.ch/sip-history), and the Enfield borer now sits in London's Science Museum.
