*Make · Malibu, USA · 1960s*
**Theodore Maiman** · a ruby rod that makes one-colour light

**Front.** In May 1960, [Theodore Maiman](https://www.hrl.com/about/laser) was a physicist at Hughes Research Labs in Malibu, working on a small budget while bigger labs raced for the same prize. The idea had been written down in 1958 by [Charles Townes and Arthur Schawlow](https://physics.aps.org/story/v15/st4), but no one had built a thing that worked. Maiman took a finger-sized rod of synthetic ruby, about [1 cm across and 2 cm long](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ruby_laser), silvered both ends like tiny mirrors, and wound a bright flash lamp around it; the flash kicks the atoms up, and they dump their energy back as light, with each new bit of light coaxing out more just like it. What came out on [16 May 1960](https://www.historypod.net/05/16/16th-may-1960-the-first-laser-was-successfully-operated-by-theodore-maiman-at-the-hughes-research-laboratories-in-malibu-california/) was new: a pulse of deep-red light at one pure colour, [694.3 nm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ruby_laser), the waves all in step, a beam you could aim and squeeze to a point.

**Back.** Other labs copied the trick within months and built lasers from gas and from chips, and the beam kept getting steadier and tighter. That one clean ray became the carrier for [optical-fibre communication](https://www.nokia.com/bell-labs/about/history/innovation-stories/maser-laser/), where flashes of laser light shoot data down glass threads, and the ruler for precision interferometry, where you measure distance by counting waves of light. The lead it opened never closed; it ran straight into the [EUV machine](https://www.hrl.com/about/laser), whose 13.5 nm light is born by blasting tin with a hard laser pulse, and into DNA sequencing, where a laser lights up the coloured tags that spell out the code.

(1) Placement: Make cell, Goal Both, mechanism Convergence, confidence Direct. This is the deck's clearest convergence-reservoir node — a single tool that feeds dozens of later cards.
(2) Analysis: It rests on Townes/Schawlow theory and Townes' 1954 maser, but the dated, working device is Maiman's, so the card is tool-forward on the ruby rod. It pairs with the EUV and DNA Sequencing cards downstream (both list it in BuildsOn once wired) and contrasts with pure-model cards (Information Theory) by being a physical Make, not an abstraction. The point of the card is fan-out: leadership in optics, comms, lithography, and biotech all draw from this one reservoir.
(3) Nuance: Wavelength (694.3 nm) and rod size (1 cm × 2 cm) are consistent across Wikipedia and RP-Photonics; flag that exact rod dimensions vary slightly by source. Attribution is contested in the larger sense (Townes vs. Gould patent fights), but the *first operating laser* on 16 May 1960 is undisputedly Maiman's. The 1958 paper is titled "Infrared and Optical Masers" and used the word maser; "laser" came later.
