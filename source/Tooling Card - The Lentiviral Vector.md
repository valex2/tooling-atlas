*Make · La Jolla, California, USA · 1990s*
**Luigi Naldini, Inder Verma and Didier Trono** · a disarmed HIV that carries a gene

**Front.** In April 1996 Luigi Naldini, a postdoc in Inder Verma's lab at the Salk Institute in La Jolla, published a way past a limit everyone in gene therapy had worked around for a decade. To put a gene into a cell you sent it in aboard a tamed mouse virus, and the mouse virus could only hand its cargo over while the cell was splitting in two. Most cells in a body never split. Neurons, liver, muscle and resting blood cells were all shut to the tool. Naldini's group went instead to HIV, which gets into resting cells because [its core slips through the pores in the wall around the nucleus](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1635064/) rather than waiting for that wall to dissolve. They pulled the virus apart and split its genes across three separate rings of DNA, threw away the genes that make it a killer, put the gene they wanted to deliver on one ring alone, and swapped the outer coat for a protein borrowed from a cattle virus so the particle would stick to almost anything. Dripped into a dish of human kidney cells, the three rings made [particles that carried the cargo and could not copy themselves](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.272.5259.263), better than a billion of them per millilitre of broth. Injected into an adult rat brain, the marker gene switched on in neurons and stayed on for months.

**Back.** The [three-ring recipe](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8402758/) went into general lab use within a few years, and got safer with each round as more of HIV's own genes were cut out. Carl June's group at Penn used it to write a cancer-hunting receptor into a patient's own T cells, and in August 2017 that treatment became [Kymriah, the first cell therapy the FDA approved](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10294746/). Naldini went home to Milan and ran the institute that used the same kind of vector on blood stem cells to treat children with metachromatic leukodystrophy, a disease that strips the coating off their nerves. Europe [cleared that therapy in December 2020](https://ir.orchard-tx.com/news-releases/news-release-details/orchard-therapeutics-receives-ec-approval-libmeldytm-treatment) and the [FDA followed in March 2024](https://ir.orchard-tx.com/news-releases/news-release-details/orchard-therapeutics-receives-fda-approval-lenmeldytm/); it sells in America as Lenmeldy at [$4.25 million a dose](https://www.technologyreview.com/2024/03/20/1089996/there-is-a-new-most-expensive-drug-in-the-world-price-tag-4-25-million/), the priciest drug in the world.
