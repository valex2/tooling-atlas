*Make · Tokyo, Japan · 1990s*
**Akira Yoshino, John Goodenough, Stanley Whittingham (Sony)** · a rechargeable lithium-ion cell

**Front.** In the 1970s, working at Exxon, [Stanley Whittingham](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/chemistry/2019/press-release/) showed that lithium ions could slide in and out of a layered material and be pushed back out again, so a battery could be recharged. The lithium-metal version kept catching fire, so it never sold. In 1980 [John Goodenough](https://news.uchicago.edu/story/john-b-goodenough-shares-nobel-prize-invention-lithium-ion-battery), then at Oxford, swapped in a cathode made of lithium cobalt oxide that held a much higher voltage. In 1985 [Akira Yoshino](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/chemistry/2019/popular-information/) paired that cathode with a carbon anode so no raw lithium metal sat in the cell, which made it safe to sell. The new thing was a cell where lithium ions just shuttle between two solid hosts as it charges and drains, storing close to [twice the energy per kilogram](https://www.smithsonianmag.com/smart-news/development-lithium-ion-battery-earns-nobel-prize-chemistry-180973310/) of the nickel-cadmium cells of the day.

**Back.** [Sony shipped the first commercial cell in 1991](https://www.batterydesign.net/sony-lithium-ion-batteries-worlds-first-commercialized-lib-1991/) to power its Handycam camcorders, and the chemistry then went into phones, laptops, and electric cars. The three inventors shared the [2019 Nobel Prize in Chemistry](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/chemistry/2019/press-release/). The science came out of the US and UK and the first product came out of Japan, but the cell factories moved to China: in 2025 [CATL and BYD together held about 55.6% of the global EV battery market](https://cnevpost.com/2026/02/04/global-ev-battery-market-share-2025/), CATL alone near 39%. The idea was born in the West; today two Chinese firms build more than half the world's cells.
