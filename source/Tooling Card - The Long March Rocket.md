*Make · China · 1970s*
**Qian Xuesen & CALT** · a war missile stretched to orbit

**Front.** In the spring of 1970, [Qian Xuesen](https://aerospace.caltech.edu/about/legends-of-galcit/qian-xuesen-tsien-hsue-shen) ran China's rocket program, fifteen years after the United States, suspecting the JPL co-founder of communism, [put him on a boat home](https://www.usni.org/magazines/naval-history/2025/december/missile-genius-america-lost-and-china-gained). Mao wanted a satellite, but China's farthest-flying missile, the two-stage [DF-4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Long_March_1), could only throw a warhead in a high arc and let it fall. Engineers at the rocket academy in Beijing stacked a third stage on top of that missile, a drum of solid fuel spun like a top so it would fly true, and the extra push made the difference between falling back and staying up. On [24 April 1970](https://www.chinadaily.com.cn/a/202312/10/WS65753129a31040ac301a704b.html) the stack, named Long March 1, rose from the Gobi desert and set a [173-kilogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dong_Fang_Hong_1) metal ball in orbit, where it played "The East Is Red" to radios below — the [fifth country](https://www.cnsa.gov.cn/english/n6465652/n6465653/c6480475/content.html) to reach orbit, on a rocket built at home.

**Back.** The ball sang for [20 days](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dong_Fang_Hong_1) and went silent; the rocket line kept flying. The academies in Beijing and Shanghai stretched the same missile stages into bigger Long Marches, which carried [China's first astronaut in 2003](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shenzhou_5) and flew the family's [500th mission](https://www.chinadaily.com.cn/a/202312/10/WS65753129a31040ac301a704b.html) in December 2023, at a success rate near 97 percent. China now marks 24 April as its national [Space Day](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_Day_of_China), and the pads at Jiuquan that fired the first Long March still launch its newest.
