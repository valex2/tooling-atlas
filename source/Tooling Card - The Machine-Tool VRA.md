*Manufacture · United States · 1980s*
**Houdaille, the NMTBA, Reagan administration** · trade restraint, the 1986 VRA

**Front.** In [May 1982](https://www.washingtonpost.com/archive/business/1982/12/23/reagan-urged-to-end-machine-tool-breaks/4373eee9-7919-4435-9272-cba8312eb173/), a machine-tool maker in Florida named [Houdaille Industries](https://grokipedia.com/page/houdaille_industries) was losing its home market to cheaper Japanese machines and wanted Washington to fight back. It asked the Reagan administration to strip the tax break American buyers got for [buying those imports](https://www.washingtonpost.com/archive/business/1982/12/23/reagan-urged-to-end-machine-tool-breaks/4373eee9-7919-4435-9272-cba8312eb173/). The next year the trade group for the whole industry went further: in [March 1983](https://www.cia.gov/readingroom/docs/CIA-RDP85-01156R000300380004-0.pdf) it filed a [national-security petition](https://www.jec.senate.gov/reports/98th%20Congress/The%20Machine%20Tool%20Industry%20and%20the%20Defense%20Industrial%20Base%20(1210).pdf) asking the government to cap imports at [17.5 percent](https://www.cato.org/policy-analysis/protectionism-or-national-security-use-abuse-section-232) of the market for five years. What was new was the paper, not a machine: the United States was declaring, on the record, that knowing how to build these machines was a thing it could not afford to lose.

**Back.** It was already lost. The skill had drifted overseas during the years of arguing, and between 1981 and 1983 the country [slid from the world's top machine-tool builder to third](https://www.construction-physics.com/p/what-happened-to-the-us-machine-tool), behind Japan and Germany. On [December 16, 1986](https://www.reaganlibrary.gov/archives/speech/statement-revitalization-machine-tool-industry) Reagan turned down hard quotas and instead got Japan and Taiwan to [hold back their exports](https://www.nber.org/system/files/chapters/c6710/c6710.pdf) for five years. It did almost nothing: by 1986 imports were [three-quarters of the computer-run machines](https://www.construction-physics.com/p/what-happened-to-the-us-machine-tool) sold in America, industry jobs had [fallen by half](https://www.construction-physics.com/p/what-happened-to-the-us-machine-tool) by 1991, and the lead never came back. You can wall off a craft, but you cannot tariff one back once the hands that held it have moved on.

*Curator's note:* **Non-tool exception:** this card is a deliberate parable (an absence of a tool / a trade policy), kept to mark that point in the thread. It is not a Make tool. **Placement.** Goal 2, Kind "Make," United States, as the policy negative / parable card; mechanism is pace-layers — a slow-layer capability (tacit machine-building craft) cannot be regrown on the fast clock of trade policy. Confidence in the back: **Direct.** The rank slide (1st→3rd, 1981-83), the 75%-of-NC-machines import figure (1986), the halving of employment by 1991, and the Dec 16 1986 VRA decision are all documented; the one number to watch is the 17.5% cap, which is the *requested* level in the NMTBA petition, not an enacted one (the VRA used different product-by-product limits). Stated as "asking to cap," which is accurate.

**Analysis.** This is the deck's clean inversion: every other "Make" card lands on someone grinding, pouring, or wiring the new thing into being, and the lead compounds. Here the only thing "made" is a petition, and the lead is already gone before the ink dries. It rhymes with other Western policy responses to a migrating industry that fail from opposite ends. This one walls a craft in *after* it is gone, a supply lever pulled too late, while subsidy schemes try to grow a craft by buying its output, a demand lever at the wrong end. It also rhymes with the durable-moat cards (van Leeuwenhoek's lost lens skill that waited 200 years is the *accidental* loss; this is the *institutional* failure to notice the loss until too late). Houdaille and the NMTBA carry the whole arc, and the villain is the calendar. The so-what column wants the lesson, not a win.

**Nuance.** Two fiddly points kept off the face. (1) Houdaille's was a §103 tax-credit petition routed through USTR, distinct from the NMTBA's §232 national-security petition — I compressed them as one escalating alarm because the card's point is the sequence, not the statutory plumbing. (2) The §232 investigation did reach a *positive* national-security finding (rare; one of the only non-petroleum positive 232s of the era), but Reagan still declined the quotas — worth a footnote if a reviewer probes "did the government agree it was strategic?" Yes, and it still didn't matter, which only sharpens the card.
