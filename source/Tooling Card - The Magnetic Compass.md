*Measure · Song China · 1080s*
**Shen Kuo (Song China)** · magnetized needle that finds south

**Front.** Around [1088](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dream_Pool_Essays), a Song official named [Shen Kuo](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Shen-Kuo), living in retirement in his garden near the Yangtze, set down what he knew in a book he called the Dream Pool Essays. Travelers still lost their way whenever clouds hid the sun by day and the stars by night. Shen described how to rub an iron needle against a piece of [lodestone](https://www.aaas.org/membership/scientia/shen-kuo-first-renaissance-man) until the needle pulled toward the south, then float it on water or hang it from a single strand of silk so it could turn freely. He added one careful note: the needle never pointed straight south but always leaned a little to the [east](https://www.aaas.org/membership/scientia/shen-kuo-first-renaissance-man), the first written record that a magnetized needle holds a steady heading of its own.

**Back.** Shen Kuo only wrote the needle down; a generation later, around 1119, [Zhu Yu's Pingzhou Table Talks](https://www.historyofinformation.com/detail.php?entryid=270) recorded sailors on the South China Sea steering by such a needle when overcast skies hid the stars. The small tool then traveled west: by about [1190](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_the_compass) an English monk named Alexander Neckam wrote of the same spinning needle guiding ships off Europe's coasts. The lead in open-water navigation went with it, and a few centuries on European crews used the needle to cross oceans that no Song fleet had ever set out to sail.
