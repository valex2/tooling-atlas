*Manufacture · Waltham, Massachusetts, USA · 1940s*
**Percy Spencer** · copper discs punched on a press and brazed into a stack

**Front.** [Percy Spencer](https://www.lindahall.org/about/news/scientist-of-the-day/percy-spencer/) left grammar school as a boy to help feed his family and learned electricity out of borrowed books. By 1941 he ran the power tube shop at [Raytheon](https://www.fundinguniverse.com/company-histories/raytheon-company-history/) in Waltham, Massachusetts, where his men were building the new British magnetron the only way anyone knew how. Take a solid lump of copper, drill a ring of holes through it, then ream and file every hole true. Skilled hands, a slow bench, [seventeen tubes a day](https://www.invent.org/inductees/percy-l-spencer), against a Navy that wanted them by the thousand. Spencer stopped treating the anode as a lump and started treating it as a stack: he had thin copper discs [punched out on presses](https://www.fundinguniverse.com/company-histories/raytheon-company-history/) with the hole pattern already cut into each one, the way a cutter stamps out cookies, then piled the discs up and brazed them into a single block. The [soldered wires inside came out too, replaced by one solid ring](https://www.invent.org/inductees/percy-l-spencer).

**Back.** The shop went from seventeen tubes a day to [2,600](https://www.invent.org/inductees/percy-l-spencer). The Navy put [$2 million into a bigger Waltham plant](https://www.fundinguniverse.com/company-histories/raytheon-company-history/), nearly every US Navy ship carried Raytheon radar by 1944, and Raytheon finished the war having built about [80 percent of the million or so magnetrons](https://www.fundinguniverse.com/company-histories/raytheon-company-history/) behind American and British radar. Britain had drawn the tube and hand-built [twelve of them at GEC in August 1940](https://blog.sciencemuseum.org.uk/1940s-the-cavity-magnetron/); it spent the rest of the war fitting American ones. Spencer's stamped tube outlived the war in an odder way: standing near one running on his bench, he found the candy bar in his pocket had gone soft, and [filed for the microwave oven in October 1945](https://www.lindahall.org/about/news/scientist-of-the-day/percy-spencer/).
