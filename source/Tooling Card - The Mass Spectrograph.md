*Measure · Cambridge, Britain · 1910s*
**Francis Aston** · a balance for single atoms

**Front.** Back in 1913 at the Cavendish Laboratory, [J. J. Thomson had found something odd: the gas neon seemed to come in two atomic weights at once](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Francis-William-Aston), about 20 and about 22. His assistant [Francis Aston](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/chemistry/1922/aston/biographical/) wanted to know whether neon was really two different things mixed together, and to settle it he had to weigh atoms one kind at a time. In [1919 he built the mass spectrograph](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Francis-William-Aston): fire charged atoms through electric and magnetic fields that bend the light ones more than the heavy ones, so each mass lands at its own spot on a photographic plate. Neon split cleanly into two lines, mass 20 and mass 22, the same element in two weights. Here at last was a balance for single atoms, fine enough to weigh the different kinds and show that an element is a family of masses, the isotopes.

**Back.** Aston went on to find [212 of the naturally occurring isotopes and a "whole-number rule"](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Francis-William-Aston) that pointed straight at the structure of the nucleus, and took the [Nobel Prize in chemistry in 1922](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/chemistry/1922/aston/facts/). Weighing atoms by mass became a universal way to ask what something is made of: it fixed the atomic weights of chemistry, it underlies dating rocks and the separation of isotopes, and today it identifies proteins and drugs by their mass in labs everywhere.
