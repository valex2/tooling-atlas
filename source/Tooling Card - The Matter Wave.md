*Model · Paris, France · 1924*
**Louis de Broglie** · the matter-wave idea

**Front.** In 1924, a French prince named [Louis de Broglie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_de_Broglie), late to physics and still finishing his doctorate in Paris, turned a question over in his head. Physicists had just swallowed a strange fact: light, long thought a pure wave, also arrives in little packets that act like particles. De Broglie asked it backwards. If a wave can act like a particle, can a particle act like a wave? In his thesis he wrote down [a short rule that gives every moving thing a wavelength](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter_wave), and for an electron the wave came out tens of thousands of times shorter than light.

**Back.** Three years later, two physicists at Bell Labs, [Clinton Davisson and Lester Germer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Davisson%E2%80%93Germer_experiment), bounced electrons off a nickel crystal and watched them ripple and interfere, exactly as de Broglie's rule said a wave should. Once an electron counted as a wave with a tiny wavelength, [Abbe's wall of light](https://www.britannica.com/science/Abbe-limit) could be stepped around. In 1931 a German engineer named [Ernst Ruska](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ernst-Ruska) began building a microscope that sees with electrons instead of light. The idea was a few free pages from a French prince, the proof was American, the tool was German. De Broglie kept the Nobel; the electron microscope grew up in Ruska's Berlin, and from there in the German optical industry that built the first machines anyone could buy.
