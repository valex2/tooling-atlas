*Manufacture · Geoje, South Korea · 2000s*
**Samsung Heavy Industries' dock and crane engineers** · ten huge hull blocks, welded afloat

**Front.** By 2001 the yards on Geoje island had more tanker orders than holes to build them in. A dry dock is a pit in the ground, and digging one takes years and a great deal of money. What the yard did have was a [floating dock, a steel box that sinks under a ship and lifts it out of the water](https://www.businesskorea.co.kr/news/articleView.html?idxno=18776), which every yard in the world used for repairs and nobody used to build. On [12 January 2002 a new ship came out of one](https://v.daum.net/v/M0MuzfdawD). Later that year the crews changed what was lifted as well as where: instead of stitching a tanker together from about a hundred small blocks, they welded [blocks of hull weighing 2,500 tonnes and up, five or six times the old size, and set ten of them into the dock with a 3,000-tonne floating crane](https://blog.samsungshi.com/575). Pipes, wiring and ladders went inside each block on shore, where a man can stand up straight and reach his work.

**Back.** Fitting a ship out on the ground and joining it afloat cut the time a hull spent in the dock [from about three months to under one and a half](https://blog.samsungshi.com/575), so a dock turned twice as often for the price of a crane. The method was Japanese before it was Korean: [Kure's engineers had worked out block building and zone outfitting under an American lease in the 1950s](https://www.construction-physics.com/p/how-japan-invented-modern-shipbuilding), and Korean yards took that and pushed the blocks until only a sea crane could lift them. [Korea passed Japan in 2001](https://www.globalsecurity.org/military/world/rok/industry-shipbuilding.htm) and took [roughly 70 percent of the world's orders in 2009 and 2010](https://www.globalsecurity.org/military/world/rok/industry-shipbuilding.htm). Then Chinese yards bought floating docks and heavy-lift cranes of their own, [passed Korea in output by 2010](https://cepr.org/voxeu/columns/industrial-policy-lessons-shipbuilding), and by 2024 Korea was second with [about 27 percent of completions](https://www.statista.com/statistics/263895/shipbuilding-nations-worldwide-by-cgt/). The crane and the dock were orderable. The join was not: a 2,500-tonne block outfitted on shore has to meet its neighbour within a few millimetres after the welds have pulled the steel in, so a yard cannot lift big until it has the [measuring and accuracy-control crews](https://www.construction-physics.com/p/how-japan-invented-modern-shipbuilding) to predict the shrink. Building those crews took the Chinese yards most of the eight years. Korea still runs the method and builds the harder ships on it; what it lost was the volume.
