*Model · Greifswald, Germany · 1900s*
**Gustav Mie** · an exact solution for light hitting a sphere

**Front.** In 1908 [Gustav Mie](https://www.photoniques.com/articles/photon/pdf/2020/02/photon2020101p22.pdf), a professor at the small Baltic university of [Greifswald](https://www.photoniques.com/articles/photon/pdf/2020/02/photon2020101p22.pdf), set his student [Walter Steubing to measure why suspensions of tiny gold specks glow ruby-red](https://www.photoniques.com/articles/photon/pdf/2020/02/photon2020101p22.pdf). Forty years earlier [Lord Rayleigh had worked out the blue of the sky](https://www.britannica.com/science/Rayleigh-scattering): specks far smaller than a light wave scatter the short blue end [much harder than the red, by the fourth power of the wavelength](https://www.britannica.com/science/Rayleigh-scattering). But the gold beads were about as wide as the light itself, and Rayleigh's small-speck rule gave the wrong colors. So Mie went back to [Maxwell's equations for light](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/andp.19083300302) and solved them straight through for a single sphere of any size and any material, writing the scattered light as an endless sum of simpler waves. He had [no computer, so by hand he could only add up the first three terms of the sum](https://scattport.org/index.php/mie-theory-1908-2008). What he had was the whole answer on paper, not just the limit for the very smallest specks.

**Back.** The full sum was punishing to add by hand, and [Mie's 69-page paper sat almost unread for close to forty years](https://www.photoniques.com/articles/photon/pdf/2020/02/photon2020101p22.pdf). When [computers arrived in the late 1940s the sum turned easy](https://www.photoniques.com/articles/photon/pdf/2020/02/photon2020101p22.pdf), and researchers reaching for a way to read particle size from scattered light turned to Mie's formulas. Today the [international standard for laser particle sizing names Mie theory as its method](https://www.malvernpanalytical.com/en/products/technology/light-scattering/laser-diffraction), and the same math sits inside aerosol optics, lidar readings of the air, cell-counting flow machines, and the scattering terms in climate models. Neither Mie nor anyone around him thought the paper mattered; he lived just long enough to watch a [nearly forgotten piece of his early career gather tens of thousands of citations](https://www.photoniques.com/articles/photon/pdf/2020/02/photon2020101p22.pdf). The tool did not win by being used. It waited for the machine that could run it.
