*Make · Philadelphia, USA · 2000s*
**Katalin Karikó** · one swapped RNA letter

**Front.** RNA is written in four letters, the way DNA is, and in a lab you can spell out an [mRNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Messenger_RNA) message letter by letter. For years, though, if you made that mRNA and put it into the body, the body's alarm system saw it as an invader and lit up with dangerous inflammation, so it was useless as a medicine. In [2005](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Katalin_Karik%C3%B3) a scientist at the University of Pennsylvania named [Katalin Karikó](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Katalin_Karik%C3%B3), working with Drew Weissman, swapped one of those letters, uridine, for a close cousin called pseudouridine. The alarm stopped firing, and the cell read the message and made the protein it asked for. The new thing was a small chemical edit that made lab-made mRNA safe to use in a person.

**Back.** That same edit sits inside the [Pfizer and Moderna COVID vaccines](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/medicine/2023/summary/), which between them reached billions of people, and it won Karikó and Weissman the [2023 Nobel Prize in Medicine](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/medicine/2023/summary/). The tool was sound for nearly twenty years before the world needed it. In that time the funding system nearly threw it away: Karikó was [demoted at Penn in 1995 and, by her own account, in almost forty years 'didn't even get an R01 grant'](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8713962/), and only small, soft money kept the work breathing.
