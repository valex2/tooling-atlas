*Make · Dudley, England · 1710s*
**Thomas Newcomen** · an engine driven by air

**Front.** In 1712, an ironmonger from Dartmouth named [Thomas Newcomen](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Thomas-Newcomen) set up a strange new machine over a flooded coal pit near [Dudley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newcomen_atmospheric_engine). Mines filled with water as they went deeper, and teams of horses could barely haul it back out. His machine let steam fill a tall [cylinder](https://www.britannica.com/technology/Newcomen-steam-engine), then a squirt of cold water inside turned that steam back to water and left almost nothing there, so the weight of the open air pushed the piston down and a rocking beam lifted the water out of the pit. Steam had filled the cylinder, but it was the empty space and the plain weight of the air that did the pulling.

**Back.** [Nearly a hundred](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Thomas-Newcomen) of these engines were draining British mines by the time Newcomen died in 1729, and they spread to coal and copper works across Europe. [Thomas Savery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Savery) had patented a steam pump in 1698 with no piston at all, so Newcomen had to work under Savery's older patent, but the cylinder and the moving piston were his own. Sixty years on, [James Watt](https://www.britannica.com/biography/James-Watt) saw how much steam was wasted heating and cooling the same cylinder over and over, added a separate vessel to do the cooling, and [roughly halved](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Watt_steam_engine) the coal it burned. The lead in steam then passed from the mine mechanic to Watt and his Birmingham partner, yet every later engine still worked the way Newcomen's had, by making a vacuum and letting the air push.
