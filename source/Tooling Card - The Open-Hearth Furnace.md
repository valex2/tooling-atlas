*Manufacture · Germany · 1860s*
**Carl Wilhelm Siemens & Pierre-Émile Martin** · furnace fed by its own waste heat

**Front.** In 1865, [Pierre-Émile Martin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre-%C3%89mile_Martin), who ran his father's small ironworks at Sireuil in western France, wanted steel he could trust: a Bessemer blast was over in twenty minutes, done or spoiled before anyone could test the metal, and the converter could not melt cold scrap at all. He took a [licence](https://www.gracesguide.co.uk/Siemens_Process) from [Carl Wilhelm Siemens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Wilhelm_Siemens), a German-born engineer in England whose furnace sent its hot exhaust down through chambers stacked with open brickwork, then reversed the flow so the incoming air and gas rode over the glowing bricks and reached the flame already hot. Heat that used to go up the chimney came back into the fire, enough by Siemens' own count to save [70 to 80 percent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open-hearth_furnace) of the fuel, and the flame burned hot enough to hold a shallow open pool of iron molten all day. Martin charged pig iron and scrap together, let the bath cook for [eight hours](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open-hearth_furnace), dipped out test samples as it went, and corrected the charge before he poured.

**Back.** A heat you could test was a heat you could certify, so mills making plate and structural steel switched over; by 1911 the open hearth poured [two thirds of American steel](https://www.construction-physics.com/p/the-rise-of-steel-part-ii). It held most of the world's output for nearly a century, until mills at [Linz in Austria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basic_oxygen_steelmaking) started blowing pure oxygen down onto the melt in 1952 and finished in under an hour what the hearth took a full shift to do. The old furnaces died slowly; in 2024 the [Zaporizhstal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open-hearth_furnace) mill in Ukraine was still pouring steel from seven 500-ton open hearths.
