*Manufacture · Osaka, Japan · 1950s*
**Akio Shindo** · ovens that bake acrylic yarn into carbon

**Front.** In April 1959 Akio Shindo had been at a government lab in Osaka for seven years, working in the carbon division, when an industrial newspaper told him that America's National Carbon Company was [making carbon fibre out of rayon](https://www.wipo.int/en/web/ip-advantage/w/stories/a-patent-that-changed-an-industry). Rayon mostly burned off. Less than a fifth of the thread you put in came out the far end as carbon. Shindo tried acrylic textile yarn instead, the stuff sweaters are made of, and rather than sealing it in a furnace he held it in [open air at about 250°C](https://www.wipo.int/en/web/ip-advantage/w/stories/a-patent-that-changed-an-industry) for half an hour to two hours before taking it up to roughly 1,000°C. Half or more of the yarn survived as carbon. He filed the patent that September, and by 1961 his lab was reporting fibre with a stiffness [above 140 GPa](https://www.acs.org/education/whatischemistry/landmarks/carbonfibers.html), several times what rayon gave.

**Back.** Shindo's institute licensed Tokai Electrode and Nippon Carbon around 1961, and neither could spin its own acrylic yarn, so the fibre stayed small. At Farnborough in 1963 [William Watt held the yarn under tension while it oxidised](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_fibers), which lines the polymer chains up along the fibre and pushes the stiffness higher, and Britain licensed the method to Rolls-Royce, Morganite and Courtaulds. Rolls-Royce put carbon fan blades in a jet engine in 1968, found that birds smashed them, and was nationalised in 1971; Morganite walked away; Courtaulds could not keep impurities out of its water-based process and [stopped making fibre in 1991](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_fibers). Toray took a licence in 1970, spun its own precursor yarn, and [began selling in 1971](https://www.cf-composites.toray/aboutus/global/). A modern line pulls the yarn through four to six stacked ovens and then furnaces at 1200 to 1500°C under nitrogen, and on aerospace tow the [yield varies by about three percent run to run](https://www.compositesworld.com/articles/the-making-of-carbon-fiber); the chemistry is printed everywhere, while the dwell time for each grade and the oven airflow tuned to one precursor's own heat of reaction are not. Boeing's 787 flies on wings and a body built from Toray prepreg, under a contract the two firms extended in 2015 to [more than 11 billion dollars](https://www.toraycma.com/toray-extends-comprehensive-agreement-with-boeing-to-supply-carbon-fiber-torayca-prepreg/).
