*Make · Sydney, Australia · 1980s*
**Martin Green & UNSW** · a solar cell with a mirrored back

**Front.** In late 1988, in a lab at the University of New South Wales in Sydney, a professor named [Martin Green](https://www.unsw.edu.au/staff/martin-green) was fighting a leak at the back of the solar cell. Sunlight knocks electrons loose inside the silicon, and a good cell gathers them, but at the sheet of metal covering the back of every cell then made, loose electrons died on touch, and light that got that far was swallowed too. His colleagues Andrew Blakers, Aihua Wang and Jianhua Zhao grew a [thin skin of glass](https://pv-manufacturing.org/perc-solar-cells/) across the back and let the metal touch the silicon only through tiny openings in it, so the back stopped eating electrons and began bouncing missed light back through the cell for a second pass. In [March 1989](https://millenniumprize.org/news-articles/news/q-a-with-scientia-professor-martin-green-of-the-unsw-sydney-australia-winner-of-the-2022-millennium-technology-prize/) their cell was measured turning [22.8 percent](https://pubs.aip.org/aip/apl/article-abstract/55/13/1363/55817/22-8-efficient-silicon-solar-cell) of sunlight into electricity, more than any silicon cell ever had, and the whole gain had come out of the glassy back.

**Back.** The paper named the design the passivated emitter and rear cell, PERC, and for a quarter century it stayed a lab champion, because no factory could put the glassy back on cheaply. Around [2012](https://millenniumprize.org/news-articles/news/q-a-with-scientia-professor-martin-green-of-the-unsw-sydney-australia-winner-of-the-2022-millennium-technology-prize/) coating machines and lasers learned to lay the layer and cut the openings by the million, and by 2021 PERC was [91 percent](https://millenniumprize.org/news-articles/news/q-a-with-scientia-professor-martin-green-of-the-unsw-sydney-australia-winner-of-the-2022-millennium-technology-prize/) of world silicon module production. The factories rose in China, where Green's student [Zhengrong Shi](https://www.acap.org.au/post/from-lab-to-global-industry-lessons-from-the-founders-of-the-solar-industry-scientia-professor-mar) went home to found Suntech in 2001 and grew it into the world's largest panel maker by 2009. China now builds [more than 80 percent](https://www.iea.org/reports/solar-pv-global-supply-chains) of the world's panels; Australia, which drew the cell inside [nine of every ten](https://www.unsw.edu.au/newsroom/news/2026/06/father-of-modern-solar-approaches-the-next-frontier) of them, makes almost none.
