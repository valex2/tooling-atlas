*Make · Sriharikota, India · 1990s*
**ISRO** · four-stage polar satellite launcher

**Front.** In September 1993, engineers on [Sriharikota](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satish_Dhawan_Space_Centre), an island off India's east coast, watched their new rocket wobble as its stages came apart and fall into the Bay of Bengal, brought down [twelve minutes in](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PSLV-D1) by an overflow in its guidance software. India was already building good Earth-watching satellites; it just had to [pay Moscow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IRS-1A) to carry them up. The team under [G. Madhavan Nair](https://www.lpsc.gov.in/madhavannair.html) had stacked four stages that took turns burning solid and liquid fuel, the liquid engine earned from France with [100 man-years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vikas_%28rocket_engine%29) of Indian engineering work; they patched the software and stacked the whole thing again. On [15 October 1994](https://www.isro.gov.in/mission_PSLV_D2.html) the rocket, the PSLV, set an [804-kg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IRS-P2) imaging satellite into a polar orbit 817 km up, and India owned its ride to space.

**Back.** The fixed rocket became the one everyone booked, carrying India's first [Moon probe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chandrayaan-1) in 2008 and its [Mars probe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars_Orbiter_Mission) in 2013 while foreign satellite owners bought the spare seats. On [15 February 2017](https://www.isro.gov.in/SuccessfullyLaunches104.html) a single flight released [104 satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PSLV-C37), tripling Russia's old record, and 88 of them were [shoebox cameras from Planet](https://www.planet.com/pulse/planet-launches-satellite-constellation-to-image-the-whole-planet-daily/) of San Francisco, an American company paying India for the ride. A Falcon 9 [took the record](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transporter-1) in 2021 with 143, but the rocket kept flying: [58 good flights out of 63](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_Satellite_Launch_Vehicle), still lifting from the island where the first one fell.
