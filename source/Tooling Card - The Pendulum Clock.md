*Measure · The Hague, Netherlands · 1650s*
**Christiaan Huygens** · a pendulum regulating a clock

**Front.** On Christmas Day 1656, [Christiaan Huygens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens) sketched out a clock with a hanging weight that swung back and forth. The clocks of his day drifted by [about 15 minutes a day](https://www.aps.org/apsnews/2017/06/huygens-patents-first-pendulum-clock), too sloppy to time a falling body or fix a ship's place at sea. Half a century earlier [Galileo had noticed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendulum_clock) that a swing of a given length always takes the same time to go and come back, but no one had tied that even beat to a clock's gears. Huygens did: he let the swing release the gear train one tooth per beat, so the swing set the pace, and the new clock lost [about 15 seconds a day](https://www.historyofinformation.com/detail.php?entryid=3506), sixty times steadier than anything before it.

**Back.** A Hague clockmaker, [Salomon Coster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salomon_Coster), built the first working ones in 1657 under licence from Huygens, and the design spread fast across Europe. Astronomers now had a clock they could read a planet against, and a steady beat became the bench tool of physics; the pendulum clock stayed [the most accurate timekeeper for nearly 300 years](https://www.aps.org/apsnews/2017/06/huygens-patents-first-pendulum-clock), until quartz in the 1930s. The one place it failed was the sea: [a ship's rocking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens) threw the swing off, and finding longitude by clock had to wait for the spring-driven chronometer a century later.

*Curator's note:* **Placement.** Measure cell, Goal Both, Convergence, Direct confidence; the moment is dated and the numbers are clean across museum and physics-society sources. **Analysis.** A foundational Measure card and the head of the Timekeeping thread: it makes time a quantity you can trust, the way the early microscopes and telescopes make the small and the far legible. Pairs with the lens cards as a parade of 1600s instruments that put numbers on what people could only eyeball before. **Nuance.** The 15-minute figure is the conventional "before" baseline rather than a single measured clock; a few sources round the gain to 10 seconds, but 15 minutes to 15 seconds (the ~60-fold figure) is the standard pairing. Galileo's prior pendulum observation is real and not hidden; the tool here is the regulator that joined swing to gear train, not the swing itself.
