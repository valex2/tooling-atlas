*Measure · Groningen, Netherlands · 1930s*
**Frits Zernike** · a ring that slows straight light

**Front.** In 1930, Frits Zernike, a physics professor in [Groningen](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/physics/1953/zernike/facts/), was puzzling over faint extra lines that showed up beside the bright ones when he shone light through a [diffraction grating](https://www.rug.nl/fse/nobelprize-zernike/zernike_s-research-in-physical-optics-?lang=en). He worked out that the light in these "ghost" lines lagged the main light by a quarter of a wave, a shift no eye can see. So he built a thin ring of material into a microscope that slowed the straight-through light by that same quarter wave, lining it up with the light bent around a cell so the two would add or cancel. A plain [see-through cell](https://www.microscopyu.com/techniques/phase-contrast/introduction-to-phase-contrast-microscopy), invisible under an ordinary microscope, now stood out in light and dark with no dye at all.

**Back.** He had shown the trick to the lens-makers at [Zeiss](https://zeiss-campus.magnet.fsu.edu/articles/basics/historical.html) in Jena in 1932, and one of their oldest hands told him, "[If this had any practical value, we would ourselves have invented it long ago.](https://www.nobelprize.org/uploads/2018/06/zernike-lecture.pdf)" No microscope came of it for years. Only in the Second World War did Zeiss finally grind the [phase rings](https://zeiss-campus.magnet.fsu.edu/referencelibrary/phasecontrast.html) and build the first working instruments; by 1943 one of their films showed a living cell splitting in two, watched for the first time without a stain to kill it. Ten years on, the method Zeiss had waved off won [Zernike the Nobel Prize](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/physics/1953/summary/).
