*Manufacture · Canada · 1940s*
**Lloyd Montgomery Pidgeon** · vacuum retorts for magnesium metal

**Front.** In 1941, Canada was at war and had no way to make magnesium, the light metal in aircraft parts and flares. At the National Research Council, a chemist named [Lloyd Pidgeon](https://mininghalloffame.ca/lloyd-m-pidgeon/) went looking for a route that did not need a giant plant running electric current through molten salt. He roasted crushed [dolomite, a common rock, mixed the powder with ground ferrosilicon](https://pubs.acs.org/doi/abs/10.1021/cen-v020n006.p369), pressed the mix into small bricks, and slid them into steel tubes called retorts; then he pumped out the air and held the tubes at about [1,200 °C](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pidgeon_process). In the vacuum, magnesium boiled out of the rock and froze at each retort's cool end as a crown of nearly pure metal, so the whole plant was rows of pipes in a furnace, and by [October 1941](https://www.mindat.org/loc-14742.html) his pilot retorts were turning out metal.

**Back.** The Canadian government paid for a full plant at [Haley Station, Ontario](https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-981-16-2171-0_2), in 1942, and [six plants](https://mininghalloffame.ca/lloyd-m-pidgeon/) across North America ran Pidgeon retorts before the war ended. Afterward the batch tubes looked slow next to big electrolytic plants and the process nearly died out; in the 1990s small smelters in China's Shaanxi coal country picked it up, because a retort plant is cheap to build and runs on coal. By 2023 China made about [85 percent](https://www.lightmetalage.com/news/industry-news/magnesium/global-magnesium-market-supply-demand-and-future-outlook/) of the world's magnesium, nearly all of it in Pidgeon retorts, with Shaanxi alone pouring close to half the world's supply, while the first Pidgeon plant at Haley [shut in 2008](https://www.theglobeandmail.com/report-on-business/timminco-shuts-ontario-magnesium-plant/article674120/) after more than sixty years.
