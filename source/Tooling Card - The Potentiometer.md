*Measure · Berlin, Germany · 1840s*
**Johann Christian Poggendorff** · balance wire for reading voltage

**Front.** In [1841](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/andp.18411301002), [Johann Christian Poggendorff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Christian_Poggendorff), a Hamburg apothecary's apprentice who had risen to edit Germany's physics journal in Berlin, faced a problem every electrician knew: the batteries of the day weakened the moment they gave current, so a meter spoiled the very number it was trying to read. He stretched a plain wire and sent a steady current down it from a separate working battery. He set the test cell against a length of that wire, through a galvanometer needle, and [slid the contact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potentiometer_(measuring_instrument)) along the wire until the needle stood dead still. At that balance the test cell gave no current at all, so its full strength could be read off the length of wire that held it, a voltage measured without ever loading it.

**Back.** The balance became the standard way to read a voltage for the next hundred years. Instrument makers boxed it: [Crompton & Co](https://chsi.emuseum.com/objects/15905/cromptontype-potentiometer) in England in the 1890s, then the [Leeds & Northrup Type K](https://physics.kenyon.edu/EarlyApparatus/Electrical_Measurements/Potentiometer/Potentiometer.html) in Philadelphia, which read to [0.00001 volts](https://physics.kenyon.edu/EarlyApparatus/Electrical_Measurements/Potentiometer/Potentiometer.html) and sat in nearly every electrical lab into the 1950s. Paired with a standard cell, the balance was how national laboratories kept the volt itself until [1987](https://www.nutsvolts.com/magazine/article/the-dc-volt), when NIST began replacing the cells with superconducting junctions; Poggendorff had printed the method in his own journal from the start, and the instrument trade it fed grew up in Chelmsford and Philadelphia, not Berlin.
