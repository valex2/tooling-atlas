*Manufacture · Mainz, Germany · 1440s*
**Johannes Gutenberg** · cast metal type, set and pressed

**Front.** Around 1440 a goldsmith in Mainz wanted to copy a page without paying a scribe to write it out by hand. A scribe took months over one book. [Johannes Gutenberg](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Johannes-Gutenberg) worked out how to cast a single letter as a small metal block, over and over, all the same height: he cut a steel punch for each letter, struck it into soft copper to make a mould, and poured a [lead, tin, and antimony](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Movable_type) alloy into an [adjustable hand mould](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Printing_press) to drop out clean type as fast as he could pour. He lined the loose letters up into a page, inked them with a sticky [oil-based ink](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Printing_press) that clung to metal, and squeezed paper against them in a screw press borrowed from winemakers. The new thing was the reusable cast letter: set a page once, then strike off copies by the hundred, break it up, and set the next.

**Back.** By 1455 he had printed his [42-line Bible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gutenberg_Bible), about 180 copies at once where a scribe would have managed one. The trade spread fast, and presses stood in [more than 250 European cities](https://www.historyofinformation.com/detail.php?id=27) within fifty years, having struck off [some 20 million books](https://www.britannica.com/topic/publishing/The-age-of-early-printing-1450-1550). The lead held: every later way of copying a page, down to printing circuits with light, copies a master onto a surface the same way the press did.

The deck's oldest card and the one the others' "copy a master at scale" logic descends from. **Analysis.** Parade card, not a hero: it anchors the [[Thread - Copying Knowledge]] lineage that runs forward through [[Tooling Card - Photolithography]], where the same set-once, strike-many move is done with light onto silicon. Contrast with the make/measure cards that produce one good thing; this one's whole point is the identical copy. **Nuance.** Copy count is contested: the 1455 Piccolomini letter cites both 158 and 180, and book totals by 1500 range from 9 to 20 million across sources. The 1440 date is the conventional start of the development; the finished Bible is 1455. Movable type predates Gutenberg in East Asia; the card is specifically his metal-type-plus-press system in Mainz. BuildsOn/Enables left empty by design; the thread carries the lineage.
