*Make · Detroit and the Midwest, USA · 1900s-20s*
**Charles Norton, the auto industry** · the heavy production grinder

**Front.** In the early 1900s a new machine came down the road, and it was hungry. The automobile needed parts by the thousand, all the same, all hard: crankshafts, camshafts, and gears cut from tough new alloy steels. That steel was too hard to cut on a lathe, and the spinning tool just wore out. So in 1900 a man named [Charles Norton](https://ledger.americanprecision.org/profile/charles-h-norton/), working in Worcester, Massachusetts, built a big heavy machine that pressed a fast-spinning [wheel of grit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grinding_machine) hard against the steel and ground it to shape and size at once. Before Norton, a grinder was a light tool that only cleaned up work a lathe had already done. His did the whole job, and on hardened steel it was the only tool for the work. One [crankshaft job](https://www.thehenryford.org/collections/explore/artifact/252348) that had taken five hours now took fifteen minutes.

**Back.** The car became the machine-tool trade's biggest buyer. After 1900 it took [25 to 30 percent of the whole industry's output](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/journal-of-economic-history/article/abs/technological-change-in-the-machine-tool-industry-18401910/BC2D1F693FDAB12D327897FF86F6B4FF). That much buying has a pull, and the buying was in Detroit and the towns near it, so the trade moved toward the cars. The old center in New England's [Connecticut Valley](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/journal-of-economic-history/article/abs/technological-change-in-the-machine-tool-industry-18401910/BC2D1F693FDAB12D327897FF86F6B4FF) faded, and Cincinnati grew into the country's machine-tool capital. The lead did not die — it picked up and walked west.

*Curator's note:* **(1) Placement.** Kind Make. Place: Detroit and the Midwest (the demand center); Norton's grinder was built in Worcester, MA, named in the front. Era 1900s-1920s, sort year 1908. Goal 2, the lead moves: this is the thread's first geographic migration, New England's Precision Valley to the Midwest. Primary mechanism: **Convergence**, the automobile (like the sewing machine before it) is an industry of origin that pulled a new general-purpose tool, the production grinder, into being; this is Rosenberg's own technological-convergence reading (1963 *Journal of Economic History*). The westward move of the center is a **Pace-layers** overtone: demand is the fast layer, the slow geography of the trade follows it over a generation. Convergence is primary because the card's engine is the auto driving a tool, not the relocation itself. Confidence Direct on the grinder, the convergence framing, and the Cincinnati shift; the 25-30% figure is a verbatim quotation but single-lineage (see nuance).

**(2) Analysis.** What it adds: it bridges the long gap between the sewing-machine convergence card (1861) and the NC/FANUC cards (1949/1956), and it is the thread's FIRST geographic migration, NE to Midwest, which rhymes with and sets up the bigger US-to-Japan jump a half-century later. It is the hero/bridge card here, not parade: without it the thread skips from one origin-industry to numerical control with no account of where the trade physically went or why. On the convergence beat it echoes the sewing-machine card, so the fresh content has to carry it: the grinder going from finishing tool to production tool (a new tool category, not just a new customer) plus the migration. If space is tight, the migration is the non-redundant payload.

**(3) Nuance.** Sibling/pair candidate: Henry Leland's 1908 Cadillac interchangeability test (the RAC/Dewar Trophy, three cars taken apart, parts mixed in a heap, reassembled and driven) is the auto-era echo of Blanc's 1785 demonstration; it belongs as its own card, not folded in here, since this card is about the grinder, not interchangeability proof. The 25-30% figure is single-lineage: it traces to Robert Woodbury, *History of the Grinding Machine* (MIT Press, 1959), quoted in Rosenberg 1963 (fn. 57); the Cambridge page reproduces it verbatim, but every modern citation runs through that one Woodbury line. The five-hours-to-fifteen-minutes crankshaft figure is from period accounts of Norton's grinder and should be treated as illustrative. Geography caveat: the maker (Norton) sat in Worcester; the demand and the new center were in Detroit and Cincinnati, and the card's claim is about where the trade's center moved, not where Norton sat.
