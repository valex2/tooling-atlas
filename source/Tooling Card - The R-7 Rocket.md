*Make · Soviet Union (Baikonur) · 1950s*
**Sergei Korolev** · the first rocket big enough to reach orbit

**Front.** [Sergei Korolev](https://www.nasa.gov/history/sputnik/korolev.html) had been beaten so badly in a Soviet prison camp that both his jaws were broken. Stalin let him out to build rockets. By 1957 he ran a secret design shop, and the public was never told his name. His job was a weapon: the [R-7](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R-7_Semyorka), a missile meant to throw a bomb at America. No one could calculate a working rocket. You built one, fired it, and watched it blow up, again and again, until it didn't. On [21 August 1957](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R-7_Semyorka) one finally flew the whole way, 6,000 kilometres to a far target. Korolev saw at once it could do something else. A thing that throws a bomb halfway around the Earth can throw a small ball [fast enough to fall around the Earth instead of into it](https://www.nasa.gov/history/65-years-ago-sputnik-ushers-in-the-space-age/). On [4 October 1957](https://www.nasa.gov/history/65-years-ago-sputnik-ushers-in-the-space-age/) he bolted a [184-pound](https://www.nasa.gov/history/65-years-ago-sputnik-ushers-in-the-space-age/) metal ball to the top and launched it from a flat steppe in Kazakhstan. What was new was not the ball. It was the rocket under it, the first one strong enough to leave the planet.

**Back.** The ball was Sputnik, and it circled overhead every 90 minutes, beeping in a voice any radio could catch. America had thought it was ahead, and it was not. The shock built two things fast: a war-research agency, [ARPA](https://spacenews.com/feb-7-1958-us-creates-arpa-response-sputnik/), in February 1958, and a space agency, [NASA](https://www.nasa.gov/history/65-years-ago-sputnik-ushers-in-the-space-age/), that October. Korolev's rocket did not stop there. It carried the first man to orbit, and its children kept flying. The same basic rocket, now called [Soyuz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R-7_(rocket_family)), has flown more times than any other rocket ever made and still carries people to space today, almost seventy years on. The lead the Soviets seized that morning compounded into the longest-running rocket in history.

*Curator's note:* **Placement.** Make cell, Soviet Union, 1957, in the Forced-by-War cluster. The tool is the R-7 itself, a built object, which keeps it a Make card and not a Model one (the orbit math was old; the rocket that could do it was not). Serves Goal 1, that reaching orbit took a made rocket and not just the equations, and Goal 2, the lead in launch moving to the Soviets, who held it for decades before the lineage carried Americans (the pair). Confidence: Direct on the launch date, the R-7 as first ICBM, the 21 August 1957 first full flight, Korolev's prison background and anonymity, the ARPA/NASA dates, and the Soyuz endurance, all cross-checked across NASA, Wikipedia, and SpaceNews. **Analysis.** This is a hero on [[Thread - Reaching Orbit]], the card that opens the rocket's climb, the moment a terror weapon turns into the first ride to orbit. The thread's own intro names the lesson this card carries: no one can calculate an engine into working, so the craft lives in the test stands and in people who have blown up enough rockets to know, which is why the lead stayed with Korolev's shop and its heirs for so long. It pairs against [[Tooling Card - The Soyuz]] across the widest gap in the deck: the same rocket family that beat America to orbit in 1957 is the one America paid Russia to ride decades later, the cleanest single-lineage case of a lead seized and then rented back. On [[Thread - Forced by War]] it is a textbook entry, a weapon program paying for a tool no market would have built on that schedule. It also rhymes with the carriers without sitting on it: von Braun carried the V-2 west to America, while the same German rocket work seeded Korolev's R-7 in the east, two carriers, one weapon, two superpowers. **Nuance.** Mechanism tagged Convergence, the war-grown reservoir (captured V-2 know-how, Glushko's engines, Korolev's test-stand craft) forced into one rocket on a missile deadline. It strains the same way the magnetron card does: the live force on the thread is really the carrier, craft moving in trained heads, more than convergence into an unbuyable moat, so treat this as another Make card leaning on a migration the model names only through the carrier. The R-7 was a missile first; Sputnik flew on a stripped, modified version (the 8K71PS) after Korolev's planned heavier science satellite ran late, so "bolted a ball to the top" compresses a real swap of payloads. Korolev was publicly only "the chief designer" until his death in 1966; the anonymity on the face is true and is itself part of the story. The moral weight here is lighter than the V-2's slave-labor record but not absent: the R-7's whole reason to exist was to deliver a nuclear warhead, and Korolev built it as a prisoner-turned-servant of the state that had broken his jaw. Total flight count for the family is cited as well over 1,900 as of 2026; the face keeps the safer "more than any other rocket ever made," which all sources agree on.
