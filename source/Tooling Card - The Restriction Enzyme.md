*Make · Baltimore, Maryland, USA · 1970s*
**Hamilton Smith and Kent Wilcox** · a protein that cuts DNA at a chosen word

**Front.** DNA is a very long string of four letters, and until 1970 nobody could break one at a spot they picked. In Baltimore, [Hamilton Smith](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-025-03635-y) was a young microbiology teacher at Johns Hopkins studying how the bacterium *Haemophilus influenzae* swallows loose DNA from its surroundings. He had his graduate student Kent Wilcox feed the bacteria some radioactive DNA from a virus and then fish it back out. Nothing came back out. After a lab meeting on a [1968 paper by Meselson and Yuan](https://www.nature.com/articles/2171110a0), who had pulled a DNA-chopping protein out of *E. coli*, Smith guessed the bacteria were chewing the foreign DNA up, and he checked it the crude way: two [glass tubes that measure how thick a liquid is](https://journals.plos.org/plosgenetics/article?id=10.1371/journal.pgen.1002466), one holding the bacteria's own DNA and one holding the virus DNA, both dosed with cell juice. Within five minutes the virus tube had gone watery and the other had not. Smith and Wilcox [purified the protein doing it](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/1330118/) and called it HindII. Smith and Thomas Kelly then worked out that it hunts for the six letters GTYRAC and slices [straight through the middle of them](https://www.neb.com/en-us/products/r0103-hincii), same place every time. The Meselson and Yuan protein had cut at a [random spot at least a thousand letters away](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Restriction_enzyme) from the word it read, which made it useless for cutting anything on purpose.

**Back.** Down the same corridor, Kathleen Danna and Daniel Nathans poured HindII onto the DNA of a monkey virus, got [eleven pieces of fixed size](https://profiles.nlm.nih.gov/spotlight/pd/feature/enzymes), laid them in order, and had the first map of a whole genome. Herbert Boyer's version of the blade, EcoRI, cut the ends that Cohen and Boyer joined in 1973 to build the first spliced gene. From 1974 a small company north of Boston began [growing the proteins in vats and selling them in tubes](https://www.neb.com/en-us/tools-and-resources/feature-articles/restriction-enzymes-at-neb-over-30-years-of-innovation), so a lab could buy a chosen cut off a shelf instead of purifying its own; bacteria have since given up more than 3,600 of these cutting proteins, and over 800 of them are [stocked for sale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Restriction_enzyme). Smith, Nathans and Werner Arber took the [1978 Nobel](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/medicine/1978/press-release/) for it, eight years after the tube of virus DNA went watery.
