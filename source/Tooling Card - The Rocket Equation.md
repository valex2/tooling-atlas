*Model · Kaluga, Russia · 1900s*
**Konstantin Tsiolkovsky** · the ideal-rocket speed equation

**Front.** In [1903](https://www.historyofinformation.com/detail.php?id=2622), a schoolteacher in the small Russian town of Kaluga named [Konstantin Tsiolkovsky](https://www.tsiolkovsky.org/en/biography/), deaf since a boyhood fever and self-taught out of library books, mailed a paper to a science magazine in St. Petersburg. He wanted to settle a question from the Jules Verne stories he loved: the giant moon cannon would squash its riders, he had checked, so what machine could really carry a person off the Earth? Years before, on [10 May 1897](https://nmspacemuseum.org/inductee/konstantin-e-tsiolkovsky/), he had worked the answer out with pen and paper, one short equation saying a rocket's final speed hangs on just two numbers: how fast the burnt gas leaves the nozzle, and how heavy the full rocket is next to the empty one. The equation turned spaceflight into arithmetic, and the arithmetic came out yes: burning [liquid hydrogen with liquid oxygen](https://www.encyclopedia.com/people/science-and-technology/space-exploration-biographies/konstantin-eduardovich-tsiolkovsky), a rocket could reach the [8,000 metres a second](https://www.tsiolkovsky.org/en/biography/) needed to circle the Earth.

**Back.** For years the paper went unread outside Russia, and [Robert Goddard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tsiolkovsky_rocket_equation) in America and Hermann Oberth in Germany each derived the same equation on their own. The equation carried hard news for every builder since: burnt gas leaves a nozzle at a few thousand metres a second at best, so nearly the whole rocket must be fuel, and in 1929 Tsiolkovsky spelled out the escape as [rocket trains](https://nmspacemuseum.org/inductee/konstantin-e-tsiolkovsky/), stages that drop their empty tanks as they climb. Published math kept no secret and gave Russia no machine, but the engineers raised on his papers aimed their first satellite launch at [17 September 1957](https://www.aps.org/apsnews/2007/10/soviets-launch-first-satellite-orbit), his hundredth birthday, missed by seventeen days, and dedicated Sputnik to the deaf teacher from Kaluga.
