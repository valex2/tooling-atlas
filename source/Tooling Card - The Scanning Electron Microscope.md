*Measure · Cambridge, UK · 1960s*
**Charles Oatley (Cambridge)** · an electron beam scanned across surfaces

**Front.** In 1948 a lecturer in the Cambridge engineering department named [Charles Oatley](https://www-g.eng.cam.ac.uk/125/achievements/oatley/cwo1.htm) handed a hard job to a new student, Dennis McMullan. The microscopes of the day shone electrons straight through a paper-thin slice; Oatley wanted the opposite, a fine beam swept back and forth across the top of a solid, whole object, point by point, the way a torch paints a wall in the dark. Each spot the beam struck knocked loose a spray of slower electrons, and a detector counted them and turned the count into a bright or dark dot on a screen. By 1952 the machine could tell apart things [50 nanometres](https://www-g.eng.cam.ac.uk/125/achievements/mcmullan/mcm.htm) apart, and its pictures had a deep, rounded look no other microscope gave; in 1965 the [Cambridge Instrument Company](https://collection.powerhouse.com.au/object/353255) turned it into a product, the [Stereoscan](https://www-g.eng.cam.ac.uk/125/achievements/oatley/semhist.htm), and shipped a first batch of five.

**Back.** Those deep-focus pictures let people look straight at the rough, three-dimensional surface of almost anything, a fly's eye or a crack in cooled metal. Sales passed [a hundred a year by 1968](https://www-g.eng.cam.ac.uk/125/achievements/oatley/semhist.htm), and firms in Japan and America built their own scanning machines soon after. The scanned beam stayed: today a close cousin, the [CD-SEM](https://iopscience.iop.org/article/10.1088/1361-6501/abd96d), sits in every chip plant, measuring circuit lines a few atoms wide to check the etching came out right, so the tool built to photograph a beetle now watches over the making of the world's processors.
