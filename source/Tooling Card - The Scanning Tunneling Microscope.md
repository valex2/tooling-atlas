*Measure · Zurich, Switzerland · 1980s*
**Gerd Binnig and Heinrich Rohrer** · a one-atom needle feeling atoms

**Front.** At IBM's Zurich lab, [Gerd Binnig and Heinrich Rohrer](https://www.ibm.com/history/scanning-tunneling-microscope) wanted to see the surface of a material atom by atom, which even an electron microscope could not quite do. Their trick used a quirk of physics: bring an extremely sharp metal tip, ending in effectively a [single atom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scanning_tunneling_microscope), to within a fraction of a nanometre of the surface, and electrons begin to [tunnel across the tiny gap as a faint current](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scanning_tunneling_microscope) that changes sharply with distance. Scan the tip back and forth, nudge it up and down to keep that current steady, and its path traces the bumps of the individual atoms below. On the night of [16 March 1981 the first images came through](https://www.ibm.com/history/scanning-tunneling-microscope); Binnig said he could not stop looking at them. The new thing was a microscope that felt single atoms, one at a time.

**Back.** It won the [Nobel Prize in 1986, only five years on](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scanning_tunneling_microscope), and opened surface science and the whole field of nanotechnology. In [1989 a researcher used the same tip to drag 35 xenon atoms into place and spell "IBM"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IBM_(atoms)), the first time anyone had arranged single atoms on purpose, so the tool that could see them could now move them. Its close cousin, the atomic force microscope, soon extended the trick to materials that carry no current, including living matter. The line that began with a draper holding a glass bead to the light reached its floor here: you cannot see anything smaller than the atoms themselves.
