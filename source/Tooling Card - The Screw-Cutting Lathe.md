*Make · London, Britain · 1800s*
**Henry Maudslay** · screw-cutting lathe

**Front.** Around 1800, a London engineer named [Henry Maudslay](https://www.lindahall.org/about/news/scientist-of-the-day/henry-maudslay/) was tired of a small, maddening fact: every screw thread was cut by hand with a file, so [no two were alike](https://collection.sciencemuseumgroup.org.uk/objects/co46284/henry-maudslays-original-screw-cutting-lathe-c-1800) and a nut made for one screw would not fit the next. He built a [lathe](https://collection.sciencemuseumgroup.org.uk/objects/co46284/henry-maudslays-original-screw-cutting-lathe-c-1800) that took the work out of the hand. A long rod, the lead screw, dragged a sliding tool-holder down the metal at a fixed pace, and a small dial on the rest set the exact depth of each cut. What was new was not a better screw but a machine that cut the same thread again and again, on its own.

**Back.** Once the cut was set by gears and a screw instead of a steady hand, you could make a machine more exact than the man working it, then use that machine to build a still more exact one. Maudslay's shop became a [school](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Henry-Maudslay): [Joseph Whitworth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Whitworth), who in [1841](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Whitworth) set Britain's first standard screw thread, [Richard Roberts](https://www.encyclopedia.com/history/encyclopedias-almanacs-transcripts-and-maps/henry-maudslay), and [James Nasmyth](https://www.ebsco.com/research-starters/history/james-nasmyth) of the steam hammer all learned the slide rest at his bench. Every machine tool built since carries his lead screw and sliding rest inside it. The lead did not wait or fade the way a lone craftsman's would; it compounded, passed hand to hand, and made Britain the place that built the world's machines.

*Curator's note:* (1) *Placement.* Make · Britain · ~1800; cell is metal-cutting precision. Goal: durable tooling leadership through a reproducible machine, mechanism being the slide rest + lead screw that move precision from the worker's hand into the machine itself. Confidence in the back: **Direct** — the apprentice "school" (Whitworth, Roberts, Nasmyth, Clement) and the descent of all later machine tools from Maudslay's slide-rest lathe are standard in the engineering-history literature (Britannica, Linda Hall, Science Museum). Figure caveat: the lathe is dated **c.1800** by the Science Museum and "perfected c.1797" by Linda Hall; I used the round ~1800 the brief specifies and left the 1797 perfection date in the note rather than the face.

(2) *Analysis.* This is the British anchor: the "from" before interchangeable-parts leadership crosses the Atlantic. It pairs against the American gauge/interchangeable-parts card as the upstream source — Maudslay makes the *tools*, the gauge later makes the *parts identical*, and the lead migrates. Against van Leeuwenhoek (skill in the hand that dies and must be regrown two centuries on), this is the deliberate opposite: skill *removed* from the hand and poured into a machine, so the lead compounds and travels instead of dying. Hero card (one shop, one man, a named lineage), and the cleanest example of "the tool that makes tools," not an echo.

(3) *Nuance.* Maudslay did not invent the slide rest in isolation; he combined the lead screw, slide rest, and change-gears into one practical machine, and earlier slide rests existed (this is why the face says "what was new was a machine that cut the same thread again and again," not "he invented the slide rest"). His later fame also rests on a bench micrometer he called the "Lord Chancellor" and on the Portsmouth Block Mills (1802–1808), both omitted to keep the card on the lathe.
