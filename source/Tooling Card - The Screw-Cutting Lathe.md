*Make · London, Britain · 1800s*
**Henry Maudslay** · screw-cutting lathe

**Front.** [Henry Maudslay](https://www.lindahall.org/about/news/scientist-of-the-day/henry-maudslay/), a London engineer, was tired of a small, maddening fact: every screw thread was cut by hand with a file, so [no two were alike](https://collection.sciencemuseumgroup.org.uk/objects/co46284/henry-maudslays-original-screw-cutting-lathe-c-1800) and a nut made for one screw would not fit the next. Around 1800 he built a [lathe](https://collection.sciencemuseumgroup.org.uk/objects/co46284/henry-maudslays-original-screw-cutting-lathe-c-1800) that took the work out of the hand. A long rod, the lead screw, dragged a sliding tool-holder down the metal at a fixed pace, and a small dial on the rest set the exact depth of each cut. The thing that mattered was not a better screw but a machine that cut the same thread again and again, on its own.

**Back.** Once the cut was set by gears and a screw instead of a steady hand, you could make a machine more exact than the man working it, then use that machine to build a still more exact one. Maudslay's shop became a [school](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Henry-Maudslay): [Joseph Whitworth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Whitworth), who in [1841](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Whitworth) set Britain's first standard screw thread, [Richard Roberts](https://www.encyclopedia.com/history/encyclopedias-almanacs-transcripts-and-maps/henry-maudslay), and [James Nasmyth](https://www.ebsco.com/research-starters/history/james-nasmyth) of the steam hammer all learned the slide rest at his bench. Every machine tool built since carries his lead screw and sliding rest inside it. The skill never died with him; it passed bench to bench through that lineage and made Britain the workshop that built the world's machines.
