*Make · Murray Hill, New Jersey, USA · 1950s*
**J. O. McNally** · a sealed amplifier for the ocean floor

**Front.** Bell Labs started looking for a vacuum tube that could live on the ocean floor in [1933](http://www.r-type.org/articles/art-279.htm), and twenty years later [J. O. McNally](http://www.r-type.org/articles/art-279.htm) and his tube group were still at it, working in nylon gowns in a shut-off room of the Murray Hill model shop. Nobody can replace a tube lying on the sea bed. So they built the [175HQ](https://lampes-et-tubes.info/sp/sp056.php?l=e) to be dull instead of strong: a heater wound from 36 inches of tungsten wire three thousandths of an inch thick, a cathode kept cool near 700°C, [0.7 milliamps of current per square centimetre where an ordinary tube ran 50](http://www.r-type.org/articles/art-279.htm), and the parts inside spaced far enough apart that nothing could ever short. Then every finished tube was left running for [5,000 hours](https://lampes-et-tubes.info/sp/sp056.php?l=e), measured six times along the way, and [six of every seven were thrown away](http://www.r-type.org/articles/art-279.htm). Three survivors went into each repeater, sealed in a steel housing thin and bendy enough to ride over a cable ship's sheave and slide off the stern without the ship stopping.

**Back.** [TAT-1 opened on 25 September 1956](https://atlantic-cable.com/Cables/1956TAT-1/), two cables running between Clarenville in Newfoundland and Oban in Scotland with [51 repeaters on each, one every 37 miles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TAT-1), and in its first day it carried [588 calls from London to the United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TAT-1). The British Post Office built its own repeater for the shallow Nova Scotia leg, [G. H. Metson's 6P12 with a platinum cathode core](http://www.r-type.org/articles/art-279.htm), and after that every ocean cable carried amplifiers on the bottom. AT&T would not put the [nine-year-old transistor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TAT-1) down there; transistors only took the repeaters on TAT-5 in 1970, and light replaced copper on [TAT-8 in 1988](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TAT-8). TAT-1 came out of service in November 1978, and across seven cable systems the [1,608 tubes](https://lampes-et-tubes.info/sp/sp056.php?l=e) Bell had left on the sea bed had run 287 million hours without one going bad.
