*Make · Murray Hill, New Jersey · 1950s*
**Chapin, Fuller, and Pearson** · a wafer that drinks sunlight

**Front.** In 1952 the phone company had a small, dull problem. Out in the wet tropics, the [dry-cell batteries that ran remote telephone gear kept rotting](https://www.aps.org/apsnews/2009/04/bell-labs-silicon-solar-cell), and Bell Labs asked an engineer named [Daryl Chapin](https://www.invent.org/inductees/daryl-chapin) to find some other way to make a little power far from any wire. Two doors down, a chemist named [Calvin Fuller](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calvin_Souther_Fuller) had worked out how to bake exact amounts of impurity into pure silicon, the same trick that was busy turning silicon into transistors. He handed Chapin and the physicist [Gerald Pearson](https://spie.org/news/photonics-focus/julyaugust-2021/gerald-pearson-co-inventor-of-the-silicon-solar-cell) a [sliver of silicon built in two layers](https://www.computerhistory.org/siliconengine/diffusion-process-developed-for-transistors/), and where the two layers met, light falling on it knocked a current loose. On [April 25, 1954](https://www.aps.org/apsnews/2009/04/bell-labs-silicon-solar-cell) they showed it off, a strip of silicon running a toy windmill and a little radio on nothing but daylight. That light makes electricity was old news; what they had was a wafer of doped silicon that turned [about 6 percent](https://www.smithsonianmag.com/innovation/document-deep-dive-patent-first-practical-solar-cell-1-180947906/) of the sun into usable power, where the best parts before had given back a tiny fraction of one percent.

**Back.** On the ground the little cell stayed a curiosity; Bell tried it on a [rural phone line in Americus, Georgia in 1955](https://www.smithsonianmag.com/innovation/document-deep-dive-patent-first-practical-solar-cell-1-180947906/), but a wafer of silicon was far too dear to power a town. Its real job waited above the air, where there is no wire to run and the sun never sets. Bell's own [[Tooling Card - Telstar|Telstar]], the first satellite to bounce a live call across the ocean, ran on [3,600 of these cells](https://americanhistory.si.edu/collections/object/nmah_711090) making about 14 watts, and from then on nearly every craft in orbit drank its power the same way. The lead did not stay with the phone company; it spread into space and then, much later and much cheaper, back down onto rooftops, growing far past the wafer Bell first held up to the light.
