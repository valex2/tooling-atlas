*Manufacture · Union, New Jersey, USA · 1990s*
**Vijay Singh** · a sterile plastic bag grown as a culture tank

**Front.** In 1996 [Vijay Singh](https://www.aiche.org/community/bio/vijay-singh), a chemist at Schering-Plough's research institute in Union, New Jersey, was trying to grow animal cells to make drug proteins. The usual way needed a fixed steel tank that had to be scrubbed and steam-cleaned between every batch, which is slow and risks letting in stray germs. Singh's [fix](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1023/A:1008025016272) was to grow the cells in a sealed plastic bag that came pre-cleaned by radiation: he half-filled the bag, set it on a tray that rocked back and forth, and let the rocking make a wave that mixed the cells and pulled in air through one tube while pushing out waste gas through another. The vessel itself was now a throwaway part, so nothing inside ever had to be cleaned.

**Back.** Singh left to found Wave Biotech in 1999, and the idea spread fast across the drug industry once makers saw they could skip the cleaning step entirely. A plant can now plumb in a fresh sterile bag and start a different drug in weeks instead of standing up a steel tank over years. The biggest buildouts are now in China: WuXi Biologics runs the [world's largest plant of this kind](https://www.wuxibiologics.com/the-worlds-largest-36000l-biomanufacturing-line-using-single-use-bioreactors-launches-gmp-operation-at-wuxi-biologics/) and pushed its disposable-tank capacity [past 430,000 liters](https://www.biopharmadive.com/news/wuxi-adds-large-scale-single-use-bioreactors/519778/), buying the giant bags and frames from Western suppliers while owning the floor space, so the capacity itself has become a US-versus-China contest.
