*Make · London, England · 1620s*
**William Oughtred** · two log scales slid by hand

**Front.** John Napier, a Scottish laird, published [a book of tables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mirifici_Logarithmorum_Canonis_Descriptio) in 1614 that did one strange thing: it turned every number into a length, picked so that adding two lengths gave you the same answer as multiplying the two numbers. A hard multiply became a simple add. Edmund Gunter then [printed those lengths along a wooden ruler](https://mathshistory.st-andrews.ac.uk/Biographies/Gunter/) in 1620, and you measured off products with a pair of dividers. Around 1622 William Oughtred, an English clergyman who taught mathematics in London, [laid two of those rulers side by side](https://www.britannica.com/biography/William-Oughtred) and slid one against the other. Sliding one scale by a distance and reading where it landed *was* the addition of the lengths, so the dividers were gone and the answer sat under your eye.

**Back.** Oughtred made a round version first, then the straight sliding pair, and [published the design in 1632](http://scihi.org/william-oughtred-slide-rule/) after a student tried to claim it. The instrument barely changed for three centuries. Engineers sized bridges and ships by sliding a scale, and Pickett rules [flew on Apollo 11 and Apollo 13](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slide_rule). The lead held until 1972, when Hewlett-Packard's [HP-35 pocket calculator](https://ethw.org/Milestones:Development_of_the_HP-35,_the_First_Handheld_Scientific_Calculator,_1972) did the same sums to ten digits. Colleges dropped slide-rule classes, and the tool was gone from working desks within a few years.
