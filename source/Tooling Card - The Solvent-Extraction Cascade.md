*Manufacture · Beijing, China · 1970s*
**Xu Guangxian** · a calculable counter-current cascade of mixing tanks

**Front.** In 1972 the chemist [Xu Guangxian](https://english.pku.edu.cn/news_events/news/people/9479.html) was handed a dull, hard job at Peking University: split praseodymium from neodymium. The seventeen rare earths sit in one block of the periodic table and are so chemically alike that no single step pulls them apart; one shake of the standard method barely moved the two, a [separation factor of about 1.4](https://pubs.rsc.org/en/content/articlehtml/2021/qi/d0qi90084b). The known fix was a long chain of stirred tanks, each mixing two liquids and letting them settle, the streams flowing in opposite directions so a tiny per-tank gain compounds over [hundreds of stages](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/engineering/rare-earth-extraction). Nobody could say in advance how many tanks to chain or how to set each, so plants were groped toward over months. Xu's new thing was to make the chain a piece of math: he found that the ratio of two elements holds steady through the working middle of the cascade, a [constant mixing-extraction ratio](https://pubs.rsc.org/en/content/articlehtml/2021/qi/d0qi90084b), and from it derived the formulas for the stage count and every setting, so a line could be designed on paper and run right the first time.

**Back.** Purity reached [99.99%](https://english.pku.edu.cn/news_events/news/people/9479.html) and the cost of magnet-grade metal fell. Xu did not patent it; the method [spread through China's state rare-earth plants](https://english.pku.edu.cn/news_events/news/people/9479.html), and the country rode the cost curve down until by [1995 it made about 90% of the world's high-purity rare earths](https://english.pku.edu.cn/news_events/news/people/9479.html). The West kept the ore and lost the industry: America's Mountain Pass mine shut at the end of the 1990s and its revived operator Molycorp [went bankrupt in 2015](https://fortune.com/2015/06/25/molycorp-bankruptcy/), while China today [refines 91% of magnet rare earths and makes 94% of the world's sintered magnets](https://www.iea.org/commentaries/with-new-export-controls-on-critical-minerals-supply-concentration-risks-become-reality). Beijing knew which asset to guard: it [banned the export of the separation technology itself in December 2023](https://www.csis.org/analysis/what-chinas-ban-rare-earths-processing-technology-exports-means), then [put seven heavy rare earths and their magnets under export licensing in April 2025](https://english.mofcom.gov.cn/Policies/AnnouncementsOrders/art/2025/art_0dd87cbee7b045bf93fabe6ab2faceee.html), and within weeks European dysprosium cost [six times the Chinese price](https://www.iea.org/commentaries/with-new-export-controls-on-critical-minerals-supply-concentration-risks-become-reality).

(2) Analysis: this is the deck's purest "tool over raw material" case and its strangest moat, because the tool began as a published equation, the most copyable thing imaginable. It pairs with the gas centrifuge as the other cascade-separation chokepoint, but there the moat is precision rotor hardware; here it is process-chemistry craft. It feeds the sintered neodymium magnet, the downstream metallurgy that turns separated oxides into the strongest permanent magnets made. (3) Nuance: the moat is rebuildable, not eternal. The equation is public and won Xu China's top science prize in 2009; what no rival can download is fifty years of cascade-tuning and magnet metallurgy stacked on top. Lynas started the first heavy-rare-earth separation outside China in Malaysia in 2025 and MP Materials is restarting magnets in Texas, both slow and sub-scale. The "one-step scale-up" and "100 days to one week" figures sit in PKU-adjacent accounts, not the RSC tribute, so they are kept out here. The ~75% drop in Chinese magnet exports after April 2025 is a trade-press estimate.
