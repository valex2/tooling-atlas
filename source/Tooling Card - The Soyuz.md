*Make · Russia (Baikonur) · 2010s*
**the Soyuz program** · a simple rocket kept flying for decades

**Front.** On [21 July 2011](https://www.nasa.gov/history/10-years-ago-sts-135-the-space-shuttles-grand-finale/) the Space Shuttle Atlantis rolled to a stop in the Florida dark, and the United States no longer had a way to send its own people to space. The Shuttle had been grand and complicated, and it was done. So NASA went to Russia and bought seats. The ride was [Soyuz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soyuz_(rocket_family)), a small rocket the Russians had been flying and fixing in tiny steps for fifty years. America did not build a new rocket. It rented the Russian one.

**Back.** For nearly a decade the only door to the space station ran through Soyuz, and the Russians knew it. The price of a seat climbed from [about $21 million early on](https://spaceflightnow.com/2020/10/13/nasa-uses-final-purchased-soyuz-seat-for-wednesday-flight-to-station/) to [roughly $90 million](https://www.space.com/nasa-pays-russia-90-million-for-soyuz-seat.html) by the end. Soyuz could charge that much because it just kept working. It grew straight out of the [R-7](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R-7_(rocket_family)), the same rocket that threw Sputnik into the sky in 1957. Its family has now flown [more than 2,000 times](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R-7_(rocket_family)), more than any other rocket ever made. The nation that won the race to the Moon spent ten years buying rides on the grandchild of the rocket that once beat it. [SpaceX flew Americans up again in May 2020](https://www.nasa.gov/news-release/nasa-astronauts-launch-from-america-in-historic-test-flight-of-spacex-crew-dragon/), and the renting stopped.
