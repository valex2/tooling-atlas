*Make · Russia (Baikonur) · 2010s*
**the Soyuz program** · a simple rocket kept flying for decades

**Front.** On [21 July 2011](https://www.nasa.gov/history/10-years-ago-sts-135-the-space-shuttles-grand-finale/) the Space Shuttle Atlantis rolled to a stop in the Florida dark, and the United States no longer had a way to send its own people to space. The Shuttle had been grand and complicated, and it was done. So NASA went to Russia and bought seats. The ride was [Soyuz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soyuz_(rocket_family)), a small rocket the Russians had been flying and fixing in tiny steps for fifty years. America did not build a new rocket. It rented the Russian one.

**Back.** For nearly a decade the only door to the space station ran through Soyuz, and the Russians knew it, so the price of a seat climbed from [about $21 million early on](https://spaceflightnow.com/2020/10/13/nasa-uses-final-purchased-soyuz-seat-for-wednesday-flight-to-station/) to [roughly $90 million](https://www.space.com/nasa-pays-russia-90-million-for-soyuz-seat.html) by the end. Soyuz could charge that much because it just kept working. It grew straight out of the [R-7](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R-7_(rocket_family)), the same rocket that threw Sputnik into the sky in 1957. Its family has now flown [more than 2,000 times](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R-7_(rocket_family)), more than any other rocket ever made. The nation that won the race to the Moon spent ten years buying rides on the grandchild of the rocket that once beat it. Let your own way to space lapse, and you end up renting it from whoever kept theirs flying. [SpaceX flew Americans up again in May 2020](https://www.nasa.gov/news-release/nasa-astronauts-launch-from-america-in-historic-test-flight-of-spacex-crew-dragon/), and the renting stopped.

*Curator's note:* **Placement.** Make cell, Russia, 2011, on the late end of [[Thread - Reaching Orbit]]. The "tool" is the Soyuz launch system, a built thing you can fire today, which keeps it a Make card and not a People one. Goal 2: this is a migration story, the lead in crewed launch sliding from the US to whoever still flew. Mechanism tagged Pace-layers, and it fits cleanly for once: the Shuttle was the fast layer (a political program retired on a budget timeline), and the capability to throw people to orbit is the slow layer that does not regrow on command, so the US spent the slow inheritance and then had to rent it back for nine years until SpaceX rebuilt it. Confidence: Direct on every load-bearing fact, all cross-checked, the July 2011 Shuttle retirement (NASA), the seat-price climb from ~$21M to ~$90M (Spaceflight Now and Space.com), the R-7-to-Soyuz lineage and the 2,000+ flight count (Wikipedia R-7 family), and the May 2020 Crew Dragon return (NASA). **Analysis.** This is the back half of its pair and a Hero on the thread. It rhymes directly against [[Tooling Card - The R-7 Rocket]]: in 1957 the R-7 humiliated America by putting Sputnik overhead; in 2011 the same rocket's descendant collected rent from America for the ride to orbit. Same rocket bloodline, two opposite ends of the power curve, which is the whole point of the pair, so this card should lean on that reversal and let the 1957 card carry the origin. What it adds the deck did not have: a clean modern pace-layers case where the loss is not craft dying (nobody forgot how, the Shuttle just stopped) but a deliberate policy gap, and the cost of the gap is measured in real dollars per seat. It also sets up the thread's closing beat, cheap rockets that land and fly again, by naming the moment the renting ended. Pairs against the Slater/magnetron migration cards as a different flavor of migration: not craft carried out in a head, but a capability simply allowed to lapse at home while another nation kept its production line warm. **Nuance.** The ~$21M low is a 2006-era contract figure, predating the 2011 gap, so the face says "early on" rather than pinning it to 2011; the seat price during the rental decade proper ran from the $60Ms up to the $90.3M final flight (Soyuz MS-17, October 2020), with ~$86M a fair mid-to-late number. The gap was nine years and change (July 2011 to May 2020), rounded to "nearly a decade." Russia is the seller, but other partners (ESA, JAXA, CSA) also bought Soyuz seats; the card keeps the US frame because the US is the one that had the capability and let it lapse. Soyuz the rocket and Soyuz the crew capsule share a name; the "tool" here is the launch system as a whole. The R-7 is also the [[Tooling Card - The R-7 Rocket]] subject in its 1957 form, so the two cards share a machine across sixty years, which is the pair's spine. Baikonur is the launch site (in Kazakhstan, leased by Russia); placed under Russia per the program's owner.
