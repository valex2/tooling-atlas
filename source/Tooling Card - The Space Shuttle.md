*Make · United States · 1980s onward*
**NASA** · a winged ship meant to fly again and again

**Front.** On [April 12, 1981](https://www.nasa.gov/mission/sts-1/), John Young and Bob Crippen rode a new kind of machine off the pad in Florida: the Space Shuttle, the first rocket built to fly, land, and fly again. Until then every rocket was thrown away. You built a tall machine, fired it once, and the whole thing fell into the sea. NASA wanted to stop that waste with a ship that flew up on wings, did its job, glided home, and went again. They sold it as the way to make space cheap: [about a hundred dollars a pound to orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Criticism_of_the_Space_Shuttle_program), [around fifty flights a year](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Criticism_of_the_Space_Shuttle_program). What was new was not the flight. It was the promise: a spaceship you could use again.

**Back.** The promise broke on how the ship was built. After every flight the [roughly 35,000 heat tiles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Criticism_of_the_Space_Shuttle_program) had to be checked one by one, each cut for its own spot, and the engines had to be pulled apart and rebuilt. So a launch ran [around a billion dollars](https://www.mdpi.com/2226-4310/12/5/364), more than fifty times the pitch, and often dearer than throwing a plain rocket away. Twice a shuttle broke apart and its crew died: [Challenger in 1986, Columbia in 2003](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Columbia_disaster). Fourteen people in all. When the fleet was [parked in 2011](https://www.nasa.gov/history/10-years-ago-sts-135-the-space-shuttles-grand-finale/), America had nothing ready to fly its own people, so for nine years it [bought seats on Russian rockets](https://www.space.com/42781-nasa-astronauts-will-ride-soyuz-spacecraft.html). Reusing the ship was the right idea, built the wrong way, and the lead did not compound. It leaked away.

*Curator's note:* **Placement.** Make cell, United States, first flight 1981, retired 2011. The tool is the part-reusable spaceplane itself, the orbiter plus its engines and heat shield, which is what makes it a Make card. Serves Goal 1 in the negative: reusable spaceflight was the moat NASA reached for and the way they built it kept them from holding it. Confidence: Direct on every dated fact, the launch, the two disasters, the fourteen dead, the 2011 retirement, the Soyuz gap. Mechanism: Convergence, but inverted and straining (see below). **Analysis.** This is a cautionary Hero on [[Thread - Reaching Orbit]], the low point of the arc the thread names: "a decade when the nation that won the Moon rented rides from Russia." Its whole job is the pair against [[Tooling Card - The Falcon 9]] (SpaceX landing and reflying a booster): reusability done wrong versus done right, the same idea twenty years apart, one that tore the ship down after every flight and one that flew it back nearly as-is. The shuttle is the deck's clean example of a tool that delivered the *demonstration* (yes, a spaceship can be reused) while missing the *economics* that was the entire point, which is a sharper failure than simply never building the thing. It pairs against the migration cards too: where the magnetron lead crossed to whoever could build by the million, here the lead did not cross to a rival nation so much as drain out, leaving a vacuum that Soyuz and later SpaceX filled. **Nuance.** "Reusable" is loose: the orbiter and the two solid boosters came back, but the big orange tank burned up every flight, so the shuttle was always part-throwaway. The cost figure is contested and depends on what you count. Marginal cost per flight near the end was about $400 million; spread the whole program's $200-plus billion over 135 flights and it runs well over a billion each, which is the "around a billion" used here. The promised "fifty flights a year" was never close: the program averaged about 4.5 a year and peaked at 9 in 1985. The deeper moral complexity is the fourteen deaths: both losses, the cold O-ring and the foam strike, were flagged as risks and flown anyway, so the cost was not only money. Mechanism strain: Convergence is the closest of the three labels but it fits backward here. The shuttle is not a reservoir of craft converging into a moat; it is a case where the convergence *failed to happen*, where reuse never became cheap enough to compound. Like the magnetron card, this is a second card reaching for a force the three mechanisms do not cleanly name, the lead that simply leaks when the tool is built wrong, worth tracking as drift rather than forcing the tag.
