*Make · United States · 1980s onward*
**NASA** · a winged ship meant to fly again and again

**Front.** On [April 12, 1981](https://www.nasa.gov/mission/sts-1/), John Young and Bob Crippen rode a new kind of machine off the pad in Florida: the Space Shuttle, the first rocket built to fly, land, and fly again. Until then every rocket was thrown away. You built a tall machine, fired it once, and the whole thing fell into the sea. NASA wanted to stop that waste with a ship that flew up on wings, did its job, glided home, and went again. They sold it as the way to make space cheap: [about a hundred dollars a pound to orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Criticism_of_the_Space_Shuttle_program), [around fifty flights a year](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Criticism_of_the_Space_Shuttle_program). The flight itself broke no ground. The sales pitch did: a spaceship you could use again.

**Back.** The promise broke on how the ship was built. After every flight the [roughly 35,000 heat tiles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Criticism_of_the_Space_Shuttle_program) had to be checked one by one, each cut for its own spot, and the engines had to be pulled apart and rebuilt. A launch ran [around a billion dollars](https://www.mdpi.com/2226-4310/12/5/364), more than fifty times the pitch, often dearer than throwing a plain rocket away. Twice a shuttle broke apart and its crew died: [Challenger in 1986, Columbia in 2003](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Columbia_disaster). Fourteen people in all. When the fleet was [parked in 2011](https://www.nasa.gov/history/10-years-ago-sts-135-the-space-shuttles-grand-finale/), America had nothing ready to fly its own people, so for the next nine years it [bought seats on Russian rockets](https://www.space.com/42781-nasa-astronauts-will-ride-soyuz-spacecraft.html) to reach a station it had largely built.
