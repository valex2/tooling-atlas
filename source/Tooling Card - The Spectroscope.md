*Measure · Heidelberg, Germany · 1850s*
**Robert Bunsen and Gustav Kirchhoff** · matter read by its light

**Front.** In Heidelberg around 1859, the chemist [Robert Bunsen](https://www.sciencehistory.org/education/scientific-biographies/robert-bunsen-and-gustav-kirchhoff/) had a clean, near-colourless gas flame, and noticed that different salts dropped into it glowed in different colours. The trouble was the colours blurred together and you could not tell close elements apart. His friend, the physicist [Gustav Kirchhoff](https://www.dpma.de/english/our_office/publications/milestones/greatinventors/gustavrobertkirchhoff/index.html), suggested passing the light through a prism first. They rigged up a [makeshift instrument from two old telescopes and a prism in a box](https://www.chemistryworld.com/opinion/kirchhoffs-spectroscope/6547.article), and found that [each element threw a unique pattern of sharp bright lines](https://www.sciencehistory.org/education/scientific-biographies/robert-bunsen-and-gustav-kirchhoff/), a fixed fingerprint. The new thing was that you could now tell what something was made of purely from its light, a flame, a prism, and two old telescopes on a bench.

**Back.** Within a year the fingerprints turned up lines no known element made, and they had [discovered two new elements, cesium in 1860 and rubidium in 1861](https://rinconeducativo.org/en/anniversaries/may-10-1860-robert-bunsen-and-gustav-kirchhoff-discover-caesium/), the first elements found by their light. Then came the astonishing reach: the same line-fingerprints sit in sunlight and starlight, so the spectroscope could read what the stars are made of, a thing thought to be forever beyond knowing. Chemistry got a way to identify anything by its glow, and astronomy got a way to weigh the heavens. A flame and a prism on one bench let a person read the makeup of a star a trillion miles off.
