*Make · Rainhill, England · 1820s*
**George Stephenson** · a steam engine on rails that won the race

**Front.** In October 1829 the owners of the half-built Liverpool and Manchester Railway still argued over what should pull the trains. They offered [£500](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rainhill_trials) to whoever could send the best engine back and forth along a level course at Rainhill. George Stephenson's shop entered Rocket, and its trick was inside the boiler: instead of one fat flue, it ran the hot gas through [25 narrow copper tubes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stephenson%27s_Rocket), each about 75 mm across, so far more metal touched the water and far more steam came up. Rocket was the only engine to finish, averaging [12 mph and topping 30 mph](https://www.railwaymuseum.org.uk/objects-and-stories/stephensons-rocket-and-rise-locomotive) while hauling 13 tons.

**Back.** The idea for the many-tube boiler came from the railway's own [Henry Booth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_Booth); George's son Robert built the engine. Stephenson's firm won the contract, and when the line opened on [15 September 1830](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stephenson%27s_Rocket) it was the first railway to run trains between two cities on a schedule. Every builder copied the layout, and the steam locomotive carried goods and people for a century before diesel and electric took over.
