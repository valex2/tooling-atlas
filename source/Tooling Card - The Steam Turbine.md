*Make · Gateshead, England · 1880s*
**Charles Parsons** · steam blown through blade rows spins a shaft

**Front.** In 1884 Charles Parsons was the junior partner running electrical work at Clarke, Chapman and Company, a marine-engine firm on the Tyne. Steam engines drove a piston up and down with a jerk, then a crank turned that into a rough rotation, far too shaky and too slow-changing to spin the new electric dynamos well. Parsons built a different machine: a shaft carrying rows of small angled blades, sat inside a casing with matching fixed blades, so steam [pushed through stage after stage](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Charles-Algernon-Parsons) and gave up its speed to the rotor a little at a time. There was no piston and no crank; the steam itself spun the shaft, and his first one ran at [18,000 turns a minute](https://www.encyclopedia.com/people/science-and-technology/technology-biographies/sir-charles-algernon-parsons) and drove a dynamo he built to match it.

**Back.** A turbine spins; a generator turns that spin into current; the two married cleanly, and in 1890 a Parsons machine lit a [Newcastle power station](https://research.ncl.ac.uk/northerninnovation/exhibits/charlesparsons/), the first to make public electricity this way. To prove it could move ships too, Parsons raced his little [Turbinia](https://www.asme.org/about-asme/engineering-history/landmarks/73-turbinia) through the lines of the Royal Navy fleet at Spithead in 1897 at over 34 knots, faster than anything that could chase it. The design was copied everywhere; today steam turbines, fed by coal, gas, or nuclear heat, spin [most of the world's electricity](https://www.ebsco.com/research-starters/environmental-sciences/steam-and-steam-turbines).

- **Placement.** Engines & Power thread, Make kind, Goal Both, Convergence mechanism, Direct confidence. Builds on Watt's reciprocating engine; the turbine is the answer to the piston's roughness once the load became a fast, steady dynamo.
- **Analysis.** Pairs tightly with Faraday's induction ring: the turbine spins, the generator converts, and one is useless for grid power without the other. The card carries the thread's pivot from heat-into-motion (Watt) to heat-into-electricity at scale. Its moat was the blading craft and high-speed balancing held by Parsons' Newcastle works, which is why the firm built turbines for the world's navies and power stations for decades.
- **Nuance.** Parsons is usually dated to 1884 at Clarke, Chapman in Gateshead (south bank of the Tyne), not Newcastle proper; his own firm in Heaton/Newcastle came in 1889, so I placed the tool at Gateshead. The 18,000 rpm figure is from his first machine and is widely repeated but is a single-source headline number. "Most of the world's electricity" is solid directionally; the often-cited ~85 to 88 percent figures are US-leaning and older, so I kept the claim qualitative.
