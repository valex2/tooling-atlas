*Measure · Manchester, England · 1840s*
**Joseph Whitworth** · three plates scraped against each other in rotation

**Front.** In 1840 a Manchester toolmaker stood up at the [British Association meeting in Glasgow](https://www.gracesguide.co.uk/Joseph_Whitworth) and told the room how to make a thing flat when you own nothing flat to copy. [Joseph Whitworth](https://www.lindahall.org/about/news/scientist-of-the-day/joseph-whitworth/) had rented a room on Chorlton Street seven years before under a sign reading "Joseph Whitworth, tool-maker, from London," and the trade's usual way of getting a flat surface bothered him. Two iron plates ground together with emery wear each other into a hollow and a mound, and the pair still sits snug: [if one were concave and the other convex, they might still coincide](https://www.gracesguide.co.uk/Joseph_Whitworth). So he used three plates and threw out the emery. He spread [red ochre and oil](https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Miscellaneous_Papers_on_Mechanical_Subjects/A_Paper_on_Plane_Metallic_Surfaces_or_True_Planes) on one, pressed a second onto it, cut away every spot the colour marked with a steel scraper [sharpened on a Turkey stone](https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Miscellaneous_Papers_on_Mechanical_Subjects/A_Paper_on_Plane_Metallic_Surfaces_or_True_Planes), then changed the pairing and did it again, round and round the three. A plate can hide a curve from one partner. It cannot hide it from two.

**Back.** The scraped plate became the flat that every other flat in the shop was judged against, and Whitworth built upward from it. His measuring machine, shown at the [Great Exhibition of 1851](https://www.gracesguide.co.uk/Joseph_Whitworth), read to a millionth of an inch, and in [1859 he sat a one-inch iron bar in it](https://imechearchive.wordpress.com/2021/05/14/objects-of-engineering-whitworths-millionth-machine/) and had a man touch the bar with a finger so the meeting could watch it grow longer. British shops took up his standard measures, and [by 1860 the system was in general use](https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Whitworth,_Joseph_(DNB00)). The plate itself moved from cast iron to black granite, and a [Grade 0 plate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_plate) today runs flat to 3.5 micrometres across a square 250 mm on a side. The rotation outlived the iron: in 1967 [Schulz and Schwider](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3848466/) compared three glass flats with light instead of ochre, and that test still certifies optical surfaces to a fraction of a nanometre.
