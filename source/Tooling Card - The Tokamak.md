*Make · Moscow, Russia · 1950s*
**Igor Tamm & Andrei Sakharov** · doughnut magnetic bottle for plasma

**Front.** In 1950, two physicists at a secret Soviet weapons lab, [Igor Tamm and Andrei Sakharov](https://pubs.aip.org/physicstoday/article/58/12/15/394713/Sakharov-Is-Tokamak-s-Originator), were still busy building the hydrogen bomb when they turned to a harder problem: how to hold a gas hot enough to fuse without it melting the pot it sits in. Any wall a plasma touches cools it and burns away, so they [proposed](https://iopscience.iop.org/article/10.1088/0029-5515/50/1/014003) bending the gas into a ring and wrapping that ring in twisted magnetic fields, so the hot plasma floats in the middle and never quite reaches the walls. Work on real machines began at the Kurchatov Institute in 1951, moving from little glass and porcelain jars up to the first true device, the [T-1](https://www.energyencyclopedia.com/en/nuclear-fusion/history/tokamak-invention), which ran in 1958. They called the doughnut-shaped bottle a tokamak, from the Russian words for a ring chamber with a magnetic coil.

**Back.** For a decade the West thought the idea was a dead end and stuck with its own designs. Then in August 1968 the Soviets announced that their bigger T-3 had held a plasma at [more than 10 million degrees](https://www.iter.org/node/20687/august-1968-revolution-fusion), ten times hotter than anyone else, and doubting British physicists came to Moscow with a laser to measure it themselves and [confirmed it](https://fire.pppl.gov/nf_50th_5_Smirnov.pdf). Within a year Princeton tore out its old stellarator and rebuilt it as a tokamak, and the lead in holding a fusion plasma quietly moved out of Moscow as every lab copied the ring. The [ITER](https://www.iter.org/node/20687/august-1968-revolution-fusion) reactor now rising in France is the same doughnut, grown enormous.
