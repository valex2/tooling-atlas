*Manufacture · Toyota City, Japan · 1950s*
**Taiichi Ohno** · make only what the next step needs

**Front.** In [1950 Eiji Toyoda toured Ford's vast River Rouge plant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toyota_Production_System) and went home to a company that was tiny and nearly broke, with a small market that wanted a few of many models, not a flood of one. Ford's mountains of parts and huge batches would have buried Toyota. So an engineer named [Taiichi Ohno](https://automotivehalloffame.org/honoree/taiichi-ohno/) built the flow a different way. Each step made only what the next step actually asked for, the request passed back down the line on a simple card called a [kanban](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toyota_Production_System), so almost nothing sat in storage (just-in-time). Any worker could stop the whole line the instant a defect appeared, a trick taken from [Sakichi Toyoda's looms, which halted themselves when a thread broke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toyota_Production_System) (jidoka). The new thing was not a faster line but a smarter one: pull instead of push, stop on the first fault, and a workforce that fixes the process while it runs.

**Back.** Toyota came to make cars cheaper and better than Detroit, and by the 1980s Japan had taken the lead in building automobiles. The West scrambled to copy the method as ["lean manufacturing"](https://www.lean.org/lexicon-terms/toyota-production-system/), but mostly bought the kanban cards and missed the system. The tacit part did not travel: the trained cadre, the habit of relentless small improvement (kaizen), the supplier web. The whole method was written down in books, and the lead stayed in Aichi anyway. America invented the moving line; Japan kept the lead because the moat was the doing, not the diagram.
