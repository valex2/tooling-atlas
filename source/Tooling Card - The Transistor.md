*Make · Murray Hill, New Jersey · 1940s*
**Bardeen, Brattain, and Shockley** · a switch made of solid crystal

**Front.** The phone company had a problem with glass. Every long call ran through [vacuum tubes](https://www.computerhistory.org/siliconengine/invention-of-the-point-contact-transistor/), little hot bulbs that pushed a fading voice back up to full strength. They burned out, ran warm, and drank power, and the network needed millions of them. So Bell Labs in Murray Hill set Bardeen and Brattain on the hunt for something better. On [December 16, 1947](https://ethw.org/Bell_Demonstrates_Transistor), [John Bardeen and Walter Brattain](https://www.computerhistory.org/siliconengine/invention-of-the-point-contact-transistor/) pressed two tiny gold points, held a hair apart by a plastic wedge, onto a clean slab of [germanium crystal](https://www.computerhistory.org/siliconengine/invention-of-the-point-contact-transistor/), and a small current at one point swelled the current at the other [up to 100 times](https://www.computerhistory.org/siliconengine/invention-of-the-point-contact-transistor/). The new thing was not the boost. It was that solid crystal, with nothing moving and nothing glowing, could now do the tube's whole job. Within a year their colleague [William Shockley](https://www.computerhistory.org/siliconengine/conception-of-the-junction-transistor/) had drawn up a sturdier kind, a sandwich of crystal layers you could build in bulk, and by [1951 Bell Labs could make it](https://www.computerhistory.org/siliconengine/conception-of-the-junction-transistor/).

**Back.** The little crystal switch did two jobs that turned out to be the same job. It could pass a signal along, which is what the phone lines wanted, and it could be flipped on or off to stand for a one or a zero, which is what a computer wanted. The same part opened the way to both the long-distance network and the machine that thinks. All three men shared the [1956 Nobel Prize in Physics](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/physics/1956/summary/). The lead compounded and then it scattered: the next wall was that every transistor still had to be [wired up by hand](https://www.computerhistory.org/siliconengine/invention-of-the-point-contact-transistor/), thousands of them, the [tyranny of numbers](https://blog.immunitynetworks.com/jack-kilby-integrated-circuit-nobel-prize-semiconductor/), and the men who broke that wall and grew the chip into a giant industry were out west, not at the phone company that paid for the crystal.
