*Make · India · 1000s*
**medieval spinners (India / Song China)** · foot-driven fibre-spinning wheel

**Front.** By the eleventh century, spinners in [India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spinning_wheel) and in the busy cloth workshops of [Song China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spinning_wheel) were turning raw cotton and silk into thread all day long. The old way was a hand spindle: one hand fed the loose fibre while the other kept a small weighted stick spinning, so a worker could only ever tend one thread at a time. Someone set the spindle on the rim of a large wheel and tied a foot [treadle](https://ms.copernicus.org/articles/13/625/2022/) to it, so a push of the foot turned the wheel and the wheel spun the spindle. Now both hands were free to draw and twist the fibre, and the thread came off much faster than any spindle could manage.

**Back.** For hundreds of years the foot-driven wheel was the quickest way to make thread, and it spread out of Asia across the [Islamic world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spinning_wheel) into Europe, where by [1533](https://www.worlds-finest-wool.com/history-of-the-spinning-wheel/) a spinner in Brunswick had added a spool that wound the thread on as fast as it was twisted. Nearly every house that made cloth kept one. Then in [1769](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_frame) [Richard Arkwright](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_frame) took the same three jobs the wheel did by hand, drawing the fibre, twisting it, and winding it on, and gave them to a frame of rollers turned by a water wheel, so one machine did the work of many spinners and a single child could mind it. The cottage spinner who had spun for the village could not keep up with a mill, and within a lifetime the treadle wheel had gone from a household living to a keepsake.
