*Measure · Portland, Oregon, USA · 1940s*
**Howard Vollum** · a screen that holds a signal still

**Front.** In 1946 [Howard Vollum](https://vintagetek.org/howard-vollum/) was back from the war and working out of a radio shop on Southeast Hawthorne Boulevard in Portland, owned by his friend [Jack Murdock](https://www.testandmeasurementtips.com/oscilloscope-pioneers-murdock-and-vollum-and-the-founding-of-tektronix/). He had spent two and a half years in England building the display units for coastal radar, hacking British oscilloscopes to keep up with high-frequency work, and he knew what was wrong with them. A scope drew its line left to right on a free-running clock of its own. The signal you wanted to see arrived on its own schedule, so the picture started at a different place every time and slid across the glass in a blur. Vollum built a circuit that watched the incoming voltage and fired the sweep the instant it crossed a level you set, so each new trace landed exactly on top of the last one and the waveform stood still. He ruled the screen with a calibrated grid. In July 1947 the shop, now called Tektronix, shipped the first [Type 511](https://w140.com/tekwiki/wiki/511), serial number 101, [$795](https://w140.com/tekwiki/wiki/511) for 10 megacycles of bandwidth and a sweep down to 100 nanoseconds per centimetre. The [DuMont 248](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_the_oscilloscope) of 1945 could also start a trace on command, but you had to feed it a pulse from somewhere else. The 511 took its cue from the signal itself, and the grid meant an engineer could read volts and microseconds straight off the glass instead of squinting at a shape.

**Back.** An instrument that reports numbers gets used differently from one that shows a picture, and the 511 arrived just as the parts inside electronics started switching faster than anyone could reason about on paper. The little Portland plant ran [three shifts and still fell six months behind](https://vintagetek.org/tektronix-first-products/); Hughes Aircraft took [thirty of them in one order in November 1951](https://vintagetek.org/tektronix-first-products/). Tektronix moved to Beaverton, and by the time its [7000 series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tektronix_analog_oscilloscopes) sat on benches through the 1970s and 1980s the company held roughly [75 percent of the world oscilloscope market](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tektronix_analog_oscilloscopes), still [57 percent in 1992](https://www.referenceforbusiness.com/history2/19/Tektronix-Inc.html). Japanese makers took the cheap portable end, digital sampling reset the contest, and Tektronix now runs [third at about 15 percent](https://www.mordorintelligence.com/industry-reports/oscilloscope-market/companies), behind Rohde & Schwarz and behind Keysight, which is what became of the Palo Alto garage across the mountains.
