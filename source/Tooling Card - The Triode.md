*Make · United States · 1910s*
**Lee de Forest and Harold Arnold** · a glass tube that makes a faint signal loud

**Front.** A voice in a wire fades as it travels, and by 1900 a phone call died after about [800 miles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triode). In 1906 [Lee de Forest](https://www.historyofinformation.com/detail.php?id=563) slid a tiny zig-zag of wire, a [grid](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Lee-de-Forest), into an empty glass bulb between the hot filament and the cold plate, so a faint signal on the grid could steer a much stronger current through the bulb. It worked, but only barely, because his bulb still held a little air. In 1913 the phone company [paid him $50,000](https://www.construction-physics.com/p/the-age-of-the-amplifier) for it and handed it to [Harold Arnold](https://ethw.org/First_Telephone_Repeater), who pumped the bulb down to a far harder vacuum and made the same faint whisper come out loud and clean. The new thing was not the idea of three wires in a bulb. It was a tube you could trust to lift a real phone call without choking on its own leftover air.

**Back.** On [January 25, 1915](https://www.pbs.org/transistor/background1/events/transcon.html), [Alexander Graham Bell, in New York, spoke to Thomas Watson in San Francisco](https://www.ebsco.com/research-starters/history/first-transcontinental-telephone-call-made). The voice crossed [3,400 miles](https://www.pbs.org/transistor/background1/events/transcon.html), lifted by these tubes at [Pittsburgh, Omaha, and Salt Lake City](https://www.pbs.org/transistor/background1/events/transcon.html). The same tube that made a fading voice loud could also make and catch a radio wave, so it became the shared root of both the telephone network and broadcast radio, and the American phone company built a deep lead in it. Then the tube hit a wall of its own: hot, fragile, power-hungry, and it would not shrink. The people who held it went looking for something to replace it.

*Curator's note:* **Placement.** Make cell, United States, 1910s. Goal Both: Goal 1 because long-distance telephony rides on the tube, and Goal 2 because the lead it built sat with AT&T and Western Electric in the US and compounded there for decades. Mechanism: Convergence, the tube is the trunk where telephony and radio (and later radar) all converge on one amplifying part. Confidence: Direct on the dates, the people, the $50,000, the 1915 call. **Analysis.** This is the fork card. [[Thread - Long-Distance Comms]] is the demand line (push a signal farther than it wants to go) and it forks here into telephony and radio on the single shared part, then this same node opens [[Thread - Electronic Switching]], because the wall the tube hits (hot, fragile, power-hungry, will not shrink) is exactly the itch the transistor scratches. So the card does double duty: payoff of the distance thread and the setup of the switch thread. It pairs against [[Tooling Card - The Transistor]]: the glass tube that amplifies by boiling electrons off a hot wire versus the cold silicon chip that does the same job with no vacuum, no heat, and no moving glass, the second forced into being by the first's wall. Hero, not parade. It is the cleanest "one tool is the trunk of two industries" convergence in the deck, distinct from the EUV convergence card, which is many crafts converging into one machine rather than one part feeding many uses. **Nuance.** De Forest is the inventor of the grid and gets the glory, but the tube only became a reliable amplifier in Arnold's hands, so the card deliberately names both; crediting de Forest alone would overclaim the discovery and hide the making, which is the part the card is about. Arnold's key move was the harder vacuum, reached with a Gaede molecular pump brought from Germany in 1913 (the same year as the $50,000 purchase). The first commercial high-vacuum repeater went into service New York to Philadelphia on October 18, 1913, before the transcontinental line. The ~800-mile unamplified limit is from the Triode reference and other accounts put the loaded-line limit higher (around 1,000 to 1,200 miles before repeaters), so it is the ballpark figure, not a hard constant. Repeater count is disputed: the well-attested 1914 test boosted the signal at three named cities (Pittsburgh, Omaha, Salt Lake City), while some accounts cite six repeater stations on the finished line; I used the three named-city framing and flag the six here. "Hands it to the transistor" is the Bell System replacing its own tube, the irony belongs in the [[Thread - Electronic Switching]] and Bell Labs cards, not on this face.
