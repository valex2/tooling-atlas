*Make · Tokyo, Japan · 1950s*
**Leo Esaki (Sony)** · a diode electrons tunnel through

**Front.** One July day in [1957](https://www.shmj.or.jp/shimura/shimura_E/ssis_shimura3_02E.html), a physicist named [Leo Esaki](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/physics/1973/esaki/facts/) was chasing a factory problem at Tokyo Tsushin Kogyo, a small firm that would rename itself Sony within the year. Its germanium transistors kept failing when the crystal was fed too much phosphorus, and Esaki was told to find out how much doping the crystal could stand. Instead of backing off, he alloyed junctions so stuffed with impurities that the wall between the two sides thinned to about [ten nanometres](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3417806/), cooled one to −80 °C, and watched the current fall as he turned the voltage up. Electrons were not climbing that wall at all; they were slipping straight through it, as quantum theory had long said they could, and his [narrow germanium junction](https://adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/1958PhRv..109..603E) was the first electronic part ever built to work by tunnelling.

**Back.** [William Shockley](https://www.shmj.or.jp/innovation50/english/detail_D01E.htm) held the diode up in his keynote at a Brussels physics meeting in 1958, and the lead left Japan almost at once: American labs rushed out their own tunnel diodes, and [IBM hired Esaki](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Leo-Esaki) away in 1960. As a product the tunnel diode died within a decade, beaten by the fast-improving transistor, but the tunnelling it proved out still [erases the flash memory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flash_memory) in every phone, and in [1973](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/physics/1973/summary/) the work brought Japan its first Nobel Prize earned inside a company. At Shockley's own lab, [Robert Noyce](https://spectrum.ieee.org/robert-noyce-and-the-tunnel-diode) had sketched the same device in his notebook in 1956 and been waved off; when Esaki's paper appeared, he told Gordon Moore, "If I had gone one step further, I would have done it."
