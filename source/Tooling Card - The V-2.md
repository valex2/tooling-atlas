*Make · Peenemünde, Germany · 1940s*
**Wernher von Braun** · the first big liquid-fuel rocket

**Front.** On [3 October 1942](https://www.thisdayinaviation.com/3-october-1942/), a team led by [Wernher von Braun](https://www.britannica.com/technology/V-2-rocket) stood on the Baltic coast at [Peenemünde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/V-2_rocket) and lit the biggest rocket anyone had built. Two earlier ones had failed. This one climbed straight up and brushed the [edge of space](https://www.thisdayinaviation.com/3-october-1942/), about 85 km, the first thing humans made to get that high. To go that far it burned [liquid oxygen and watered-down alcohol](https://www.enginehistory.org/Rockets/RPE02/RPE02-2.shtml), and a [spinning pump](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/V-2_rocket) shoved both into the fire faster than any tank could pour them. The fire was so hot it would have melted the engine, so the cold fuel ran through the walls first to cool them on its way in. The altitude made the headlines, but the real machine was underneath it: a big liquid-fuel rocket motor that fed and cooled itself and did not tear apart.

**Back.** Two years later the same machine was loaded with a warhead and fired at London and Antwerp as the [V-2](https://www.britannica.com/technology/V-2-rocket), the first long-range guided missile. The rocket killed perhaps [5,000 to 9,000 people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/V-2_rocket); building it killed far more, around [20,000 prisoners](https://airandspace.si.edu/stories/editorial/wonder-weapons-and-slave-labor) worked to death in an underground factory. When the war ended the United States took the engineers and the parts, and the same hands that fed London a warhead built the [Saturn V that carried men to the Moon](https://www.pbs.org/wgbh/americanexperience/features/chasing-moon-wernher-von-braun-and-nazis/). The pump-fed, self-cooled engine von Braun proved at Peenemünde is the shape almost every big rocket has copied since, from Florida to Baikonur.
