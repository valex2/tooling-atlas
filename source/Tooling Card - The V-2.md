*Make · Peenemünde, Germany · 1940s*
**Wernher von Braun** · the first big liquid-fuel rocket

**Front.** On [3 October 1942](https://www.thisdayinaviation.com/3-october-1942/), a team led by [Wernher von Braun](https://www.britannica.com/technology/V-2-rocket) stood on the Baltic coast at [Peenemünde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/V-2_rocket) and lit the biggest rocket anyone had built. Two earlier ones had failed. This one climbed straight up and brushed the [edge of space](https://www.thisdayinaviation.com/3-october-1942/), about 85 km, the first thing humans made to get that high. To go that far it burned [liquid oxygen and watered-down alcohol](https://www.enginehistory.org/Rockets/RPE02/RPE02-2.shtml), and a [spinning pump](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/V-2_rocket) shoved both into the fire faster than any tank could pour them. The fire was so hot it would have melted the engine, so the cold fuel ran through the walls first to cool them on its way in. What was new was not the long shot through the sky. It was the engine: a big liquid-fuel rocket motor that fed and cooled itself and did not tear apart.

**Back.** Two years later the same machine was loaded with a warhead and fired at London and Antwerp as the [V-2](https://www.britannica.com/technology/V-2-rocket), the first long-range guided missile. The rocket killed perhaps [5,000 to 9,000 people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/V-2_rocket); building it killed far more, around [20,000 prisoners](https://airandspace.si.edu/stories/editorial/wonder-weapons-and-slave-labor) worked to death in an underground factory. When the war ended the United States took the engineers and the parts, and von Braun built the [rocket that carried men to the Moon](https://www.pbs.org/wgbh/americanexperience/features/chasing-moon-wernher-von-braun-and-nazis/). The pump-fed, self-cooled engine he proved at Peenemünde is the shape almost every big rocket has copied since. The tool that opened space was born as a weapon, and the lead walked west with the men who made it.

*Curator's note:* **Placement.** Make cell, Germany, 1944, in the Forced-by-War cluster. The tool is the first large liquid-fuel rocket engine: turbopump-fed, regeneratively cooled, the working template later rockets inherit. Serves Goal 1 (reaching orbit rides on this one unbuyable engine craft, the thing no one can calculate into working) and Goal 2 (the lead crossed the Atlantic in von Braun and his team after the war). Mechanism tagged Convergence: a decade of amateur and army liquid-fuel work, pump design, and cooling tricks forced into one working machine on a war deadline no market would set. The tag holds reasonably here, since the engine genuinely is a reservoir-into-one-object moment, though the deeper force on this card is the carrier (see below). Confidence: Direct on the date, the ~85 km altitude, the propellants, the turbopump and regenerative cooling, von Braun's lead, and the death-toll disparity, all cross-checked across This Day in Aviation, Britannica, the Smithsonian, the Aircraft Engine Historical Society, and Wikipedia. **Analysis.** This is the hero that opens [[Thread - Reaching Orbit]] at its source: the thread's own intro names "a Nazi terror weapon" as the start of the rocket's climb, and this is that card. It carries the thread's core claim physically, that the rocket engine is a tool no one can calculate into working, only build and fire and rebuild, so the craft lives in people and test stands. On [[Thread - Forced by War]] it is the cleanest case of war paying for the impossible, an engine no peacetime budget would have funded. It pairs naturally against a later Reaching-Orbit card, the Apollo or Saturn beat, as the same engine craft compounding once it crossed to America; the carrier rhyme is with Slater's Water Frame and Zeiss Divided, craft walked out in trained heads, here a whole rocket team taken by an army. Hero, not parade: one named lead, one dated first flight, one physical engine that becomes the template. **Nuance.** The "first object in space" claim is layered and the card softens it on purpose. The 3 October 1942 flight reached about 84.5–90 km (sources vary inside that band), below the modern 100 km Kármán line, so it is the first to *brush* the edge of space and the first into the thermosphere, which is why the face says "about 85 km" and "the edge of space," not "crossed into space." The first V-2 unambiguously above 100 km was the vertical test [MW 18014 on 20 June 1944](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MW_18014), apogee 176 km, retroactively the first human-made object in space; that detail is too fiddly for the face and the 1942 flight is the one the Peenemünde team itself celebrated. Combat deaths are given variously as ~5,000 (Baxter-era figures) up to ~9,000 including military; prisoner deaths at Mittelbau-Dora are ~20,000 of roughly 60,000 sent there, so "far more" is solid in either direction. The moral complexity is real and kept on the face, not buried: the same man and the same engine that opened spaceflight also ran a slave-labor weapons program, and von Braun's degree of knowledge of conditions at Mittelwerk is documented and damning. The card states the death toll plainly rather than letting the Moon-shot ending launder it.

*Carrier note:* The rocket's lead moved with the men, not the hardware. After 1945 about 1,600 German engineers were taken to the US under Operation Paperclip, the USSR took its own share, and Europe took none and lost the capability.
