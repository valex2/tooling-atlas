*Measure · Langley, Virginia · 1930s*
**Eastman Jacobs, Kenneth Ward, and Robert Pinkerton** · a wind tunnel squeezed to 20 atmospheres

**Front.** A wing's lift depends on its size and its speed together, so a small model in an ordinary wind tunnel does not behave like a real wing, and its numbers lie. In 1933 at [Langley, Virginia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_Density_Tunnel), three NACA engineers, [Eastman Jacobs, Kenneth Ward, and Robert Pinkerton](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/19930091108), beat that with a blunt trick: they sealed a wind tunnel and [pumped the air inside to twenty times normal pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_Density_Tunnel). Packed that tight, the air pushed on a small model just as thin open air pushes on a full-size wing, so the little model finally told the truth. They ran a whole family of wing shapes through it and wrote down what each one did. The new thing was not any one wing. It was [a free book, Report 460](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/19930091108), that listed [78 wing shapes](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/19930091108) and exactly how each one flew.

**Back.** Now an American planemaker did not have to guess at a wing, or build one and crash it to find out. Open the NACA book, pick a shape, and you knew its lift and drag before you cut any metal. The numbered [NACA wings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NACA_airfoil) flew on the DC-3, the P-51 Mustang, and thousands of other aircraft, and the same four- and five-digit shapes are still in use today. For decades the power to know a wing before building it was American, and free for the taking.
