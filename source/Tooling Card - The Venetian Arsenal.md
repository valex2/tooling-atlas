*Manufacture · Venice, Italy · 1400s*
**Republic of Venice** · galley outfitting on a moving line

**Front.** One day in 1436, a Spanish traveler named [Pero Tafur](https://depts.washington.edu/silkroad/texts/tafur.html) stopped in Venice on his way to the Holy Land, and his hosts walked him through the [Arsenal](https://www.historynet.com/arsenal-venice-worlds-first-weapons-factory/), the walled yard where the Republic built its war fleet. Inside, a boat towed a finished galley hull down a canal lined with storehouses, and from each window workers passed down the ship's gear, rope from one window, arms from the next. Every piece had been [made ahead of time to a known pattern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venetian_Arsenal) and stored ready at the windows, so the ship came to the parts instead of the parts coming to the ship. Tafur watched [ten galleys](https://warhistory.org/@msw/article/venice-a-call-to-arms) come out fully armed between the hours of three and nine.

**Back.** The Republic kept bare hulls in sheds and finished gear in storehouses instead of a fleet rotting in the water, so it could put a navy to sea faster than its rivals could gather one. In 1570, when the Ottomans attacked Cyprus, the Arsenal armed [about 100 galleys inside two months](https://www.historynet.com/arsenal-venice-worlds-first-weapons-factory/), and [over half the fleet](https://www.worldhistory.org/article/2776/arsenale/) that fought at Lepanto the next year came out of its sheds. The line died with the galley: when sea war moved to tall sailing ships with heavy guns, the Arsenal kept building galleys, and Venice's lead at sea wore away. When [Ford started a moving assembly line](https://www.history.com/this-day-in-history/october-7/moving-assembly-line-at-ford) at Highland Park in 1913, his engineers took the idea from Chicago packing houses; no thread ran back to Venice.
