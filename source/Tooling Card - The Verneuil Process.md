*Manufacture · Paris, France · 1900s*
**Auguste Verneuil** · flame-fusion crystal growth

**Front.** In [1902](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Verneuil-process), a chemist at the natural history museum in Paris named [Auguste Verneuil](https://www.chemistryworld.com/opinion/verneuils-rubies/3010286.article) showed the world a furnace he had spent years building. Jewelers were passing off cloudy "reconstructed" rubies of murky origin, and Verneuil wanted to grow a true one to order. His machine sifted fine white alumina powder down through a flame [hotter than 2,000 °C](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Verneuil_method), so the powder melted into tiny drops that fell, one after another, onto a slowly lowered rod below. Layer by layer a single red boule grew on the rod, the first time anyone could grow a single crystal to order.

**Back.** Within five years his Paris workshop was turning out about [a thousand kilograms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Verneuil_method) of ruby and sapphire a year, and by 1912 more than three times that. The hard little crystals became the [jewel bearings](https://www.rockngem.com/rock-science-flame-fusion-rubies/) inside watches and precision instruments, millions of tiny pierced discs that let a pivot spin for decades without wearing down. Then in 1960 a rod of flame-grown ruby sat at the heart of [the first working laser](https://americanhistory.si.edu/collections/object/nmah_711120), which Theodore Maiman flashed on in a California lab, half a century after Verneuil first grew one in Paris.
