*Measure · Ornans, France · 1630s*
**Pierre Vernier** · sliding scale that reads finer

**Front.** In [1631](https://www.lindahall.org/about/news/scientist-of-the-day/pierre-vernier/), a self-taught instrument maker in Franche-Comté named [Pierre Vernier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_Vernier) was trying to read an angle off a quadrant to better than its smallest painted mark. The finest scale he could rule still left him guessing at the gap between two lines. So he laid a short second scale beside the main one: [ten little marks spread across the space of nine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vernier_scale), free to slide. When he moved it, only one pair of marks ever lined up cleanly, and which pair it was told him the missing fraction, down to a [tenth of a division](https://www.lindahall.org/about/news/scientist-of-the-day/pierre-vernier/).

**Back.** [Pedro Nunes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonius_(device)) had reached for the same fraction a century earlier with a nest of scales that was murder to read, and Vernier's slide was so easy that instrument makers dropped the older trick and copied his. The little scale spread onto [sextants and theodolites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vernier_scale), then onto the sliding caliper, where a matched version now reads a hundredth of a millimeter. His name only got attached to it in the 1760s, long after the workshops of Europe had made it the ordinary way to read any graduated instrument.
