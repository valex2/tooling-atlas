*Manufacture · Bedford, USA · 1970s*
**GCA's engineers** · print one field, step, repeat

**Front.** Through the 1970s a chip was patterned by shining light through a mask laid over the whole wafer at once. As the patterns shrank, that stopped working: you could not hold an entire wafer in sharp focus to the fineness the smallest features needed. In [1978 a Massachusetts company, GCA, shipped a machine called the DSW](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stepper), for Direct Step on the Wafer, that did it differently. It projected the pattern onto just one small patch of the wafer, in tight focus through a [precision Zeiss lens](https://lithoguru.com/scientist/litho_history/milestones_tools.pdf), then moved the wafer over a step and printed the next patch, and the next, tiling the whole wafer one sharp field at a time. Trading speed for precision, it could keep shrinking the pattern after the whole-wafer method had run out of focus.

**Back.** Whoever makes the best stepper sets how small a chip can go, so the machine became the most fought-over tool in the industry, and the fight moved across the world. In [1978 American suppliers made about 70% of the world's lithography machines](https://www.asianometry.com/p/how-japan-won-the-lithography-industry); GCA led. Then Japan's [Nikon and Canon shipped steppers from 1984](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stepper) with better precision and reliability and [held 70% of the market by 1989](https://www.asianometry.com/p/how-japan-won-the-lithography-industry), and GCA collapsed. Then a small Dutch firm spun out of Philips, [ASML, founded in 1984](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ASML_Holding), climbed slowly, bought its way into the lead, and ended up the only company that can make the most advanced machine of all, EUV. The stepper was invented in America, led from Japan, and now sits in one firm in the Netherlands.
