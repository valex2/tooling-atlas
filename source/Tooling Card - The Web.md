*Make · Geneva, Switzerland · 1989-1993*
**Tim Berners-Lee** · HTTP, HTML, and the URL

**Front.** In March 1989 Tim Berners-Lee was a software contractor at [CERN](https://home.cern/science/computing/birth-web/short-history-web), the physics lab outside Geneva, where thousands of scientists kept their papers and data on machines that could not easily share them. He wrote a [proposal](https://www.w3.org/History/1989/proposal.html) for a way to tie those documents together, and his boss scrawled "vague, but exciting" on the cover. By the end of 1990 he had built the three pieces that made it real: a rule for one machine to ask another for a page ([HTTP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Wide_Web)), a plain way to write a page so it carried clickable links to other pages (HTML), and an address scheme so every page had one name (the URL). What was new was the link itself: a word on one page could point straight to any other page on any computer on the internet, and a click would fetch it.

**Back.** He wrote the first browser and the first server too, and put up the [first website](https://www.history.com/articles/the-worlds-first-web-site) at CERN in December 1990. The real turn came on [30 April 1993](https://home.cern/news/news/computing/30-years-free-and-open-web), when CERN put the code in the public domain and gave up all rights to it, free for anyone to copy and change. That choice is why the Web won over walled rivals: by the mid-1990s browsers and servers spread across the whole internet, and the link became the way the world reads and writes.

*Curator's note:*
(1) Placement: Make cell, Goal Both, Convergence mechanism, Direct confidence. A built system of protocols, not a discovery.
(2) Analysis: This sits one layer above [[Tooling Card - TCP-IP]] in The Network thread. TCP/IP moved bytes between any two machines; the Web added a document layer on top so humans, not just programs, could navigate that reach. The free 1993 release is the load-bearing fact: the convergence here is partly legal, not just technical. The contrast to draw in the essay is with proprietary online services of the era that charged for access and kept users inside a single company; the royalty-free grant is why the open Web outran them.
(3) Nuance: The "1989" Year is the proposal date; the working system dates to late 1990 and the public-domain release to April 1993, so the card spans 1989-1993. Berners-Lee did not invent hypertext (Ted Nelson coined the term in the 1960s, and Vannevar Bush's 1945 "memex" essay is often cited as an ancestor); his contribution was wiring hypertext onto the internet with a simple, open address scheme. The 1993 release was a public-domain grant; CERN moved to an open-source style license in 1994, a distinction often blurred in popular accounts.
