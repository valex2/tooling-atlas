*Make · Geneva, Switzerland · 1989-1993*
**Tim Berners-Lee** · HTTP, HTML, and the URL

**Front.** In March 1989 Tim Berners-Lee was a software contractor at [CERN](https://home.cern/science/computing/birth-web/short-history-web), the physics lab outside Geneva, where thousands of scientists kept their papers and data on machines that could not easily share them. He wrote a [proposal](https://www.w3.org/History/1989/proposal.html) for a way to tie those documents together, and his boss scrawled "vague, but exciting" on the cover. By the end of 1990 he had built the three pieces that made it real: a rule for one machine to ask another for a page ([HTTP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Wide_Web)), a plain way to write a page so it carried clickable links to other pages (HTML), and an address scheme so every page had one name (the URL). The link was the heart of it: a word on one page could point straight to any other page on any computer on the internet, and a click would fetch it.

**Back.** He wrote the first browser and the first server too, and put up the [first website](https://www.history.com/articles/the-worlds-first-web-site) at CERN in December 1990. The real turn came on [30 April 1993](https://home.cern/news/news/computing/30-years-free-and-open-web), when CERN put the code in the public domain and gave up all rights to it, with no royalty owed to copy or change it. That choice is why the Web won over walled rivals like AOL and CompuServe, which charged for access and kept users inside one company: by the mid-1990s browsers and servers had spread across the whole internet, and the proprietary services were gone within a decade.
