*Measure · London, England · 1950s*
**Rosalind Franklin** · tilting X-ray camera that kept the fibre damp

**Front.** In 1951 [Rosalind Franklin](https://profiles.nlm.nih.gov/spotlight/kr/feature/dna) came to King's College in London to point X-rays at DNA. You cannot see a molecule. It is far too small for any lens. The trick is old: shine X-rays at a thing and read its shape from the way the rays scatter and land. But DNA was a fussy subject. The strand changed shape as the air around it dried or dampened, so old pictures came out as a blur of two shapes mixed. Her fix was the [camera](https://kingscollections.org/exhibitions/archives/dna/early-work/equipment) itself. With her student [Raymond Gosling](https://embryo.asu.edu/pages/photograph-51-rosalind-franklin-1952) she built a tilting micro-camera fed by a [fine, sharp X-ray beam](https://kingscollections.org/exhibitions/archives/dna/early-work/equipment), and she sealed the fibre in a small chamber where she could set the damp by hand. She bubbled hydrogen through [salt water](https://embryo.asu.edu/pages/photograph-51-rosalind-franklin-1952) and let that wet air wash over the fibre. Above about [75% damp](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK6545/) the strand stretched long and thin, one clean shape and not two. The hydrogen also kept the surrounding air from fogging the picture. She mounted a fibre [as thin as a hair](https://www.rosalindfranklin.edu/rf100/discovery.html), held it wet, and let the X-rays pour through for [62 hours](https://embryo.asu.edu/pages/photograph-51-rosalind-franklin-1952). The new thing was control: a camera that held a molecule at one exact damp long enough to print a sharp pattern of spots.

**Back.** The picture from the damp form, the 51st they took, showed a clean **X** of spots. That cross is the mark a spiral leaves, and the spacing of the spots gave hard numbers: the strand turned once every [34 ångström](https://explorebiology.org/collections/cell-biology/the-twisting-path-to-the-dna-double-helix) and stacked a rung every [3.4 ångström](https://explorebiology.org/collections/cell-biology/the-twisting-path-to-the-dna-double-helix), about ten rungs to a turn. [James Watson and Francis Crick](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photo_51) used those numbers to build their [1953 model](https://www.nature.com/articles/171737a0) of two strands twisted together. The wet-fibre method opened the way to reading any orderly molecule's shape from a fibre, the read step that the rest of the loop builds on. The credit did not follow the work: Franklin [died in 1958](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photo_51), and the [1962 Nobel Prize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photo_51) went to Watson, Crick, and Wilkins.

*Curator's note:* **Placement.** Measure cell, London, 1951 to 1952. Goal 1: the math of a spiral was already known, but only a built thing, a camera that pinned a molecule at one exact damp, could make the real shape print sharp enough to measure. Mechanism: Convergence, the X-ray-scattering craft from crystallography meeting a physical chemist's control of humidity, arriving at the one molecule that mattered most. Confidence: Direct on the camera, the humidity trick, the 62-hour exposure, the B-form X, the 34 and 3.4 ångström numbers, and the 1953 model built from them, all multiply sourced. **Analysis.** Reframed off the *picture* and onto the *camera*: the deck rule is tool-forward, and the durable instrument here is the wet-fibre X-ray setup, not the one famous plate it produced. This is the bottom of the [[Thread - Reading & Writing DNA]] loop, the slow physical read of a molecule's shape. It pairs against [[Tooling Card - AlphaFold]]: this card is the slow physical way to see a shape (one fibre, held damp, 62 hours of X-rays, one plate), AlphaFold the fast computed way (a guess on a screen, no fibre at all). That same mirror, a physical stand-in versus pure computation, is why it also opens [[Thread - Predict Before Building]]: X-ray camera to AI guess is the same move as wind tunnel to simulation. Hero card, one named scientist refining one instrument at one bench. **Nuance.** Exposure time is cited two ways: King's College London and the Embryo Project say 62 hours; Wikipedia and others say about 100 hours. The face uses the 62-hour figure. The sharp picture is of the "B" (wet, high-humidity) form; Franklin and Gosling named the dry and wet forms "A" and "B" themselves. Gosling physically took and developed the plate while working under Franklin, and the tilting camera was machined by Len Pitches in the King's workshops, so "Franklin's camera" is shorthand for her design, her humidity method, and her control of the technique. The credit story is honestly messy: Watson saw the image without Franklin's knowledge, and the model also rested on her own measurements and on Crick's theory of how a helix should scatter, so the picture was decisive but not the only input. The Nobel does not make posthumous awards, the stated reason Franklin was left out, though she was under-credited well before her death.
