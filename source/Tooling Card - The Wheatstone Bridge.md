*Measure · London, UK · 1840s*
**Charles Wheatstone (Samuel Hunter Christie)** · four resistances balanced to zero

**Front.** In 1843, [Charles Wheatstone](https://ethw.org/Charles_Wheatstone), a music-shop owner's son turned London professor, so shy of crowds that Michael Faraday often read his papers aloud for him, was trying to pin down exactly how hard different wires push back against a current. Every meter of the day was suspect: its reading drifted as the battery faded and bent with the meter's own faults. So he wired four resistances into a diamond, set a battery across one pair of opposite corners and a current needle across the other, and slid a known resistance up and down until the needle stood dead on [zero](https://nationalmaglab.org/magnet-academy/history-of-electricity-magnetism/museum/wheatstone-bridge-1843/); at that balance the unknown resistance follows from the known ones by plain ratio, and the battery and the needle drop out of the answer. The circuit itself came from a [ten-year-old paper](https://www.jstor.org/stable/107990) by Samuel Hunter Christie, and Wheatstone [credited him](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rstl.1843.0014) before the Royal Society; the world called it Wheatstone's bridge anyway.

**Back.** Telegraph engineers picked the bridge up first: the British Post Office built it into a [plug box](https://collection.sciencemuseumgroup.org.uk/objects/co33905/post-office-box-wheatstone-bridge-for-line-and-oth-wheatstone) its linemen carried, and balancing it against a dead line told them [how many miles out](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Post_office_box_(electricity)) the break lay. The null idea kept compounding: when [Simmons and Ruge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strain_gauge) glued fine wire to metal in 1938 to sense strain, a bridge read the tiny resistance change, and one still sits inside every load cell that weighs a truck. Christie, who had drawn the diamond in [1833](https://www.encyclopedia.com/science/dictionaries-thesauruses-pictures-and-press-releases/christie-samuel-hunter), got his name back only in the history books; German and French translations of Wheatstone's lecture dropped the credit within a year, and the bridge has been Wheatstone's ever since.
