*Measure · Manchester, UK · 1840s*
**Joseph Whitworth** · one national rule for threads

**Front.** In 1841, a Manchester toolmaker named [Joseph Whitworth](https://www.gracesguide.co.uk/Joseph_Whitworth) kept running into the same trouble: every engine shop in England cut screw threads its own way, so a bolt from one works would not fit a nut from the next, and a broken machine waited while a fitter cut a matching screw by hand. Whitworth [collected screw bolts from the principal workshops across England](https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Miscellaneous_Papers_on_Mechanical_Subjects/A_Paper_on_an_Uniform_System_of_Screw_Threads), measured the thread on each, and found the angles fell around a mean of 55 degrees. He drew up one table (sides at exactly [55 degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Standard_Whitworth), rounded tops and roots, a fixed count of threads to the inch for each size of bolt) and read it as a short paper at the Institution of Civil Engineers. He built no new machine; the table was the tool, the first national rule for a machine part, and any shop that cut to it made bolts that fit every other shop's work.

**Back.** British railways cut to the table first, and [seventeen years on](https://www.gracesguide.co.uk/Joseph_Whitworth) Whitworth could report his thread in general use across the country: a bolt had become a stock part you ordered by size, not a one-off filed to fit. Then in [1864](https://www.asme.org/about-asme/engineering-history/landmarks/234-the-united-states-standard-screw-threads) a Philadelphia machine-builder, William Sellers, redrew the thread at 60 degrees with flat tops, easier for a plain shop to cut with a plain tool, and American railroads and government shops standardized on his angle instead. Britain held its 55 degrees through two world wars of Allied machines that could not swap bolts, then [gave it up in 1948](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unified_Thread_Standard) for the American 60; the old angle survives chiefly in [pipe threads](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Standard_Pipe), still cut at Whitworth's 55 degrees today.
