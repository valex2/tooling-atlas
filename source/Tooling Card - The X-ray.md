*Measure · Würzburg, Germany · 1890s*
**Wilhelm Röntgen** · rays that print bone through skin

**Front.** On the evening of [8 November 1895](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/physics/1901/rontgen/biographical/), Wilhelm Röntgen was alone in his lab at the University of Würzburg, running current through a glass tube he had wrapped in black cardboard. He wanted to seal the tube's own light in, so he could study the faint glow it made. Across the room a small screen painted with [barium platinocyanide](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Wilhelm-Rontgen) lit up anyway, [almost two metres away](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Wilhelm-Rontgen), with the tube fully covered. Something invisible was passing through the cardboard, and when he held things in its path he found it passed through paper, wood, and flesh but stopped at bone and metal, so the screen could now show the shape of the dense thing inside.

**Back.** On [22 December 1895](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:First_medical_X-ray_by_Wilhelm_R%C3%B6ntgen_of_his_wife_Anna_Bertha_Ludwig's_hand_-_18951222.jpg) he sat his wife's hand on a photographic plate and made a picture of her finger bones with the wedding ring floating around one of them. His paper came out [days later, on 28 December](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray), and within months hospitals across Europe and America were photographing broken bones and lodged bullets. The tool won him the [first Nobel Prize in Physics in 1901](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/physics/1901/rontgen/biographical/), and the same rays were soon turned on crystals and, much later, sliced into the layered scans of a whole body.
