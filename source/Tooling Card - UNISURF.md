*Model · Boulogne-Billancourt, France · 1960s*
**Pierre Bézier** · car surfaces from pulled points

**Front.** In 1968, [Pierre Bézier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_B%C3%A9zier), an engineer thirty-five years into a Renault career spent building the machines that cut car parts, watched every new car begin as a [full-size clay sculpture](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/rspa/article-abstract/321/1545/207/13264/Example-of-an-existing-system-in-the-motor), copied by hand onto drawings and master models that each drifted a little from the last. He wrote a program called [UNISURF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UNISURF) in which a draftsman laid down a cage of a few straight lines and the computer bent a smooth curve to follow it; pull one corner of the cage and the whole curve moved with it. The same few points then steered a numerically controlled mill that carved the master model. He had [printed the mathematics in 1966](https://www.farinhansford.com/gerald/papers/history.pdf), but the system was the thing: one short list of numbers now carried a car's shape from first sketch to steel die.

**Back.** Renault had UNISURF in [full use by 1975](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UNISURF), and by 1999 about [1,500 Renault people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UNISURF) were designing cars on it. The plane-maker Dassault [built its own surface program on Bézier's curves](https://www.farinhansford.com/gerald/papers/history.pdf), and that program grew into [CATIA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CATIA), the tool most of the world's aircraft are now drawn in, so a lead born in a car body shop compounded into aerospace. Across Paris at Citroën, [Paul de Casteljau](https://www.farinhansford.com/gerald/papers/history.pdf) had worked out the same curves nine years earlier, but the company kept his papers secret; the curves on every design screen today carry the name of the man whose employer let him build.
