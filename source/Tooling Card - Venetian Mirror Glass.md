*Make · Murano, Venice, Italy · 1600s*
**Murano mirror-makers and Jean-Baptiste Colbert** · cast flat glass backed with tin-mercury

**Front.** From 1291 Venice penned all its glass furnaces on one small island, [Murano](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venetian_glass), so the fires stayed off the wooden city and the craft stayed in one place it could watch. There the masters held two secrets the rest of Europe wanted: clear [cristallo](https://crystalglass.ca/the-hidden-story-of-cristallo-glass/) glass, cooked nearly colorless, and the flat mirror. To make the mirror they laid a sheet of [tin foil on a flat stone, flooded it with liquid mercury, then slid a glass plate over the top](https://cameo.mfa.org/wiki/Tin-mercury_mirror); the metals knit into a bright crystalline skin, about three parts tin to one part mercury, that gripped the glass and threw back a clean image. The new thing was a mirror that was truly flat and truly clear, not a dim spotted disk, and for a century only Murano hands knew how to cast it. A master who carried the trade abroad was branded a traitor; [leaving could be punished by death](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venetian_glass), and his family could be jailed to call him home.

**Back.** Venetian mirrors were the luxury of Europe, and France's minister [Jean-Baptiste Colbert](http://atynerantiques.blogspot.com/2016/12/king-louis-xiv-breaks-murano-monoploy.html) wanted them made at home, so in 1665 he sent agents to Murano and paid the men in gold to come to Paris. The first to arrive was [a master and his two apprentices, then four more](https://barbaraathanassiadis.com/history/venetian-glassmakers-in-paris). Venice answered the way the law promised: it leaned on their families and, [in 1667, two of the key craftsmen died in agony](https://barbaraathanassiadis.com/history/venetian-glassmakers-in-paris), widely read as poison, and Colbert let the frightened survivors go home. It did not matter, because France had already learned the craft by hand; the workshop Colbert founded became [Saint-Gobain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_manufactories_in_France), which cast the [357 mirror panels of the Hall of Mirrors](https://www.parisdigest.com/versailles/hall-of-mirrors.htm) at Versailles and is still making glass today. The secret was stolen, and a death-penalty ban could not pull it back, because the craft lived in men who could simply walk to Paris.

*Curator's note:* **Placement.** Make cell, Murano, 1665, on [[Thread - Precision Optics]]. Goal 2 (commercial luxury and strategic prestige); Mechanism Fragility. Confidence Direct. **Analysis.** The deck's clean case that a legislated secret is a fragile one. The tacit-craft moats elsewhere on the optics thread never needed a law, because the knowledge could not be written down and walked off; Venice wrote the harshest law there is and still lost the trade the moment a rival bought the carriers. It pairs as the negative of the durable-moat cards: when the secret is in a handful of nameable people, money beats the death penalty. **Nuance.** Tin-mercury silvering was developed on Murano in the 16th century (the Del Gallo brothers are usually named); 1665 marks the theft and the founding of the royal works, not the tool's invention. The 1667 poisonings are reported by the French side and widely repeated but rest on period diplomatic letters rather than hard proof, so the cause of death is plausible, not certain; the recruitment and the loss of monopoly are firm. **Secrecy:** stolen — Venice guarded clear glass and the tin-mercury mirror with an island prison and a death-penalty ban, and France broke it by luring the artisans themselves, since the craft was carried in hands, not on a page.
