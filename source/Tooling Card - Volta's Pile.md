*Make · Pavia, Italy · 1800s*
**Alessandro Volta** · a stack of discs that pours current

**Front.** Around 1800, [Alessandro Volta](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Alessandro-Volta), a professor in Italy, was in a long argument with Luigi Galvani, who thought the twitch in a dead frog's leg was a special "animal electricity" made by the flesh itself. Volta suspected the real cause was the two different metals touching the leg, not the frog. To prove it, he needed electricity from metals alone, and he needed it to last, because all anyone could make before was a single brief spark. On his own bench he stacked [discs in a tall pile: zinc, then copper or silver, then a layer of cloth soaked in brine, over and over](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voltaic_pile). The pile gave a [steady, unbroken current, the first in history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voltaic_pile). This was the whole of electrical science in one man's hands: a column of cheap metal discs on a single bench.

**Back.** Within months others were running Volta's current through water to [split it into hydrogen and oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voltaic_pile), and Humphry Davy built a giant pile to [pull apart sodium, potassium, and calcium for the first time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voltaic_pile); Volta showed the device to [Napoleon in 1801](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Alessandro-Volta). Every electrical thing that followed needed a source of current to begin with, the telegraph, the electroplating bath, and the experiments that would soon reveal electromagnetism. That tower of discs on one quiet bench is the bottom of the whole electrical age: everything later in this deck that switches, computes, or talks runs first on having a current to run on.

It pairs forward with [[Tooling Card - Faraday's Induction Ring]]: Volta made a current you could store and tap, Faraday made one you could generate from motion, and together they are the source and the dynamo under everything electric. The card carries the Little-to-Big argument plainly, the lone bench beneath the grid. **Nuance.** Volta described the pile in a two-part 1799 letter to the Royal Society, published in 1800, which is the date used here. The exact disc metals vary by account (zinc with copper, or zinc with silver); both are correct for different versions. The Galvani dispute is the real origin: the pile was built to win an argument about frogs.
