*Make · Pavia, Italy · 1800s*
**Alessandro Volta** · a stack of discs that pours current

**Front.** A twitching frog's leg started the fight. Luigi Galvani thought the twitch came from a special "animal electricity" made by the flesh itself; [Alessandro Volta](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Alessandro-Volta), a professor in Italy, suspected the real cause was the two different metals touching the leg, not the frog, and around 1800 he set out to prove it. To prove it, he needed electricity from metals alone, and he needed it to last, because all anyone could make before was a single brief spark. On his own bench he stacked [discs in a tall pile: zinc, then copper or silver, then a layer of cloth soaked in brine, over and over](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voltaic_pile). The pile gave a [steady, unbroken current, the first in history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voltaic_pile). This was the whole of electrical science in one man's hands: a column of cheap metal discs on a single bench.

**Back.** Within months others were running Volta's current through water to [split it into hydrogen and oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voltaic_pile), and Humphry Davy built a giant pile to [pull apart sodium, potassium, and calcium for the first time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voltaic_pile); Volta showed the device to [Napoleon in 1801](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Alessandro-Volta). The telegraph, the electroplating bath, and Faraday's experiments on electromagnetism all needed a steady current before they could begin, and for the first half-century there was only one way to make one: a column of zinc and copper discs, copied off Volta's Pavia bench.
