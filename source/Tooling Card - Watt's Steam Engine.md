*Make · Glasgow, Scotland · 1760s-1770s*
**James Watt** · steam cooled in a separate vessel

**Front.** In 1765 James Watt, a Scottish instrument maker in Glasgow, was repairing a model of the Newcomen engine, the pump that drained Britain's mines. He saw why it ate so much coal: each stroke sprayed cold water straight into the cylinder to pull the piston down, so the whole cylinder had to be heated back up the next stroke. Watt's fix, [patented in 1769](https://blog.sciencemuseum.org.uk/james-watt-and-the-separate-condenser/), was to run the steam off into a [separate vessel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Watt_steam_engine) and condense it there with a water jet, leaving the cylinder hot the whole time. What was new was the split: condense in one place, keep the working cylinder hot in another, so no heat was wasted reheating iron every stroke.

**Back.** Built into working engines with Matthew Boulton at the [Soho works in Birmingham from the mid-1770s](https://www.worldhistory.org/Watt_Steam_Engine/), the engine [burned about a third of the coal](https://www.britannica.com/technology/Watt-steam-engine) a Newcomen engine did for the same work. That meant a mill or factory could run on steam far from any pit, not just at the coal face. Boulton and Watt sold the engines across Britain, and the design opened the way to steam driving every kind of machine, the prime mover of the Industrial Revolution.
