*Make · Glasgow, Scotland · 1760s-1770s*
**James Watt** · steam cooled in a separate vessel

**Front.** In 1765 James Watt, a Scottish instrument maker in Glasgow, was repairing a model of the Newcomen engine, the pump that drained Britain's mines. He saw why it ate so much coal: each stroke sprayed cold water straight into the cylinder to pull the piston down, so the whole cylinder had to be heated back up the next stroke. Watt's fix, [patented in 1769](https://blog.sciencemuseum.org.uk/james-watt-and-the-separate-condenser/), split the job in two: run the steam off into a [separate vessel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Watt_steam_engine) and condense it there with a water jet, while the working cylinder stayed hot the whole time. No heat was wasted reheating iron every stroke.

**Back.** Built into working engines with Matthew Boulton at the [Soho works in Birmingham from the mid-1770s](https://www.worldhistory.org/Watt_Steam_Engine/), the engine [burned about a third of the coal](https://www.britannica.com/technology/Watt-steam-engine) a Newcomen engine did for the same work. That meant a mill or factory could run on steam far from any pit, not just at the coal face. Boulton and Watt sold the engines across Britain, and within a lifetime that same separate-condenser engine was driving looms, mills, and the first locomotives, the prime mover of the Industrial Revolution.
