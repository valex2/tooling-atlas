*Make · Bersham, Wales · 1770s*
**John Wilkinson** · a bar braced at both ends bores a true hole

**Front.** In 1774, [John Wilkinson](https://www.britannica.com/biography/John-Wilkinson), an ironmaster at Bersham in Wales who cast cannon for a living, [took out a patent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Wilkinson_(industrialist)) on a new way to bore a gun. The old way hung the cutter on a bar gripped at one end only, so the bar bent under the load and the hole wandered off true. Wilkinson [cast the barrel solid](https://www.gracesguide.co.uk/Early_Boring_Mills) and slid it onto a heavy bar held fast at both ends, then turned the iron against a fixed cutter so the hole came out straight and round the whole way down. No machine before it had held its cutter that stiff, and none could bore so wide a hole so nearly true.

**Back.** Within a year the same rigid bar was set to work on steam-engine cylinders. [James Watt's engine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Wilkinson_(industrialist)) had leaked for years because its cylinders came out of round and steam slipped past the piston; in 1776 Wilkinson bored one 50 inches across that did not err [the thickness of an old shilling](https://www.gracesguide.co.uk/Early_Boring_Mills) anywhere in the bore. Boulton and Watt handed him an exclusive contract, and for years every Watt engine ran on a cylinder bored at Bersham. The machine has since been called [the first machine tool](https://www.historyofinformation.com/detail.php?id=4755), and for a century after, a British steam cylinder began as a hole cut by a bar propped at both ends.
