*Measure · Leeds, Britain · 1910s*
**William Henry and William Lawrence Bragg** · atoms read from scattered X-rays

**Front.** In 1912 [Max von Laue had shown that X-rays passing through a crystal come out in a pattern of spots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bragg's_law), proof that the atoms inside sat in an orderly grid, but no one could read the pattern back into a structure. A father and son took it up: [William Henry Bragg](https://www.ebsco.com/research-starters/history/x-ray-crystallography-developed-braggs), a professor at Leeds, and his son [William Lawrence Bragg](https://blog.oup.com/2013/08/100th-anniversary-first-crystal-structure-determinations-bragg/), still a student at Cambridge. The son found the simple rule, [nλ = 2d sinθ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bragg's_law), that ties the angle of each spot to the spacing of the atomic layers, so you could run the pattern backwards and work out exactly where the atoms sit; the father built a precise [X-ray spectrometer](https://blog.oup.com/2013/08/100th-anniversary-first-crystal-structure-determinations-bragg/) to measure the angles. In [1913 they solved their first structures, common salt and diamond](https://blog.oup.com/2013/08/100th-anniversary-first-crystal-structure-determinations-bragg/), showing just how the atoms are stacked. The new thing was a way to see the arrangement of atoms inside a solid, which no microscope could reach.

**Back.** It became the master tool for the structure of matter, and won the two a [Nobel Prize in 1915, the son at 25 still the youngest physics laureate](https://www.ebsco.com/research-starters/history/x-ray-crystallography-developed-braggs). Metallurgists, chemists, and drug-makers used it to read how atoms pack; it gave [Franklin the camera that caught DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bragg's_law), and it solved the shapes of penicillin, vitamins, and thousands of proteins. A century later, the heap of protein structures it had painstakingly measured became the training data a machine learned to predict. The tool that maps where atoms sit is the quiet floor under all of materials science.
