*Measure · Uppsala, Sweden · 1950s*
**Kai Siegbahn** · reads electrons knocked off a surface

**Front.** In 1957, [Kai Siegbahn](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/physics/1981/siegbahn/facts/) ran a physics lab in [Uppsala](https://www.uu.se/en/about-uu/history/nobel-prizes/kai-siegbahn), where his father had measured X-rays before him. He wanted to read the exact energy of a single electron more sharply than anyone yet had. So he shone X-rays at a thin sheet of [copper](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/analytical-science/articles/10.3389/frans.2024.1509438/full), which knocked electrons loose, and bent their flight through a large iron-free magnet ring so the fast ones and the slow ones landed in different spots, letting him read each electron's energy off where it fell. The electrons that got out came only from the [top few atoms](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/analytical-science/articles/10.3389/frans.2024.1509438/full) of the sheet, and each kind of atom left its own sharp line, so the reading named which atoms sat on the surface.

**Back.** A year later the lines moved a hair when the copper had [rusted](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/analytical-science/articles/10.3389/frans.2024.1509438/full), so the same reading told not just which atom was there but how it was bonded. The machine was slow to spread, but by the 1970s firms were building it to order, and it became the standard way to check the thin skin of computer chips and catalysts; Siegbahn named it ESCA. In [1981](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/physics/1981/summary/) the physics prize went to him for the work, the same prize his father [Manne Siegbahn](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/physics/1924/summary/) had taken in 1924.
