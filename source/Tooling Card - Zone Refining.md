*Manufacture · Murray Hill, New Jersey, USA · 1950s*
**William Pfann** · a molten band swept along a solid bar

**Front.** [Bill Pfann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Gardner_Pfann) walked into Bell Labs in 1935 as an eighteen-year-old messenger boy and took his chemical engineering degree at night. By 1951 he was a metallurgist there, and [Bill Shockley](https://www.computerhistory.org/siliconengine/development-of-zone-refining/) was pressing the materials men for germanium cleaner than anyone had ever made. A colleague described the usual cure to him: freeze the ingot slowly from one end, pour off the last tenth where the dirt has collected, drag the crucible out of the furnace, and do the whole thing four or five times. ["I told him it sounded like a lot of work," Pfann wrote](https://www.cambridge.org/core/services/aop-cambridge-core/content/view/E8464711C34D8CE1DFA4797B4BB54B15/S0883769400068688a.pdf/zone_refiningwilliam_g_pfann.pdf). He was tipped back in his chair with his head against the office window sill, taking his usual lunch-hour nap, when the answer came: run a long germanium bar through a whole row of heating coils instead of one. Each coil melts a narrow band of the bar. Dirt dissolves more readily in the liquid than in the solid, so every band that creeps along carries its load of dirt with it and leaves clean metal freezing behind, and the filthy end gets sawn off at the finish. "I remember the feet of the chair hitting the floor with a loud clack as I jumped up," he said. The crucible never had to leave the furnace.

**Back.** Bell Labs ran the process commercially [in 1953](https://www.ebsco.com/research-starters/engineering/zone-refining) and brought germanium below [one stray atom in ten billion](https://www.cambridge.org/core/services/aop-cambridge-core/content/view/E8464711C34D8CE1DFA4797B4BB54B15/S0883769400068688a.pdf/zone_refiningwilliam_g_pfann.pdf), where a few parts per million had been considered excellent. Silicon would not take the treatment, since molten silicon eats whatever holds it, so [Henry Theuerer](https://www.computerhistory.org/siliconengine/development-of-zone-refining/) clamped a silicon rod at both ends, stood it upright, and let the molten band hang there on its own surface tension with no container at all. [Keck and Golay](https://journals.aps.org/pr/abstract/10.1103/PhysRev.89.1297) at the Army Signal Corps and R. Emeis at Siemens reached the same container-free trick within a year, and clean silicon, which keeps working at [150 C where germanium leaks badly above 70](https://www.electricalvolt.com/2022/04/why-silicon-is-preferred-over-germanium/), pulled the industry off germanium and kept it off. The band is still swept today, through the silicon in power devices and radiation detectors and through the [gallium, indium and tellurium](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8072940/) that compound semiconductors are made from. Pfann learned only after his [1952 paper](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/BF03398137) that Pyotr Kapitza had swept a molten zone along a rod in 1928, thought about how the crystal grew rather than where the dirt went, and left it there.
